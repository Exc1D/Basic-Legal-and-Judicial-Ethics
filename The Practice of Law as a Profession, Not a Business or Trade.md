
## TL;DR

- In Philippine law, the practice of law is a **privilege burdened with public interest and conditions**, not a property right or commercial enterprise; the governing maxim—"the law is a profession and not a business" (later "not a trade")—originates in _In re Tagorda_ (53 Phil. 37, March 23, 1929) and is reaffirmed through _Director of Religious Affairs v. Bayot_ (1944), _[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep v. The Legal Clinic]]_ (B.M. No. 553, 1993), _Khan v. Simbillo_ (A.C. No. 5299 / G.R. No. 157053, 2003), and _[[Burbe v. Atty. Magulta|Burbe v. Magulta]]_ (A.C. No. 5713, 2002), with "duty to public service, not money," as the primary consideration.
- The doctrine is now codified in the **Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (CPRA, A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC)**, launched/promulgated April 11–13, 2023, published May 14, 2023, and effective May 29, 2023, principally through Canon II (Propriety, §§17–18 on non-solicitation/advertising and §§25–27 on firm structure) and Canon III (Fidelity, §§42–43 on division/non-sharing of fees with non-lawyers), reinforced by Article XII, §14 of the 1987 Constitution and Article 1815 of the Civil Code.
- Crossing the line into commercialism—self-laudatory advertising, ambulance chasing, fee-splitting with non-lawyers, deceptive firm names, or corporate practice—is **malpractice** and a ground for suspension or disbarment under Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court (now Canon VI of the CPRA); penalties range from reprimand (_Bayot_) to one-month and one-year suspensions (_Tagorda_; _Simbillo_, _Linsangan_) to disbarment (_Edillon_).

## Key Findings

1. **Constitutional anchor.** Article VIII, §5(5) of the 1987 Constitution vests in the Supreme Court the exclusive power to regulate admission to the practice of law and the Integrated Bar; Article XII, §14 limits the practice of all professions to Filipino citizens. These provisions frame law as a state-regulated public function rather than an ordinary trade open to commerce.
    
2. **The practice of law is a privilege, not a right or business.** _In re Edillon_ (A.C. No. 1928, August 3, 1978) holds that "the practice of law is not a vested right but a privilege, a privilege clothed with public interest." This justifies compulsory IBP membership, mandatory dues, MCLE, and the full disciplinary apparatus.
    
3. **Broad but profession-bounded definition of practice.** _[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]_ (G.R. No. 100113, September 3, 1991) defines practice of law expansively—"any activity, in or out of court, which requires the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training and experience"—but ties it to acts "characteristic of the profession," reinforcing that what is regulated is professional, not commercial, activity.
    
4. **The CPRA codifies the doctrine.** Canon II (Propriety) §17 bans solicitation and self-laudatory advertising; §18 bans self-promotion; §§25–27 govern firm names and structure; Canon III §43 prohibits fee-sharing with non-lawyers; §42 regulates referral fees among lawyers. The CPRA repealed the 1988 CPR and Rule 139-B and applies to pending and future cases.
    
5. **Consistent enforcement.** From _Tagorda_ (1929) to _Linsangan v. Tolentino_ (A.C. No. 6672, 2009), the Court has disciplined lawyers who "commercialize" the profession. As of mid-2025, however, the Court has **not yet issued a CPRA-era decision squarely applying §17** to an advertising/solicitation case; the doctrine's operative jurisprudence remains the pre-CPRA line of cases, which the CPRA expressly carries forward (§17 is annotated as deriving from old Rule 2.03).
    

## Details

### 1. Constitutional and Statutory Framework

**1987 Constitution.**

- **Article VIII, §5(5)** grants the Supreme Court power to "promulgate rules concerning... the admission to the practice of law, the Integrated Bar, and legal assistance to the underprivileged." This is the constitutional source of the Court's plenary, exclusive authority over the legal profession—distinguishing it from professions regulated by the executive-branch Professional Regulation Commission.
- **Article XII, §14** ("National Economy and Patrimony"): "The practice of all professions in the Philippines shall be limited to Filipino citizens, save in cases prescribed by law." Together with the citizenship requirement in Rule 138, this confirms that the privilege to practice is tied to loyalty and accountability to the Philippine legal system, not market access. Narrow exceptions exist (e.g., pro hac vice admission; resumption of practice after reacquisition of citizenship under R.A. No. 9225).

**Rules of Court.** Rule 138 governs admission and the duties of attorneys; **Section 27, Rule 138** makes the "soliciting of cases at law for the purpose of gain, either personally or through paid agents or brokers" malpractice and a ground for disbarment or suspension. (Sections 20–37 of Rule 138 and Rule 139-B were repealed by the CPRA in 2023, with the disciplinary process now in Canon VI.)

**Civil Code.**

- **Article 1767** allows two or more persons to "form a partnership for the exercise of a profession"—the legal basis for the **general professional partnership (GPP)**, the only permissible vehicle for joint law practice.
- **Article 1815** requires that every partnership operate under a firm name and that persons who are not partners but include their names in the firm name assume the liability of a partner—central to the _In re Sycip_ ruling against retaining deceased partners' names.
- **Article 1825** imposes partner-level liability on a non-partner who allows his name in the firm name. These provisions treat the law partnership as fundamentally different from a commercial enterprise with transferable goodwill.

**Corporation Code (Revised Corporation Code, R.A. No. 11232).** A corporation cannot engage in the practice of law. Law cannot be practiced through a stock corporation; professionals may incorporate "only if provided by special law," and no such special law authorizes corporate law practice. Non-lawyers cannot hold equity, be partners, or sit on the management of a law firm. The "one-person corporation" innovation expressly excludes professional practice. This is the statutory expression of the doctrine against corporate/commercial practice of law.

**Integration statutes.** R.A. No. 6397 (effective September 17, 1971) confirmed the Supreme Court's power to integrate the Bar "to raise the standards of the legal profession, improve the administration of justice, and enable the Bar to discharge its public responsibility more effectively." Presidential Decree No. 181 constituted the IBP as a body corporate with the power to levy and collect membership dues and special assessments.

### 2. The Integrated Bar of the Philippines (IBP)

The IBP was created by Supreme Court resolution (January 9, 1973) under Rule 139-A, pursuant to R.A. No. 6397 and P.D. No. 181. In **In re: Integration of the Bar** (A.M. No. 526, January 9, 1973), the Court enumerated the purposes of integration: to assist in the administration of justice; to foster high ideals of integrity, learning, and professional competence; and to enable the Bar to discharge its public responsibility. The premise throughout is that "the legal profession is imbued with public interest," justifying state regulation.

**In re Edillon (A.C. No. 1928, August 3, 1978)** is the doctrinal cornerstone for the IBP's regulatory authority. Atty. Marcial Edillon refused to pay IBP dues, claiming compulsory membership violated his freedoms of association and against deprivation of property. The Supreme Court (Castro, C.J.) unanimously rejected the challenge and ordered him stricken from the Roll of Attorneys, holding:

- Integration does not compel association—he was already a member of the Bar upon passing the exams; integration merely organizes that existing body.
- "The practice of law is not a vested right but a privilege, a privilege clothed with public interest because a lawyer owes substantial duties not only to his client, but also to his brethren in the profession, to the courts, and to the nation."
- The State may require lawyers to share the cost of improving the profession.

Edillon was later **reinstated** (Resolution, December 19, 1980) after paying his dues and acknowledging the Court's authority. The principle that "membership in the bar is a privilege burdened with conditions" was reaffirmed in **Letter of Atty. Cecilio Y. Arevalo, Jr.** (B.M. No. 1370, May 9, 2005), which denied a dues exemption based on non-practice and overseas residence and ordered payment of P12,035.00 in assessed dues for 1977–2005.

The IBP, through its **Commission on Bar Discipline**, investigates complaints and recommends sanctions; it also runs the **National Committee on Legal Aid**, expanding free legal services to indigents—an institutional embodiment of the "public service" character of the profession. The Revised IBP By-Laws were adopted by the Court in 2023.

### 3. The Bench: Landmark Supreme Court Decisions

**In re Tagorda (53 Phil. 37, March 23, 1929)** — The foundational case. Luis Tagorda, a provincial board member, used cards and a circular letter advertising himself as a lawyer and notary public offering free consultation and land-registration services at fixed prices. He was **suspended from the practice of law for one month**, with his "youth and inexperience at the bar" treated as mitigating. The Court declared: "The law is a profession and not a business," and condemned solicitation by circulars or advertisements as unprofessional, "destructive of the honor of a great profession."

**Director of Religious Affairs v. Bayot (A.C. No. L-1117, March 20, 1944; 74 Phil. 579)** — Atty. Estanislao Bayot published a newspaper ad offering to secure marriage licenses promptly, avoid delay/publicity, and give free consultation to the poor. The Court held: "It is highly unethical for an attorney to advertise his talents or skill as a merchant advertises his wares. Law is a profession and not a trade." Because Bayot pleaded leniency, advertised only once, and gained no cases, he was merely **reprimanded** (vs. the one-month suspension in _Tagorda_, deemed more serious because solicitations there were repeated and insistent). Source of the enduring line: "The most worthy and effective advertisement possible... is the establishment of a well-merited reputation for professional capacity and fidelity to trust."

**In re Sycip (G.R. No. X92-1, July 30, 1979; 92 SCRA 1)** — Two firms (Sycip Salazar and Ozaeta Romulo) petitioned to retain the names of deceased partners (Alexander Sycip, d. May 5, 1975; Herminio Ozaeta, d. February 14, 1976). The Court **denied** the petitions, holding: (a) under Article 1815 of the Civil Code, firm names must be those of living partners who can be subject to liability; (b) there is no Philippine custom permitting retention of deceased partners' names; (c) the personal and confidential nature of the attorney-client relationship forbids any practice that "could give rise to the possibility of deception"; and (d) the public-relations/goodwill value of an old firm name creates unfair commercial advantage incompatible with the profession. _(Note: the CPRA, Canon II §26, now expressly permits continued use of a deceased, incapacitated, or retired partner's name provided the firm indicates the partner's status in all communications—superseding Sycip on this point while preserving its anti-deception rationale.)_

**In re Edillon (A.C. No. 1928, 1978)** — discussed above (privilege clothed with public interest).

**[[Cayetano v. Monsod]] (G.R. No. 100113, September 3, 1991)** — Renato Cayetano challenged Christian Monsod's qualification as COMELEC Chairman, arguing Monsod had not "practiced law for at least ten years." The Court (Paras, J.) upheld the appointment, adopting a broad, modern definition: "Practice of law means any activity, in or out of court, which requires the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training and experience... to practice law is to give notice or render any kind of service, which device or service requires the use in any degree of legal knowledge or skill." While primarily about the definition of practice (not the profession-vs-business doctrine), it is the controlling definition repeatedly invoked—and is partly codified in CPRA Canon III §1. Justice Padilla's dissent emphasized habituality, compensation, and "holding oneself out" as the markers of practice.

**[[Burbe v. Atty. Magulta|Burbe v. Magulta]] (A.C. No. 5713, June 10, 2002)** — Atty. Alberto Magulta misappropriated his client's P25,000 filing fee, falsely claimed the complaint was filed, and later argued no lawyer-client relationship existed because he had not been paid; when confronted, he offered to reimburse the client through two postdated checks (P12,000 dated June 1 and P8,000 dated June 5, 1999). The Court (Panganiban, J.) **suspended him one year** for violating Rules 16.01 and 18.03 of the CPR, and articulated the doctrine memorably: "Lawyering is not a business; it is a profession in which duty to public service, not money, is the primary consideration... Lawyering is not primarily meant to be a money-making venture, and law advocacy is not a capital that necessarily yields profits. The gaining of a livelihood is not a professional but a secondary consideration. The practice of law is a noble calling in which emolument is a byproduct."

**[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.]] (B.M. No. 553, June 17, 1993)** — Atty. Mauricio Ulep sought to enjoin The Legal Clinic, Inc. from running newspaper ads offering "secret marriage," Guam divorce, annulment, and visa services for fixed fees. The Clinic claimed it rendered only "legal support services" through paralegals. The Court **enjoined** the advertisements, holding: (a) the services constituted the **practice of law** (which only natural-person members of the Bar in good standing may render), so the corporation was engaged in unauthorized practice; (b) the ads were misleading and promoted illegal/immoral arrangements (divorce, secret/bigamous marriage); and (c) "The proscription against advertising of legal services or solicitation of legal business rests on the fundamental postulate that the practice of law is a profession." It reaffirmed that a lawyer's best advertisement is "a well-merited reputation for professional capacity and fidelity to trust." (The Court enumerated narrow permissible forms: reputable law lists, ordinary simple professional cards, simple signs, and announcements; the matter was referred to the IBP, OBC, and OSG for appropriate action.)

**Khan, Jr. v. Simbillo (A.C. No. 5299 and G.R. No. 157053, August 19, 2003)** — Atty. Rizalino Simbillo ran a paid advertisement in the _Philippine Daily Inquirer_ (July 5, 2000) reading "ANNULMENT OF MARRIAGE Specialist 532-4333/521-2667," with similar ads in the _Manila Bulletin_ (August 2 and 6, 2000) and _The Philippine Star_ (August 5, 2000). When a Supreme Court Public Information Office staffer called, Mrs. Simbillo said her husband "can guarantee a court decree within four to six months" for a fee of P48,000.00, "half payable at the time of filing... and the other half after a decision." The Court (Ynares-Santiago, J.) found him guilty of violating Rules 2.03 and 3.01 of the CPR and Section 27, Rule 138, and **suspended him one year**. Key holdings: "The practice of law is not a business... it is a profession in which duty to public service, not money, is the primary consideration"; solicitation is not absolutely prohibited but must be "compatible with the dignity of the legal profession" and "made in a modest and decorous manner." His self-styling as an annulment "specialist" and guarantee of quick results also "erodes and undermines... the sanctity of marriage." Aggravating his liability, he re-advertised in the _Buy & Sell Free Ads Newspaper_ (August 14, 2001 and October 5, 2001) even after pleading contrition—conduct the Court called "a deliberate and contemptuous affront on the Court's authority."

**Linsangan v. Tolentino (A.C. No. 6672, September 4, 2009)** — Atty. Nicomedes Tolentino, through paralegal Fe Marie Labiano, lured complainant's seafarer clients (named in the case as Cenen Magno, Henry Dy, James R. Gregorio, and Noel Geronimo) to switch counsel with promises of financial assistance—per James Gregorio's affidavit, in exchange for "a loan of P50,000." A calling card read "NICOMEDES TOLENTINO LAW OFFICE CONSULTANCY & MARITIME SERVICES / W/ FINANCIAL ASSISTANCE." The Court (Corona, J.) **suspended him one year** for violating Rules 1.03, 2.03, 8.02, and 16.04 and Canon 3 of the CPR, and Section 27, Rule 138. Holdings: "the practice of law is a profession and not a business; lawyers should not advertise their talents as merchants advertise their wares"; the rule "proscribes 'ambulance chasing'... as a measure to protect the community from barratry and champerty"; and "This crass commercialism degraded the integrity of the bar." Lending money to clients to secure their cases violated Rule 16.04. The Court called the IBP's recommended reprimand "a wimpy slap on the wrist."

**Achacoso v. Macaraig (G.R. No. 93023, March 13, 1991)** — _Caveat for examinees:_ This case is frequently mis-associated with the practice-of-law doctrine, but it is in fact a **civil-service security-of-tenure** case holding that a permanent appointment (with security of tenure) issues only to one possessing all qualifications, and that an appointee lacking eligibility holds only a temporary appointment terminable at will. It is best understood as an analog to _Cayetano_'s "qualifications" theme (Achacoso lacked Career Executive Service eligibility) rather than a profession-vs-business authority. (The related _Philippine Lawyers Association v. Agrava_, G.R. No. L-12426, February 16, 1959, on practice before the Patent Office, is the more apt companion definition case.)

**Recent (2023–2025) CPRA-era developments.** As of mid-2025, the Court has decided CPRA cases under Canon II but **none squarely applying §17 (advertising/solicitation) to reaffirm the profession-not-business doctrine** in the classic commercialization context. Verified CPRA-era Canon II decisions include:

- _In re Atty. Lorenzo Gadon_ (A.C. No. 13521, June 27, 2023) — the first decision applying the CPRA's social-media/propriety regime (Canon II, §§2, 3, 4); disbarment for abusive, misogynistic language. Not an advertising case.
- _Julieta L. Co v. Atty. Jorge P. Monroy_ (A.C. No. 13753, February 6, 2024) — disbarment of a government lawyer under Canon II §§1, 2, and 28 for a deceitful scheme, plus a P20,000 fine for defying IBP orders; not an advertising/solicitation case.

The continuing operative authority on the doctrine therefore remains _Tagorda–Ulep–Simbillo–Linsangan_, which the CPRA expressly carries forward (Canon II §17 is annotated as derived from old Rule 2.03).

### 4. The CPRA (A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC) Provisions

The CPRA was promulgated/launched April 11–13, 2023, published in newspapers of general circulation on May 14, 2023, and took effect **May 29, 2023** (fifteen days after publication). Its General Provisions provide that it **applies to all pending and future cases** (except where retroactive application is infeasible or unjust) and **repeals** the 1988 CPR, Sections 20–37 of Rule 138, and Rule 139-B; the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]] was amended.

- **Canon I (Independence).** Requires merit-based practice and freedom from improper influence; a lawyer relies "solely on the merits of a cause." Independence from external commercial or political pressure underlies the profession-not-business principle.
- **Canon II (Propriety).** The principal locus of the doctrine (44 sections):
    - **§17 (Non-solicitation and impermissible advertisement).** "A lawyer shall not, directly or indirectly, solicit, or appear to solicit, legal business." Advertising is permitted only "with the use of dignified, verifiable, and factual information, including biographical data, contact details, fields of practice, services offered, and the like... In no case shall the permissible advertisement be self-laudatory." It also bars paying media practitioners, award-giving bodies, professional organizations, or personalities for publicity or recognition to attract clients. (Derived from old Rule 2.03.)
    - **§18 (Prohibition against self-promotion).** A lawyer shall not make public appearances/statements regarding a terminated case for self-promotion, self-aggrandizement, or to seek public sympathy.
    - **§25 (Responsibility of a solo practitioner); §26 (Definition of a law firm; choice of firm name).** A law firm is "any private office, partnership, or association, exclusively comprised of a lawyer or lawyers engaged to practice law"—no non-lawyer participation. No false, misleading, or assumed firm name; continued use of a deceased, incapacitated, or retired partner's name is permitted only if the firm indicates the partner's status in all communications.
    - **§27 (Partner who assumes public office).** Such partner must withdraw and have the name removed from the firm name unless allowed by law to practice concurrently.
    - **§§36–44 (Responsible use of social media)** — a new regime requiring online posts to uphold the dignity of the profession, barring disinformation, fraudulent accounts, and disclosure of confidences.
- **Canon III (Fidelity).** Largest canon (56 sections):
    - **§1** codifies the practice-of-law definition (drawing on _Cayetano_).
    - **§41 (Fair and reasonable fees); §42 (Division of fees upon referral)**—regulates fee-sharing among lawyers.
    - **§43 (Non-sharing of fees with non-lawyers).** "A lawyer shall not share, split, or divide or stipulate to divide, directly or indirectly, a fee for legal services with persons or organizations not licensed or authorized to practice law." (Limited exceptions: payments to a deceased lawyer's estate/heirs under a pre-existing agreement; completing a deceased lawyer's unfinished business; retirement plans including non-lawyer employees.)
    - **§44 (Payment of compensation by third party); §§49–50** (accounting for and segregating client funds); **§51** (no acquiring interest in the litigation object); **§52** (no borrowing from/lending to clients).
- **Canon IV (Competence and Diligence).** Requires competent, diligent, conscientious service "regardless of the nature of the legal matter... and whether for a fee or pro bono," and lifelong learning—stressing that professional duty is owed independent of profit. (The task's reference to "Canon IV (Independence)" reflects older draft numbering; in the final CPRA, Independence is Canon I and Competence/Diligence is Canon IV.)
- **Canon V (Equality).** Non-discrimination; service to vulnerable and indigent persons.
- **Canon VI (Accountability).** The disciplinary regime (replacing Rule 139-B): grounds, procedure, and sanctions (serious, less serious, and light offenses), with penalties up to disbarment; decisions are immediately executory.

### 5. Distinct Doctrines Flowing from the Principle

- **"Law is a learned profession" doctrine.** Law requires specialized education, bar passage, an oath, and continuing legal education (MCLE, Bar Matter No. 850); it is a calling demanding intellectual and moral qualifications, not a mere occupation. (_Tagorda_; _Bayot_; _[[Cayetano v. Monsod|Cayetano]]_.)
- **"Public service first / public interest" doctrine.** Duty to public service and the administration of justice is the primary consideration; "emolument is a byproduct." (_[[Burbe v. Atty. Magulta|Burbe v. Magulta]]_; _Khan v. Simbillo_.) Lawyers are officers of the court owing duties to client, courts, the Bar, and the nation. (_Edillon_.)
- **Doctrine against commercialization of legal services.** Lawyers may not advertise like merchants, hawk services, or use "crass commercialism." (_[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep]]_; _Simbillo_; _Linsangan_; CPRA Canon II §§17–18.)
- **Unauthorized practice as quasi-public harm.** Allowing non-lawyers/corporations to render legal services harms the public, who lack protection of the bar's competence and discipline. (_[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep v. The Legal Clinic]]_; Rule 138.) Hence the bans on corporate practice and non-lawyer participation.
- **Fiduciary duty doctrine.** The lawyer-client relationship is one of trust and confidence; client funds must be held in trust and accounted for; loyalty and confidentiality are paramount. (_[[Burbe v. Atty. Magulta|Burbe v. Magulta]]_; CPRA Canon III §§49–52.)
- **Prohibition on non-lawyer participation in profits.** No fee-sharing with non-lawyers; no non-lawyer equity or management; no partnerships with non-lawyers for legal work. (CPRA Canon III §43; Civil Code Arts. 1815, 1825; Revised Corporation Code; _Lijauco v. Terrado_, A.C. No. 6317, August 31, 2006.) This protects the lawyer's independent professional judgment.

### 6. Legal and Judicial Ethics: How the Rules Operationalize the Principle

- **Advertising and solicitation.** Prohibited: self-laudatory ads, guarantees of results, soliciting business directly or through agents, paying for publicity/awards. Permitted: dignified, verifiable, factual information; reputable law lists; simple professional cards and signs; announcements of firm changes. (CPRA Canon II §§17–18; _[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep]]_; _Simbillo_; old CPR Canon 3, Rules 2.03, 3.01.)
- **Fee arrangements.** Contingency fees are allowed if fair and reasonable (CPRA Canon III §41), but champertous arrangements and "ambulance chasing" are prohibited (_Linsangan_). Referral fees may be divided **among lawyers** in proportion to work or by agreement (§42), but **never shared with non-lawyers** (§43). A lawyer may not lend to or borrow from clients (§52) or advance funds beyond necessary litigation expenses.
- **Law corporations and firm ownership.** Historically and presently, law cannot be practiced through a corporation; the only vehicle is the general professional partnership of lawyers (Civil Code Art. 1767). No non-lawyer ownership, equity, or management; firm names limited to lawyers and subject to anti-deception rules (CPRA Canon II §26; _In re Sycip_; _Dacanay v. Baker & McKenzie_, G.R. No. L-39247, on the misleading use of a foreign firm name).
- **Multi-disciplinary partnerships (MDPs).** Not permitted where they involve sharing legal fees with, or control by, non-lawyer professionals; joint marketing with non-lawyer entities is treated as unauthorized practice (CPRA Canon III §43).
- **Pro bono duty.** A lawyer appointed counsel de officio must render pro bono limited legal services and cannot refuse on conflict-of-interest grounds (CPRA Canon III §36, §48); Canon V requires service to indigents. This affirms that legal service is a public obligation, not contingent on profit.
- **Anti–ambulance chasing.** Solicitation through runners, agents, or brokers is malpractice (Section 27, Rule 138; _Linsangan_), designed to protect the public from barratry (stirring up litigation) and champerty (financing suits for a share of recovery).

### 7. Contrast with Business/Trade and Disciplinary Consequences

Practices prohibited precisely because they treat law as a business/trade include: advertising/soliciting like a merchant; guaranteeing outcomes; self-promotion through media; splitting fees with non-lawyers; operating or fronting a law corporation; using deceptive or goodwill-trading firm names; ambulance chasing; and subordinating client/public duty to profit.

**Consequences.** Under Section 27, Rule 138 (now Canon VI of the CPRA), such acts constitute malpractice, deceit, or gross misconduct, warranting:

- **Reprimand** — e.g., _Bayot_ (single, non-repeated ad; plea of leniency).
- **Suspension** — e.g., _Tagorda_ (one month); _Simbillo_ (one year for repeated annulment-specialist ads); _Linsangan_ (one year for ambulance chasing and client lending); _[[Burbe v. Atty. Magulta|Burbe]]_ (one year for misappropriation).
- **Disbarment / striking from the Roll** — e.g., _Edillon_ (refusal to pay dues; later reinstated); and CPRA-era _Gadon_ (2023) and _Co v. Monroy_ (2024) for grave Canon II violations.
- **Injunctive and quo warranto relief / referral to the Solicitor General** — e.g., _[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep]]_ (enjoining the corporation; referral for possible quo warranto).

### 8. Bar Examination Significance

This principle is a perennial favorite in **Legal and Judicial Ethics** (now examined under the CPRA). Typical patterns:

- **Doctrinal/quote identification:** "The practice of law is a profession, not a business" traced to _Tagorda_ and _Bayot_; "duty to public service, not money" to _[[Burbe v. Atty. Magulta|Burbe]]_ and _Simbillo_.
- **Advertising fact patterns:** A lawyer posts a Facebook ad or newspaper notice as a "specialist," guarantees results, or pays an influencer—test under CPRA Canon II §§17–18 (dignified, verifiable, factual; no self-laudatory content; no paid publicity). Distinguish permissible (law lists, simple cards, factual fields of practice) from impermissible.
- **Solicitation/ambulance chasing:** Use of runners/paralegals to recruit clients—_Linsangan_; Section 27, Rule 138; barratry and champerty.
- **Fee-sharing:** Whether a lawyer may split fees with a non-lawyer referrer (no—§43; _Lijauco v. Terrado_) versus division among lawyers (yes—§42).
- **Firm names and structure:** Retention of a deceased partner's name (compare _In re Sycip_ with current CPRA §26); use of a foreign firm name (_Dacanay v. Baker & McKenzie_); non-lawyer partners/corporate practice (prohibited).
- **Unauthorized practice:** Corporations or "legal clinics" offering legal services (_[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep]]_).
- **Privilege vs. right:** Compulsory IBP membership and dues (_Edillon_; _Arevalo_).
- **Definition of practice:** _[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]_—the broad definition and its application to non-traditional roles.

Examiners reward answers that (a) state the principle, (b) cite the controlling case and the specific CPRA Canon/Section, and (c) apply the dignity/public-service rationale to the facts.

## Recommendations

For bar reviewees and teachers using this as a reference:

1. **Anchor every answer in both case and code.** Pair the doctrine's leading case with its CPRA provision: advertising/solicitation → _Simbillo_/_[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.|Ulep]]_ + Canon II §§17–18; fee-sharing → _Lijauco_ + Canon III §43; firm names → _Sycip_ + Canon II §26; privilege → _Edillon_ + Article VIII §5(5). Examiners increasingly expect CPRA section numbers.
2. **Master the permissible/impermissible advertising line.** Memorize §17's safe harbor (dignified, verifiable, factual; biographical data, contact details, fields of practice; not self-laudatory) and the prohibited categories (guarantees, paid publicity, self-promotion).
3. **Watch the numbering trap.** In the final CPRA, **Canon I is Independence and Canon IV is Competence and Diligence** (some draft materials reversed these). Cite the final, effective numbering.
4. **Note doctrinal evolution, don't just memorize old holdings.** _Sycip_'s flat ban on deceased-partner names is now modified by CPRA §26 (allowed with a status indication). Flag this in essays for full credit.
5. **Track CPRA-era jurisprudence as it develops.** Because no CPRA decision has yet squarely applied §17 to advertising, cite the pre-CPRA line as controlling but note the CPRA's codification. **Benchmark to revisit:** if and when the Court issues a §17 advertising/solicitation disbarment or suspension, update teaching materials to lead with it.
6. **For practitioners:** structure any legal-tech, referral, or marketing arrangement so that (a) no fee is shared with non-lawyers, (b) no non-lawyer obtains equity or control over professional judgment, and (c) all advertising is dignified, verifiable, and non-self-laudatory. Non-compliance risks suspension or disbarment.

## Caveats

- **No CPRA-era §17 advertising case yet exists (as of mid-2025).** This is a sourced "absence of evidence" finding; an unreported or very recent 2025 resolution could exist. Treat the pre-CPRA line as controlling but provisional.
- **CPRA effectivity date discrepancy.** The CPRA's own §3 Effectivity Clause text reads that it "shall take effect on April 11, 2023" (its promulgation/launch date), yet it was published in newspapers of general circulation on May 14, 2023 and, per official statements (e.g., the AMLC), "took effect on 29 May 2023, fifteen (15) days after its publication." Reviewers should be aware of and able to explain this internal discrepancy; the operative effectivity for most purposes is the post-publication date (May 29, 2023).
- **Draft vs. final Canon numbering.** Several widely circulated reviewers use the proposed draft's numbering (where advertising sections or the Independence/Competence canons were ordered differently). This report uses the final, published CPRA structure; verify section numbers against the official A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC text.
- **Achacoso v. Macaraig** is a civil-service security-of-tenure case, not a profession-vs-business authority; it is included because the task listed it, with the correction noted.
- Some underlying case digests were drawn from secondary databases (jur.ph, chanrobles, Studocu); for citation in formal work, verify full texts against the Supreme Court E-Library (elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph) or lawphil.net.
