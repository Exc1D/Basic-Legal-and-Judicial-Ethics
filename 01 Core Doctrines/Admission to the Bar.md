# Admission to the Bar

## Rule

Admission to the practice of law is not a matter of legislation. Under Article VIII, Section 5(5) of the 1987 Constitution, the Supreme Court has the exclusive power to promulgate rules on admission to the practice of law, the Integrated Bar, and legal assistance to the underprivileged. Congress may not lower, raise, or bypass the qualifications the Court sets, and the Court's power over **admission to practice** is distinct from the State's power over **legal education**, which remains an executive function exercised through the Legal Education Board under the Legal Education Reform Act (RA 7662). The Court merely uses completion of LEB-prescribed law courses as a filter for who may sit the Bar; it does not thereby take over legal education itself (*Pimentel, et al. v. Legal Education Board*, G.R. Nos. 230642 & 242954, 10 September 2019).

The governing rule is **Rule 138 of the Rules of Court**. Rule 138 was substantially amended by **A.M. No. 24-10-05-SC** (En Banc Resolution dated 12 August 2025, effective 3 November 2025), which modernized the qualifications, the academic prerequisites, and the format of the Bar Examinations. Because many review materials printed before late 2025 still carry the pre-amendment text, this is a live bar-trap area: always confirm which version of Section 2 is being tested.

## Requirements for admission (current rule)

Under Rule 138, Section 2, as amended, every applicant for admission to the Bar must show:

1. **Philippine citizenship.**
2. **Legal age** — the amended rule dropped the old fixed "twenty-one years of age" figure in favor of simply being of legal age.
3. **Good moral character**, proven by satisfactory evidence before the Supreme Court.
4. **No charge involving moral turpitude decided with finality** against the applicant, in any Philippine or foreign court or tribunal.
5. **Compliance with the academic requirements** — completion of the prescribed law courses (Political Law and Public International Law, Commercial Law, Taxation Law, Civil Law and Land Titles and Deeds, Labor Law and Social Legislation, Criminal Law, Remedial Law, and Legal and Judicial Ethics) in a law school recognized by the Legal Education Board, **and** completion of the **Clinical Legal Education Program (CLEP)**.

> **Bar trap:** the amended rule no longer lists **residency in the Philippines** as a separate, free-standing requirement, and it raised the moral-turpitude bar from a charge merely "filed or pending" to one **"decided with finality."** Both changes make the current rule more permissive than the version most older reviewers quote. See [[Law Student Practice Rule]] for what completing CLEP now requires — CLEP used to matter only for students who wanted limited court practice; it is now a **prerequisite to sit the Bar at all**.

### What older materials still show

Pre-amendment Section 2 required: (1) Philippine citizenship, (2) **residence in the Philippines**, (3) **at least 21 years of age**, (4) good moral character, (5) no charge of moral turpitude **filed or pending**, and (6) compliance with academic requirements under the older Sections 5 and 6 (a prescribed four-year law course plus a pre-law degree carrying specific majors such as political science, logic, English, Spanish, history, or economics). In practice, the pre-law major list had already been effectively superseded years earlier by RA 7662 and Legal Education Board issuances, which allow any bachelor's degree that carries the LEB-prescribed unit distribution. Treat the pre-amendment list as historical background and a way to spot outdated reviewer text, not as the controlling rule today.

### Foreign law graduates

A Filipino citizen who obtained a Juris Doctor or equivalent law degree from a foreign law school may sit the Philippine Bar Examinations only after submitting to the Supreme Court proof that the degree was completed and that the foreign law school is recognized or accredited by the proper authority in that jurisdiction. This traces back to **Bar Matter No. 1153** (9 March 2010), which first opened the Philippine Bar to foreign-trained Filipino law graduates, and is now folded into the amended Rule 138's foreign-degree provisions. This is a distinct question from whether an actual foreign national may practice law in the Philippines — see [[Foreign Lawyers]] for that separate, much narrower topic.

### Citizenship in practice: election, loss, and reacquisition

"Philippine citizenship" as an admission requirement is not always a simple yes-or-no fact, and two cases anchor the ways it gets litigated:

- **Election of citizenship.** A legitimate child born under the 1935 Constitution to a Filipino mother and an alien father did not acquire Filipino citizenship at birth — only an inchoate right to elect it upon reaching the age of majority, within a period the Court has construed as roughly **three years**. In [[In Re Application for Admission to the Philippine Bar of Vicente D. Ching|In Re: Vicente Ching]], an applicant who passed the Bar Examinations was nonetheless denied admission because he elected Philippine citizenship fourteen years after reaching majority — far beyond any reasonable reading of the rule — and decades of de facto life as a Filipino (voting, holding office, working as a CPA) could not substitute for a timely formal election.
- **Loss and reacquisition.** Citizenship, once validly held, can also be lost — most commonly through naturalization in another country — and later reacquired under RA 9225 (the Citizenship Retention and Re-Acquisition Act of 2003). In [[In Re Petition to Re-acquire the Privilege to Practice Law, B.M. No. 2112|In Re: Muneses]], the Court held that reacquiring citizenship under RA 9225 does not, by itself, restore a lawyer's practice privilege: the lawyer must petition the Court directly and retake the Lawyer's Oath, because citizenship is a **continuing** requirement, not merely a one-time admission fact. See [[Continuing Requirements for the Practice of Law]] for the fuller treatment of citizenship as an ongoing obligation.

## From passing the exam to practicing law

Passing the Bar does not, by itself, authorize the practice of law. Rule 138 still requires, after a passing result:

1. **Oath-taking** — subscribing to the Lawyer's Oath before the Supreme Court. See [[Revised Lawyer's Oath]].
2. **Signing the Roll of Attorneys** — the act that formally makes a bar passer a member of the Philippine Bar and gives the clerk of court's certificate its effect as authority to practice.

Until both steps are complete, a successful examinee is a **bar passer**, not yet a **lawyer**, and any act of practice in the interim is unauthorized practice of law. See [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] for the definition of practice being applied, and [[Aguirre v. Rana]] for the consequence of practicing before signing the Roll.

## The 2025 Bar Examinations, in outline

The amended Rule 138 made the Bar Examinations **electronic and regionalized**, with testing centers designated across the country rather than a single Manila venue. The exam is held in September over three days, clustered as follows: Day 1 — Political and Public International Law (15%) and Commercial and Taxation Laws (20%); Day 2 — Civil Law and Land Titles and Deeds (20%) and Labor Law and Social Legislation (10%); Day 3 — Criminal Law (10%) and Remedial Law, Legal and Judicial Ethics with Practical Exercises (25%). A general average of 75% remains the default passing standard, subject to the Supreme Court En Banc's discretion to set a different rate for a given year. This reform responded to long-standing calls going back to the 2019 National Legal Education Summit for a more accessible, digitized, and regionally decentralized Bar.

## Good moral character: condition precedent and continuing requirement

Good moral character does the most work of any single requirement in this area because it operates twice: once as a **condition precedent** to admission, and again as a **continuing requirement** for remaining a member of the Bar in good standing. A finding of good moral character at the time of admission does not immunize a lawyer from later scrutiny of conduct that occurred before or after admission — proof of bad moral character can still support discipline later, and conversely a rehabilitated applicant is not permanently barred by past misconduct if good moral character is convincingly re-established. See [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712]] for the leading illustration of rehabilitation and conditional admission, and [[Aguirre v. Rana]] for the separate point that admission and authority to practice are not the same event.

The "continuing requirement" half of the doctrine has its own leading illustration: in [[Garrido v. Attys. Garrido and Valencia]], the Court disbarred two lawyers for a bigamous-in-substance relationship sustained over decades, holding that the time elapsed between the immoral conduct and the complaint does not matter — the only question is whether the lawyer *currently* possesses the moral fitness the profession demands. Read Argosino and Garrido together: Argosino shows the Court forgiving past misconduct before admission upon a convincing showing of rehabilitation; Garrido shows the Court withdrawing the privilege after admission once gross immorality is proven, however long ago it began.

## Related cases

- [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] — the definition of "practice of law" invoked whenever admission or qualification for a law-related office is contested.
- [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712]] — good moral character as both a threshold and a rehabilitative inquiry before oath-taking.
- [[Garrido v. Attys. Garrido and Valencia]] — good moral character as a continuing requirement; disbarment for gross immorality regardless of how long ago it began.
- [[Aguirre v. Rana]] — passing the Bar is not admission; unauthorized practice before signing the Roll is still unauthorized practice.
- [[Edillon, A.C. No. 1928, 84 SCRA 554 (August 3, 1978)]] — integration of the Bar and the compulsory obligations that follow admission.
- [[In Re Application for Admission to the Philippine Bar of Vicente D. Ching]] — election of citizenship must be made within a reasonable time; passing the Bar does not cure an untimely election.
- [[In Re Petition to Re-acquire the Privilege to Practice Law, B.M. No. 2112]] — reacquiring citizenship under RA 9225 does not automatically restore the practice privilege.
- [[Pimentel v. Legal Education Board]] — the Court's exclusive power over admission to practice, distinct from LEB's power over legal education generally.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Rule 138 still requires 21 years of age and Philippine residency."

Outdated. Under the amended Section 2 (A.M. No. 24-10-05-SC, effective 3 November 2025), the requirement is simply "legal age," and a stand-alone residency requirement no longer appears. Examinees relying on pre-2025 material will answer this incorrectly.

### Trap 2: "A pending criminal case for a crime involving moral turpitude automatically disqualifies an applicant."

Outdated under the amended rule. The current text disqualifies only where such a charge has been **decided with finality** against the applicant — a pending, undecided charge is no longer, by itself, an automatic bar.

### Trap 3: "Completing CLEP only matters if a student wants to appear in court while still in law school."

Wrong under the current rule. CLEP completion is now a Bar Examination prerequisite for every applicant, independent of whether the student ever sought Level 1 or Level 2 certification under [[Law Student Practice Rule]] to personally appear in a case.

### Trap 4: "Passing the Bar makes you a lawyer."

Wrong. A bar passer must still take the Lawyer's Oath and sign the Roll of Attorneys before practicing law; see [[Aguirre v. Rana]].

### Trap 5: "Good moral character is only checked once, at admission."

Wrong. It is a continuing requirement; loss of good moral character after admission is a ground for discipline, not merely a bar to entry.

### Trap 6: "Passing the Bar Examinations cures any lingering doubt about citizenship."

Wrong. See [[In Re Application for Admission to the Philippine Bar of Vicente D. Ching|In Re: Vicente Ching]] — an applicant who passed the Examinations was still denied admission because his election of Philippine citizenship, made fourteen years after reaching the age of majority, was untimely. Citizenship must be established as a fact independent of exam performance.

### Trap 7: "Reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 automatically lets a lawyer resume practicing."

Wrong. See [[In Re Petition to Re-acquire the Privilege to Practice Law, B.M. No. 2112|In Re: Muneses]] — a Supreme Court petition and a fresh Lawyer's Oath are still required.

## Recall questions

Q: What body has exclusive power to set the qualifications for admission to the Bar?  
A: The Supreme Court, under Article VIII, Section 5(5) of the Constitution.

Q: Under the amended Rule 138, does an applicant still need to be "at least 21 years of age" and a "resident of the Philippines"?  
A: No. The amended Section 2 requires only "legal age" and drops the stand-alone residency requirement.

Q: What is the effect of a pending, undecided moral-turpitude charge on admission under the amended rule?  
A: None by itself — only a charge decided with finality against the applicant disqualifies.

Q: Is completing the Clinical Legal Education Program (CLEP) still optional?  
A: No. It is now a prerequisite to sit the Bar Examinations for every applicant.

Q: Does passing the Bar Examinations authorize the practice of law?  
A: No. The examinee must still take the Lawyer's Oath and sign the Roll of Attorneys.

Q: Is good moral character checked only at the time of admission?  
A: No. It is both a condition precedent to admission and a continuing requirement for remaining a lawyer in good standing.

Q: Within what period must a legitimate child born under the 1935 Constitution to a Filipino mother and an alien father elect Philippine citizenship?  
A: Within a reasonable time after reaching the age of majority, construed as roughly three years.

Q: Does reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 automatically restore a lawyer's privilege to practice?  
A: No. The lawyer must petition the Supreme Court directly and retake the Lawyer's Oath.
