# Practice of Law

## What counts as practice of law

Under the CPRA, the practice of law is the rendering of legal service, or the performance of any act involving the application of law, legal principles, legal procedure, and legal judgment to a person, cause, matter, or engagement — in court or out of it. That sentence sounds tidy, but the Supreme Court has spent decades stretching and testing exactly how far "out of it" goes, and the case that stretched it furthest is also the one taught in every Legal Ethics class for a different subject entirely: a constitutional confirmation fight.

**[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]** arose when Renato Cayetano challenged the Commission on Appointments' confirmation of Christian Monsod as COMELEC Chairman, on the sole ground that Monsod had not been "engaged in the practice of law for at least ten years" as Article IX-C requires. Monsod's resume made the challenge plausible: after passing the 1960 Bar and a brief stint in his father's law office, he spent his career as a World Bank operations officer negotiating loans in Costa Rica and Panama, then as an investment-bank and conglomerate executive, then as NAMFREL's national chairman, and as a member of the 1986 Constitutional Commission — decades without a single reported court appearance. Justice Paras, writing for a divided Court, refused to read "practice of law" as litigation-only. Drawing on the Constitutional Commission's own deliberations — where Commissioner Foz had gotten the body to confirm on the record that a lawyer's work in a body like the COA counts as practice so long as it requires applying legal knowledge — the majority held that negotiating contracts, giving legal advice, and applying legal judgment to business and policy problems is practice of law even without a courtroom in sight. Monsod, the majority wrote, had practiced as "a lawyer-economist, a lawyer-manager, a lawyer-entrepreneur of industry, a lawyer-negotiator of contracts." The confirmation stood.

Four justices dissented, and their disagreement is worth knowing because their narrower test is the one that actually governs most of the cases later in this note. Justice Padilla argued that "practice" requires habituality — customarily and repeatedly holding oneself out to the public as a lawyer, for compensation, in an actual attorney-client relationship — and cited **[[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva|People v. Villanueva]]** for that proposition. *Villanueva* is a smaller, older case with an outsized influence: a City Attorney named Ariston Fule, authorized by the Secretary of Justice and on official leave, appeared once as a private prosecutor in a malicious-mischief case, without pay. The Court held that a single, uncompensated, officially sanctioned appearance is not "practice" at all, because practice "consists in frequent or customary actions, a succession of acts of the same kind" — not an isolated act. Justice Cruz's *Monsod* dissent warned that without some habituality requirement, the majority's definition would sweep in real estate brokers and insurance adjusters; Justice Gutierrez added that Monsod, if anything, had *lawyers working for him* rather than practicing himself.

Both tests are good law today, because the Court reaches for whichever one fits the question actually being asked. *Monsod*'s broad, no-habituality-required test controls when the question is definitional — does an activity, by its nature, require legal knowledge and judgment? *Villanueva*'s narrower, habituality-plus-compensation test controls when the question is regulatory — has this specific person crossed into the kind of *engagement* a licensing or conflict-of-interest rule is trying to police? The rest of this note is organized around cases that apply one test or the other to a specific fact pattern, and knowing which test is in play is usually the key to the right answer.

## Self-representation is not practice of law — but representing someone else, even briefly, is

**[[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]]** draws this line cleanly. Imelda Maderada, a court clerk, filed a forcible entry case in her own name and also appeared for her co-plaintiff. The Office of the Court Administrator tried to charge her with moonlighting for both acts. The Supreme Court split them apart: her own case was pure self-representation under Rule 138, Sec. 34's guarantee that a party may litigate *in propria persona* — "a person does not 'practice law' by representing himself any more than he practices medicine by administering first aid to himself," the Court wrote — and self-representation carries no habituality or compensation at all, so it can never be "practice." But appearing for her co-plaintiff was different in kind, not degree: she was now applying legal judgment to *someone else's* cause, without SC authority to do so as a court employee. She was reprimanded for that act alone, not for filing her own complaint.

## An isolated, unpaid appearance still needs written permission — even when the judge already knows

This is where *Villanueva*'s habituality test does real work — and where it stops being the whole story. **[[Office of the Court Administrator vs. Atty. Misael M. Ladaga|OCA v. Atty. Ladaga]]** involved a branch clerk of court who appeared *pro bono*, on approved leave, across four separate hearing dates for his cousin's falsification case — his presiding judge knew about every one of them. Applying *Villanueva*, the Court agreed these were "isolated instances," not the habitual, compensated engagement the private-practice prohibition targets, so Ladaga was cleared of that charge entirely. But the Court still reprimanded him — for a completely separate reason. Civil Service rules require **written permission from the head of the Department** before any government employee engages in outside professional activity, and for judiciary employees that head is the **Supreme Court itself**, not the presiding judge down the hall. Ladaga's judge knowing and approving his leave slips did not substitute for the Court's own written go-ahead. The lesson the facts teach: "not private practice" and "properly authorized" are two separate boxes, and clearing the first does not automatically clear the second.

## Work that looks clerical can still be practice of law, if it takes legal judgment

**[[Arienda v. Monilla]]** shows the *Monsod* definition doing regulatory work rather than constitutional work. Evelyn Monilla, a court stenographer with no law degree, agreed — together with her lawyer husband — to prepare and finalize an extrajudicial settlement of a client's mother's estate, for a fee of nearly ₱50,000. On paper this looks like paperwork, not "practice." The Supreme Court disagreed: drafting an extrajudicial settlement requires applying legal knowledge to a specific estate's facts, which is exactly the *Cayetano v. Monsod* definition, and Monilla had no license to do it. She was found guilty of simple misconduct and fined four months' salary out of her retirement benefits — a reminder that the line between "clerical" and "practice" is drawn by whether legal judgment was exercised, not by whether the output looks like a form.

## A corporation cannot practice law — not even by employing lawyers to run it

**[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.]]** is the leading case for this rule, and its facts are almost a caricature of what the rule is meant to prevent. The Legal Clinic, Inc. ran newspaper ads soliciting clients for "secret" marriages, foreign divorces, annulments, adoption, and immigration visas — priced, packaged, and sold like a menu. It was incorporated by a lawyer, Atty. Rogelio Nogales, who staffed it with lawyer "specialists" backed by paralegals, modeled openly on a medical clinic. The Supreme Court held the clinic was engaged in the practice of law regardless of the corporate wrapper, because Rule 138, Sec. 1 reserves practice to *natural persons* duly admitted to the Bar — a restriction that exists precisely so the Court retains disciplinary reach over whoever is doing the legal work, which a corporate shield defeats. The Court also rejected the clinic's claim that its non-lawyer staff were merely "paralegals" American-style, since the Philippines has no accredited framework for that role, and it found the advertising itself unethical under Canon 3. Only Atty. Nogales — the one natural person the Court could actually discipline — was reprimanded; the corporation's fate was referred to the Solicitor General for a possible *quo warranto* action.

## Passing the Bar is not enough — and the two steps after it cannot be skipped, even by accident

Two cases make this point from opposite directions. In **[[Aguirre v. Rana]]**, Edwin Rana passed the 2000 Bar and was scheduled for mass oath-taking — but the day before, he was already signing pleadings as "Counsel for... Vice Mayoralty Candidate George Bunan" before the Municipal Board of Election Canvassers. The Supreme Court denied his admission outright: "a bar candidate who is morally unfit cannot practice law even if he passes the bar examinations," and holding himself out as counsel before he had any authority to do so was itself the moral unfitness the admission process screens for. Full-fledged membership requires *both* the oath *and* signing the Roll of Attorneys — and practicing before either is complete can permanently bar the door.

**[[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|In Re: Medado]]** reaches almost the opposite outcome on almost the same facts, and the difference is entirely about intent and timing. Medado took the oath in 1980, misplaced his notice to sign the Roll, and mistakenly believed the document he'd signed at the PICC entrance *was* the Roll. He practiced corporate and tax law for over thirty years before rediscovering the misplaced notice while cleaning out old files. The Court allowed him to belatedly sign — but only after a penalty. His mistake of fact (believing he'd already signed) excused him only up to the moment he found the notice; from that instant he knew he was not a full member of the Bar, and continuing to practice afterward was a **willful** violation, because *ignorantia facti excusat; ignorantia legis neminem excusat* — a mistake of fact may excuse, but a mistake of law never does. Rana was denied admission outright because his unauthorized practice was deliberate and preceded any admission step; Medado was allowed to cure his because it began as a genuine accident, disclosed voluntarily decades later. See [[Signing of the Roll of Attorneys]] for the fuller treatment of this contrast.

## Suspension and disbarment do not just limit practice — they end it, and a power of attorney cannot resurrect it

**[[Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas]]** shows how far a suspended lawyer will sometimes go to keep working, and how little a paper trick helps. Atty. Edgardo Era was two years into a suspension for an unrelated conflict-of-interest case when he showed up at a public auction on behalf of his old labor-case clients, bid on their behalf, obtained the certificate of sale, and personally negotiated a settlement in his own law office — all under a Special Power of Attorney he argued let him act as a mere agent, not a lawyer. The Supreme Court rejected the SPA as a shield: the power existed only *because* he was counsel, his clients had even required his written consent before any settlement, and letting the SPA launder his suspended status would "cunningly undermine" the suspension order itself. He drew three more years on top of the original two; his associate Atty. Bragas, who helped him force open the client's premises knowing he was suspended, drew one month for assisting.

**[[Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Attys. Rivera and Bangoy]]**, decided under the CPRA, refines exactly where "assisting" begins. Atty. Bede Tabalingcos had been disbarred for bigamy but kept filing motions at the BSP under his old firm's name. Atty. Bangoy co-signed a motion with him two months *after* Bangoy admitted knowing about the disbarment — active, substantive participation, and a six-month suspension followed. Atty. Rivera, on the other hand, had only signed a routine notice of change of address as "co-counsel of record" for the same firm — the Court called this passive and ministerial, since a law firm has a juridical personality distinct from its named partners, and cleared him of the unauthorized-practice charge entirely. (Rivera was not off the hook, though: his own pattern of simply ignoring the Court's orders throughout the case — never filing a comment, never paying an earlier fine — was itself gross misconduct, drawing a ₱50,000 fine on top of the unpaid original one.) Read together, *Bonifacio* and *Gonzales* mark the line: actively performing legal work for a suspended or disbarred colleague is assisting unauthorized practice; being named as counsel of record on paper, without more, is not.

## A lawyer's authority to appear, once given, is presumed to continue — even through appeal

Not every case in this area is about catching unauthorized practice; **[[Sps. Agbulos v. Gutierrez]]** is about protecting authorized practice from being second-guessed. Atty. Magbitang filed a notice of appeal for his clients after their case was dismissed, without first getting their express, individual sign-off — and one client wrote the trial court to say she'd been "surprised" to learn of it. The Supreme Court held the appeal valid anyway: Rule 138, Sec. 22 presumes an attorney who appeared below continues to represent the client on appeal until a formal withdrawal is filed, and even a technically unauthorized act can be **ratified** by the client's own silence once the appeal is underway — ratification that retroacts to validate the lawyer's original act. If anything, the Court treated filing the appeal without waiting for explicit instructions as commendable diligence, not overreach. See [[Privileges of a Lawyer]] for this presumption discussed as one of the privileges the practice carries.

## Detention removes the privilege entirely, with no case-by-case exception

**[[People of the Philippines v. Hon. Bonifacio Sanz Maceda and Avelino T. Javellana|People v. Maceda]]** is the starkest illustration of just how conditional a "privilege" the practice of law really is. Avelino Javellana, a lawyer facing criminal charges, had been allowed to serve his detention in the clerk of court's residence rather than the provincial jail, on account of threats to his life. He used the arrangement to go on practicing law, appearing as counsel in unrelated cases. When the Supreme Court revisited the arrangement years later and found the original threat no longer existed, it did more than send him back to jail: it held that a detention prisoner **cannot practice law at all**, in any case, with the sole exception of appearing to defend his own liberty. The privilege of appearing as counsel presupposes freedoms — to confer with clients, to travel, to prepare — that custodial restraint categorically forecloses; there is no case-by-case balancing once detention status attaches.

## Public officials and unauthorized practice, in brief

Two large sub-topics get their own dedicated notes because the case law under each is deep enough to deserve full treatment: [[Public Officials and Private Practice]] (which government lawyers face absolute versus merely qualified restrictions, and what happens when a lawyer's public role creates a conflict with a private engagement) and [[Unauthorized Practice of Law]] (the full framework for when a non-lawyer's acts, or a lawyer's own lapsed authority, cross the line, including the CPRA's Canon II, Sec. 35 successor to the old "Canon 9" duty not to assist). The cases already discussed above — *Ladaga*, *Bonifacio*, *Gonzales* — are the throughline between this note and those two; start there and follow the links.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Practice of law means court appearance only."

Wrong, on the authority of *Cayetano v. Monsod* itself — a case about a man with almost no courtroom record. Legal advice, drafting, negotiation, and notarial acts all count.

### Trap 2: "Passing the bar already authorizes practice."

Wrong. *Aguirre v. Rana* denied admission outright to a bar passer who acted as counsel before completing the oath and Roll.

### Trap 3: "No compensation means no practice of law."

Only true within the *Villanueva*/habituality frame (an isolated, unpaid, authorized appearance like Fule's or Ladaga's is not "private practice"). It is not true under the *Monsod* frame — Arienda's document preparation was practice of law regardless of what she was paid, and even a free, one-time act of legal judgment for someone else's cause can qualify.

### Trap 4: "A non-lawyer who represents himself is practicing law."

Wrong. *Maderada* is direct authority: self-representation is the exercise of a party's own right, not practice of law for another — but the same case shows the line is crossed the moment the person appears for someone *else*, even a co-plaintiff.

### Trap 5: "A corporation may offer legal services if it hires lawyers."

Wrong. *Ulep* rejected exactly this argument; only natural persons admitted to the Bar may practice, and a corporate structure cannot launder that restriction away.

### Trap 6: "An isolated, unpaid court appearance by a government lawyer needs no clearance at all, as long as it isn't 'private practice.'"

Wrong. *Ladaga* clears the private-practice charge but still reprimands for the separate failure to get the Supreme Court's own written permission — the presiding judge's informal awareness does not count.

### Trap 7: "Decades of otherwise-clean practice cure an unsigned Roll once the lawyer learns about it."

Wrong. In *In Re: Medado*, an honest mistake of fact excused the lawyer only until he discovered the omission; continuing to practice after that point was willful, because a mistake of law never excuses. Compare *Aguirre v. Rana*, where practicing before admission was complete led to outright denial rather than a lenient cure — the difference is that Rana's unauthorized practice was deliberate from the start, while Medado's began as a genuine accident.

### Trap 8: "A Special Power of Attorney lets a suspended lawyer keep working for old clients."

Wrong. *Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas* rejected this exact argument — an SPA that exists only because of the lawyer-client relationship cannot be used to disguise continued legal practice during a suspension.

## Recall questions

Q: Is the practice of law limited to appearances in court?  
A: No. *Cayetano v. Monsod* held that legal advice, drafting, negotiation, and other out-of-court legal work all qualify, so long as they require legal knowledge and judgment.

Q: Why does *Monsod*'s broad test coexist with *Villanueva*'s narrower habituality test instead of replacing it?  
A: Because they answer different questions — *Monsod* asks whether an activity is inherently legal in nature; *Villanueva* asks whether a specific person's conduct rose to the level of habitual, compensated engagement that a licensing or conflict rule targets.

Q: In *Maderada*, why was self-representation cleared but representing a co-plaintiff was not?  
A: Self-representation exercises a party's own right under Rule 138, Sec. 34, and involves no application of legal judgment to another's cause; representing someone else, even briefly and without pay, does.

Q: Why was Atty. Ladaga reprimanded even though his appearances were not "private practice" under *Villanueva*?  
A: Because government and judiciary employees separately need the Supreme Court's own written permission before any outside legal activity — a requirement his presiding judge's informal awareness could not satisfy.

Q: Can a corporation practice law by employing licensed lawyers?  
A: No. *Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.* held that Rule 138, Sec. 1 reserves practice to natural persons, because the restriction exists to keep whoever does the legal work within the Court's disciplinary reach.

Q: What is the key factual difference between *Aguirre v. Rana* and *In Re: Medado*, given that both practiced without a signed Roll?  
A: Rana practiced deliberately before his admission was even complete and was denied admission outright; Medado's lapse began as a genuine mistake of fact, disclosed decades later in good faith, so he was allowed to belatedly sign after a penalty.

Q: Can a Special Power of Attorney authorize a suspended lawyer to keep handling a former client's legal affairs?  
A: No. *Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas* held that an SPA arising from the lawyer-client relationship cannot be used to disguise continued unauthorized practice during a suspension.

Q: Under *Sps. Agbulos v. Gutierrez*, what happens if a lawyer files a notice of appeal without the client's express prior confirmation?  
A: The appeal stands — Rule 138, Sec. 22 presumes continuing authority absent a formal withdrawal, and the client's silence once the appeal proceeds counts as ratification, which retroacts to the lawyer's original act.

Q: May a detention prisoner practice law in any case other than his own?  
A: No. *People v. Maceda* holds the privilege categorically incompatible with custodial restraint, with no exception except appearing to defend his own liberty.
