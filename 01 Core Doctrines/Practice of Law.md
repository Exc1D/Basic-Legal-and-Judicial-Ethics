# Practice of Law

## Rule

The **practice of law** is the rendering of legal service, or the performance of acts involving the application of law, legal principles, legal procedure, and legal judgment to a person, cause, matter, or engagement. It may happen **in court or out of court**.

Under the CPRA, practice of law includes legal work done in the public service or private sector and requires membership in the Philippine Bar as the professional qualification.

## Nature

The practice of law is a **privilege burdened with public interest**, not a natural, property, or constitutional right. It is granted, regulated, suspended, or withdrawn by the Supreme Court because lawyers are officers of the court and participants in the administration of justice.

A lawyer does not practice law merely to earn income. The profession is tied to public service, fiduciary loyalty to clients, candor to courts, and the orderly administration of justice.

## Core idea

A practical test is this:

> Does the act require legal knowledge, legal training, legal procedure, legal judgment, or the application of law to another person’s cause or objective?

If yes, the act likely falls within the practice of law, even if it is done outside the courtroom.

## Indicators

An activity is more likely to be considered practice of law when it involves:

1. **Application of law or legal procedure** — legal principles are applied to facts, documents, claims, rights, liabilities, or remedies.
2. **Legal judgment** — the person is not merely clerically filling blanks but making legal choices or recommendations.
3. **Representation of another** — the act is done for a client, party, organization, office, or cause.
4. **Attorney-client or similar engagement** — the service arises from a professional engagement, public office, legal clinic, or other regulated relationship.
5. **Court or tribunal participation** — appearance, pleading, argument, prosecution, defense, negotiation, or submission before a court or tribunal.
6. **Regularity or compensation** — habitual legal work or paid legal service strengthens the conclusion, although compensation is not always indispensable.

## Common acts that constitute practice of law

- Appearing for a client before courts, tribunals, agencies, or quasi-judicial bodies.
- Drafting pleadings, motions, affidavits, contracts, demand letters, legal opinions, or other documents requiring legal knowledge.
- Giving legal advice on rights, obligations, remedies, risks, or legal strategy.
- Negotiating or settling claims while applying legal judgment for another person.
- Handling litigation, prosecution, defense, appeals, or execution of judgments.
- Performing notarial acts when done by a commissioned notary public.
- Serving as a government lawyer, corporate counsel, legal officer, or other legal professional role requiring Philippine Bar membership.

## What is not automatically practice of law

### Self-representation

A person who represents himself or herself in a case is not practicing law for another. The person acts as a party, not as counsel. This is why self-representation is treated separately from legal representation of another.

### Purely clerical work

Typing, encoding, filing, or mechanically reproducing a document is not practice of law if no legal judgment is used. The line is crossed when the person chooses legal theories, drafts legal language, advises on rights, or applies law to another person’s facts.

### General legal information

General explanation of law for public education is different from legal advice. Legal advice applies law to a specific factual situation and may create professional duties.

Key reminders:

- A party may personally conduct his or her own case, subject to procedural and constitutional limits.
- Some non-lawyer assistance is allowed in first-level courts, administrative tribunals, labor proceedings, cadastral proceedings, and other special settings.
- Law student practice is allowed only within the limits of the applicable student practice rule, certification level, and supervision requirements.
- Some proceedings restrict or prohibit the appearance of lawyers as counsel, such as Katarungang Pambarangay and small claims, subject to specific rules.

## Public officials and private practice

Some lawyers in public office are absolutely prohibited from private practice. Others may practice only within strict limits. The issue is not merely whether the lawyer is licensed, but whether public office creates a constitutional, statutory, civil-service, or ethical restriction. Even where the underlying acts fall short of "private practice" — an isolated, unpaid appearance rather than a habitual one — a government lawyer may still need prior written permission from the head of the department before engaging in it at all; informal clearance from an immediate superior is not enough. See [[Office of the Court Administrator vs. Atty. Misael M. Ladaga]].

## Unauthorized practice of law

Unauthorized practice may occur when a person who is not allowed to practice law performs acts reserved to lawyers, or when a lawyer practices despite a disability, suspension, disbarment, lack of admission, or failure to complete admission requirements. The duty not to assist unauthorized practice — the case law still calls it "Canon 9," after the repealed 1988 Code of Professional Responsibility — now lives in **CPRA, Canon II, Section 35** (non-delegable legal tasks), and it subsumes a lawyer's **own** unauthorized practice, not only assistance rendered to someone else. Assistance itself is not all-or-nothing: actively co-signing pleadings under a disbarred lawyer's firm name is sanctionable, while a merely passive, ministerial filing as counsel of record may not be, because a law firm has a juridical personality distinct from its named partners.

## Related cases

- [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] — broad definition of practice of law; legal work may be in or out of court.
- [[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva]] — isolated appearance by a public official did not necessarily amount to private practice in the disciplinary sense.
- [[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.]] — corporations cannot practice law; commercialized legal services and legal-document services may still involve practice of law.
- [[Aguirre v. Rana]] — passing the bar is not enough; a bar passer must take the oath and sign the Roll before practicing; practicing before either one is complete can permanently bar admission.
- [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado]] — failure to sign the Roll may make later law practice unauthorized once the omission is known; contrast with *Aguirre v. Rana* on when leniency versus outright denial applies.
- [[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]] — self-representation is different from representing another as counsel.
- [[Catu v. Rellosa]] — notarization is practice of law; government lawyers must observe restrictions on private practice.
- [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712]] — good moral character as a condition the Court may defer, and later clear, before oath-taking.
- [[Office of the Court Administrator vs. Atty. Misael M. Ladaga]] — isolated, unpaid court appearances by a court employee are not "private practice," but still require the Supreme Court's prior written permission, not just the presiding judge's informal knowledge.
- [[People of the Philippines v. Hon. Bonifacio Sanz Maceda and Avelino T. Javellana]] — a detention prisoner cannot practice law; the privilege presupposes freedoms incompatible with custodial restraint.
- [[Arienda v. Monilla]] — a non-lawyer court employee who prepares legal documents for a fee is engaged in unauthorized practice of law, regardless of the private, off-duty nature of the arrangement.
- [[Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas]] — a suspended lawyer who keeps acting for clients is practicing law despite the suspension; an associate who knowingly helps him is separately liable for assisting.
- [[Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Attys. Rivera and Bangoy]] — decided under the CPRA; distinguishes active assistance in a disbarred lawyer's unauthorized practice from passive, ministerial association with the same firm name.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: “Practice of law means court appearance only.”

Wrong. Practice of law includes legal work outside court, such as legal advice, legal drafting, legal negotiation, notarial acts, and legal service in public or private employment.

### Trap 2: “Passing the bar already authorizes practice.”

Wrong. Bar passage is not enough. The person must also take the lawyer’s oath and sign the Roll of Attorneys.

### Trap 3: “No compensation means no practice of law.”

Wrong. Compensation is only an indicator. Legal representation, legal advice, or legal judgment for another may still be practice even if unpaid.

### Trap 4: “A non-lawyer who represents himself is practicing law.”

Wrong. Self-representation is the exercise of a party’s own right. It is not the practice of law for another.

### Trap 5: “A corporation may offer legal services if it hires lawyers.”

Wrong. A corporation cannot practice law. Lawyers may not use a corporate vehicle or business front to commercialize legal practice or solicit legal employment.

### Trap 6: “Notarial work is just ministerial.”

Wrong. Notarization is not a casual clerical act. It is connected with the practice of law and public trust.

### Trap 7: “Decades of otherwise-clean practice cure an unsigned Roll once the lawyer learns about it.”

Wrong. In *[[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|In Re: Medado]]*, an honest mistake of fact (believing the Roll had been signed) excused the lawyer only until he discovered the omission. From that point, continuing to practice was willful and unexcused, because a mistake of law — not knowing that practicing without a signed Roll is unauthorized — never excuses. Compare [[Aguirre v. Rana]], where practicing before admission was complete led to outright denial rather than a lenient cure.

## Recall questions

Q: Is the practice of law limited to appearances in court?  
A: No. It includes in-court and out-of-court legal work requiring legal knowledge, training, procedure, or judgment.

Q: Why is practice of law called a privilege?  
A: Because it is granted and regulated by the Supreme Court, subject to continuing mental, moral, and professional qualifications.

Q: What must a bar passer do before practicing law?  
A: Take the lawyer’s oath and sign the Roll of Attorneys.

Q: Is self-representation practice of law?  
A: No. A party representing himself or herself acts as a party, not as counsel for another.

Q: Give examples of out-of-court practice of law.  
A: Legal advice, legal opinions, contract drafting, demand letters, legal negotiation, compliance advice, and notarial acts.

Q: Can a corporation practice law?  
A: No. Law practice is a personal professional privilege of qualified members of the Bar.

Q: Is compensation necessary before an act is considered practice of law?  
A: No. Compensation is an indicator but not an absolute requirement.

Q: Why are public officials treated separately?  
A: Because even if they are lawyers, public office may impose constitutional, statutory, civil-service, or ethical limits on private practice.

Q: Is "Canon 9, Code of Professional Responsibility" still the operative text for the duty not to assist unauthorized practice?  
A: No. The 1988 CPR is repealed; the modern anchor is CPRA, Canon II, Sec. 35, though case law still narrates the duty as a "Canon 9" violation.

Q: Does a mistake of fact excuse unauthorized practice indefinitely?  
A: No. It excuses only until the true facts are discovered; continued practice afterward is willful, because a mistake of law never excuses.
