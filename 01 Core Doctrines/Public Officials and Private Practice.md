# Public Officials and Private Practice

## Rule

A lawyer who enters government service does not stop being a lawyer, but public office layers additional, often stricter, restrictions on top of ordinary CPRA obligations. The examiner's real question is rarely "is this person a lawyer" — it is **which category of restriction applies**: (1) an **absolute** prohibition that bars private practice entirely for as long as the office is held, (2) a **qualified** prohibition that allows practice only within defined limits, or (3) a **retired-official** restriction that survives even after the person leaves office, tied to a pension.

The umbrella rule under the CPRA is **Canon III, Section 21**: a lawyer currently serving in government shall **not** practice law privately, unless otherwise authorized by the Constitution, the law, or applicable Civil Service rules and regulations. Where private practice is allowed at all, it must be upon the **express authority of the lawyer's superior**, for a **stated, specific purpose or engagement**, and only **during an approved leave of absence** — and even then, the lawyer may never represent an interest **adverse to the government**.

## Officials absolutely prohibited from private practice

These offices bar private practice of law entirely, for the duration of the office:

- The **President, Vice-President, Members of the Cabinet, and their deputies and assistants** (1987 Constitution, Art. VII, Sec. 13) — and the prohibition extends to holding *any* other office or employment during tenure, not merely law practice, subject only to constitutionally specified exceptions (e.g., the Vice-President sitting in the Cabinet) or ex officio posts required by the primary functions of the office (*Funa v. Agra*, applying *Public Interest Center, Inc. v. Elma*).
- **Members of the Constitutional Commissions** — COMELEC, COA, CSC (1987 Constitution, Art. IX-A, Sec. 2). Even uncompensated legal advice or document preparation given "as a favor" while holding this office is private practice within the constitutional prohibition, applying the broad *Cayetano v. Monsod* definition of practice of law.
- The **Ombudsman and the Deputy Ombudsmen** (1987 Constitution, Art. IX, Sec. 8, 2nd par.).
- **Judges and other officials and employees of the superior courts**, and **officials and employees of the Office of the Solicitor General** (Rule 138, **Sec. 35** of the Rules of Court — commonly mis-cited in review materials as "Rule 148"; the correct anchor is Rule 138 itself). **[[Ziga v. Judge Arejola]]** shows the rule applied without any softening for sympathetic facts. Ramon Arejola, while still with the Public Attorney's Office, filed a land registration case on behalf of himself and his co-heirs — including his own sister, Nelia Arejola-Ziga. He was then appointed a Municipal Trial Court judge. Rather than withdrawing, he kept the case: nearly a year into his judgeship he filed a Motion for Reconsideration, then a Manifestation, then personally appeared as counsel at a hearing — all in a case that was, by then, both a private engagement and his own family's inheritance dispute. It was his sister and co-heir who filed the complaint against him. The Court found illegal practice of law regardless: the prohibition carries no exception for a case that predates the appointment, and none for keeping a matter in the family — a sitting judge must formally withdraw the moment the robe goes on, not just keep quiet about still handling it.
- **Government prosecutors** (applying the rationale in *People v. Villanueva*, which treats a public prosecutor's appearance for the State as incompatible with simultaneously appearing in a private capacity in the same matter).
- **Civil service officers or employees whose duties and responsibilities require that their entire time be at the disposal of the government** (*Ramos v. Rada*, A.M. No. 202, 22 July 1975).
- **All governors, and city and municipal mayors** (RA 7160, Sec. 90) — full-time local chief executives, unlike ordinary Sanggunian members, are barred outright.
- Anyone else **prohibited by special law**.

## Officials not absolutely prohibited — qualified practice

### Senators and Members of the House of Representatives

Under Art. VI, Sec. 14 of the 1987 Constitution, a Senator or Member of the House may **not personally appear as counsel** before any court, the Electoral Tribunals, or quasi-judicial and other administrative bodies. Note carefully what this does and does not cover:

- It bars **personal appearance** — which includes not only arguing a case, but also **filing a pleading, motion, or answer** on a client's behalf.
- It does **not** categorically bar the legislator from the practice of law altogether; a legislator-lawyer may still, for instance, run a law firm, so long as the legislator does not personally sign or appear.

### Members of the Sanggunian, including Vice-Governors and Vice-Mayors

**Vice-Governors, Vice-Mayors, and members of the Sangguniang Panlalawigan/Panlungsod/Bayan are all, by statutory structure, members of the Sanggunian** — the Vice-Governor presides over the Sangguniang Panlalawigan and the Vice-Mayor over the Sangguniang Panlungsod/Bayan, but presiding does not remove them from the same RA 7160, Sec. 91 category as the rank-and-file Sanggunian members discussed below. This distinguishes them sharply from governors and city/municipal mayors, who are full-time local *chief executives* absolutely barred from private practice under RA 7160, Sec. 90. A Vice-Governor or Vice-Mayor who is a lawyer may practice, subject to the same qualified restrictions as any other Sanggunian member.

Under RA 7160, Sec. 91, Sanggunian members who are lawyers may practice their profession, but **may not**:

- Appear as counsel before any court in a civil case in which a local government unit, or any office, agency, or instrumentality of the government, is the adverse party;
- Appear as counsel in a criminal case where an officer or employee of the government is accused of an offense related to that office;
- Collect fees for appearances in administrative proceedings involving the local government unit they serve, unless defending the LGU's own interests; or
- Use government property or personnel in their private practice, except when the Sanggunian member is defending the interests of the government itself.

### Barangay officials — outside RA 7160's restrictions, but not outside civil service rules

Neither Section 90 (governors and mayors) nor Section 91 (province/city/municipal Sanggunian members) of RA 7160 says anything about the **punong barangay** or members of the **sangguniang barangay**. That silence matters: applying *expressio unius est exclusio alterius* — naming some officials implicitly excludes the rest — a barangay official who is a lawyer is **not** restricted by RA 7160 at all. Neither Sec. 90's absolute bar nor Sec. 91's qualified restrictions reach the barangay level, and it makes structural sense: barangay officials are not required to serve full time, since the sangguniang barangay itself is required to meet only twice a month.

That does not mean a lawyer-punong barangay is free to practice without any further step, as **[[Catu v. Rellosa]]** shows. Atty. Vicente Rellosa, punong barangay of Barangay 723 in Malate, Manila, presided over Katarungang Pambarangay conciliation between the Catu family and Elizabeth Diaz-Catu and Antonio Pastor, occupants of a unit the Catus co-owned. When conciliation failed, Rellosa — in his official capacity — issued the certification allowing the case to proceed to court. The Catus then filed an ejectment suit, and Rellosa entered his appearance as counsel **for the defendants** — the very parties on the other side of the dispute he had just tried to conciliate.

The complainant framed this as a conflict of interest, and the IBP-CBD agreed, recommending discipline under **Rule 6.03 of the CPR** (which bars a lawyer from accepting engagement, after leaving government service, on a matter handled while still in that service) together with RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2). The Supreme Court rejected both theories. Rule 6.03 could not apply because Rellosa was still an **incumbent** punong barangay when he took Elizabeth's case, not a former government lawyer — the rule by its own text reaches only lawyers who have already left government service. RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) gave way too: because RA 7160 is a special law specifically governing elective local officials' practice of profession, it displaces RA 6713's general authorization requirement for that class of official (*lex specialibus derogat generalibus*), and, as just explained, RA 7160 does not touch barangay officials at all.

What the Court found instead was narrower but still fatal. As a civil service officer whose duties do not require his entire time to be at the government's disposal, Rellosa still needed **prior written permission from the head of his department** — for a punong barangay, the DILG Secretary — before engaging in private practice, under **Section 12, Rule XVIII of the Revised Civil Service Rules**. He never obtained it. That omission, not the conflict-of-interest theory the complainant argued, is what the Court punished: failing to secure the required permission was itself "unlawful... conduct" under **Rule 1.01**, a breach of the duty to "obey the laws of the land" under **Canon 1**, and conduct falling short of the integrity Canon 7 demands. The Court raised the IBP's recommended one-month suspension to **six months** — and closed by advising Rellosa to look up and take to heart the meaning of the word *delicadeza*, making clear that the underlying propriety problem the complainant sensed was real, even though the rule that actually decided the case was a missing permission, not Rule 6.03.

Catu is worth remembering precisely because the "obvious" theory turns out to be the wrong one: barangay officials sit outside RA 7160's Sec. 90/91 scheme entirely, but remain bound by the general civil-service permission requirement that applies to any officer or employee not required to serve full time — and Rule 6.03 CPR is not a general conflict-of-interest catch-all; it is narrowly limited to lawyers who have already left the government position in question.

### Retired Justices and Judges

Under **RA 910, Section 1** — the proviso attached to the pension itself, not Section 5, which many reviewers mis-cite (Section 5 concerns GSIS premium contributions, not the practice restriction) — a retired Justice or Judge receiving a pension under the Act may **not appear as counsel**:

- In any **civil case** in which the Government, or any subdivision or instrumentality thereof, is the adverse party; or
- In any **criminal case** in which an officer or employee of the Government is accused of an offense committed in relation to office;

and may not collect a fee for appearing in an administrative proceeding to maintain an interest adverse to the national, provincial, or municipal government. The restriction is a **condition of the pension**, not a bar-membership restriction — a retired Justice or Judge who forgoes the pension is not bound by it, but almost none do.

### Lawyers in government service generally (CPRA Canon III, Sec. 21)

Outside these specific offices, the default CPRA rule already stated above governs every other government lawyer: private practice is barred **unless** the Constitution, a law, or Civil Service rules allow it, and even then only upon the superior's express authority, for a stated purpose, during approved leave, and never adverse to the government. A lawyer who practices privately without securing and documenting this authorization is not shielded merely because the employing office has a general policy allowing outside practice — the authorization must be case-specific. See *Yumol, Jr. v. Ferrer, Sr.*, A.C. No. 6585 (21 April 2005), where a Commission on Human Rights lawyer was held liable for private practice despite a CHR resolution permitting it in principle, because he never obtained the required written request, approval, and leave of absence.

### Government lawyers separated from the service

Leaving government office does not immediately clear the way for private practice, either — RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) imposes a **one-year post-employment restriction**: a former official or employee may not practice their profession "in connection with any matter before the office" they used to serve, for one year after separation. This is a distinct rule from Rule 138, Sec. 35's absolute bar on *current* judges and superior-court/OSG officials — it instead reaches *former* officials and employees generally, and is time-bound rather than permanent.

**[[In Re Query of Atty. Karen M. Silverio-Buffe|In Re: Silverio-Buffe]]** shows how strictly the Court reads that one-year window. Atty. Karen Silverio-Buffe resigned as Clerk of Court VI of RTC Branch 81, Romblon, effective February 2008 — and within the year, she was appearing as private counsel in cases pending before that very same branch, the court she had just finished administering. She did not wait to be caught; she brought the question to the Court herself, arguing that Sec. 7(b)(2) really only codified an ordinary case-by-case conflict-of-interest check, so if no actual conflict existed in a given case, she should be free to appear. The Court rejected that reading outright and fined her: the one-year restriction is a **categorical, time-bound bar**, not a conflict-of-interest inquiry she could argue her way around case by case — her intimate, insider familiarity with that specific branch's records, personnel, and processes was exactly what the rule exists to keep out of the courtroom for a full year, whether or not a particular case felt conflict-free to her.

> **Bar trap:** do not assume that once a government lawyer resigns or retires, private practice — including practice before the lawyer's own former office — is immediately unrestricted. RA 6713's one-year post-employment window is a separate, additional restriction layered on top of whatever restriction applied during incumbency.

## RA 6713 restrictions that apply to all public officials

Separately from the practice-specific rules above, **Section 7(b) of RA 6713** (Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees) prohibits **all** public officials and employees, lawyers or not, from:

- Engaging in the **private practice of their profession**, unless authorized by the Constitution, law, or applicable Civil Service rules — and, if allowed, only upon the superior's express authority, for a stated purpose, during an approved leave of absence (**Sec. 7(b)(2)** — the CPRA's Canon III, Sec. 21 essentially mirrors this civil-service-wide rule for lawyers specifically).
- Owning, controlling, managing, or accepting employment as an officer, employee, consultant, counsel, broker, agent, trustee, or nominee in any private enterprise regulated, supervised, or licensed by their own office, unless expressly allowed by law.
- Recommending any person to a position in a private enterprise that has a regular or pending official transaction with their office.
- Using or divulging confidential or classified information officially known to them by reason of office and not available to the public.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "A Senator who is a lawyer cannot practice law at all while in office."

Wrong. The constitutional bar is narrower: no **personal appearance** as counsel before courts, Electoral Tribunals, or quasi-judicial/administrative bodies. It is not a blanket ban on all law practice.

### Trap 2: "Filing a motion is not the same as 'appearing,' so a legislator may sign and file pleadings personally as long as he does not argue in court."

Wrong. "Appearance" for this purpose includes signing and filing a pleading, motion, or answer — not just oral argument.

### Trap 3: "Sanggunian members may never practice law while in office."

Wrong. Unlike governors and mayors, Sanggunian members who are lawyers may practice their profession, subject to specific restrictions (no appearance against the government, no use of government property/personnel except to defend the LGU's own interest).

### Trap 4: "The retired-judge restriction under RA 910 is found in Section 5."

Wrong, though frequently repeated by reviewers. The counsel-appearance restriction is a **proviso to Section 1** (the pension-granting provision itself); Section 5 addresses GSIS enforcement and premium contributions.

### Trap 5: "If an agency's internal policy allows its lawyers to practice privately, that alone is enough authorization."

Wrong. Under CPRA Canon III, Sec. 21 (and RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2)), the lawyer still needs case-specific express authority from the superior, a stated purpose, and an approved leave of absence. A general agency policy is not a substitute. See *Yumol, Jr. v. Ferrer, Sr.*

### Trap 6: "Rule 138, Section 35's bar on judges and court officials practicing law is found in 'Rule 148.'"

A citation error worth catching in your own notes — the correct anchor is **Rule 138, Sec. 35**, not "Rule 148," which does not exist in the Rules of Court's attorney provisions.

### Trap 7: "Vice-Governors and Vice-Mayors, since they preside over the Sanggunian, are absolutely barred from private practice like governors and mayors."

Wrong. Presiding over the Sanggunian does not convert a Vice-Governor or Vice-Mayor into a local chief executive; they remain Sanggunian members under RA 7160, Sec. 91's qualified-practice regime, not Sec. 90's absolute bar.

### Trap 8: "Once a government lawyer resigns, private practice is immediately unrestricted, including before their old office."

Wrong. RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) imposes a one-year post-employment restriction on practicing before the former office, applied strictly in [[In Re Query of Atty. Karen M. Silverio-Buffe]].

### Trap 9: "Catu v. Rellosa disciplined a punong barangay for a conflict of interest under Rule 6.03 of the CPR."

Wrong, and a favorite reviewer shorthand that overstates the actual holding. The Supreme Court expressly **rejected** Rule 6.03 in [[Catu v. Rellosa]], because that rule only binds a lawyer who has already **left** government service on a matter handled while there — Rellosa was still incumbent. He was disciplined instead for practicing without the **written departmental permission** civil service rules require of an official whose time is not fully at the government's disposal.

### Trap 10: "A punong barangay who is a lawyer is barred from private practice just like a mayor."

Wrong. RA 7160, Sec. 90 restricts only governors and city/municipal mayors; Sec. 91's qualified restrictions reach Sanggunian members at the province/city/municipality level. Neither section mentions the punong barangay or sangguniang barangay members — per [[Catu v. Rellosa]], barangay officials sit outside RA 7160 entirely, though they still need written departmental (DILG) permission under Civil Service Rules before practicing.

## Recall questions

Q: What is the umbrella CPRA rule for lawyers currently serving in government?  
A: They may not practice law privately unless authorized by the Constitution, law, or Civil Service rules, and even then only upon express authority of a superior, for a stated purpose, during an approved leave of absence, and never against the government's interest.

Q: Are Sanggunian members absolutely barred from private practice, unlike governors and mayors?  
A: No. Sanggunian members may practice, subject to specific restrictions on appearing against the government and using government resources.

Q: What exactly does the constitutional bar on Senators/Representatives prohibit?  
A: Personal appearance as counsel before courts, Electoral Tribunals, and quasi-judicial/administrative bodies — including signing or filing pleadings — not law practice generally.

Q: Where in RA 910 is the retired Justice/Judge counsel-appearance restriction actually found?  
A: In the proviso to Section 1 (the pension provision), not Section 5.

Q: Does an agency policy permitting private practice excuse a government lawyer from securing individual authorization?  
A: No. Case-specific express authority, a stated purpose, and an approved leave of absence are still required.

Q: Are Vice-Governors and Vice-Mayors absolutely barred from private practice like governors and mayors?  
A: No. They remain Sanggunian members under the qualified-practice regime of RA 7160, Sec. 91, despite presiding over their respective Sanggunian.

Q: Does resigning from government service immediately clear a lawyer to practice before their former office?  
A: No. RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) imposes a one-year post-employment restriction on practicing before the former office.

Q: Is a lawyer-punong barangay restricted from private practice under RA 7160, the way governors, mayors, and Sanggunian members are?  
A: No. Neither Sec. 90 nor Sec. 91 mentions barangay officials, so RA 7160 imposes no restriction on them — but per [[Catu v. Rellosa]], they still need prior written permission from their department head under Civil Service Rules before practicing privately.

Q: Was the punong barangay in Catu v. Rellosa disciplined for violating Rule 6.03 of the CPR?  
A: No. The Court held Rule 6.03 inapplicable because it binds only lawyers who have already left government service. He was disciplined instead for practicing without the required written departmental permission.
