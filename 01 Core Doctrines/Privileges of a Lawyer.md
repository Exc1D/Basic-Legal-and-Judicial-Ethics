# Privileges of a Lawyer

## Rule

Because the practice of law is a privilege in the first place, it comes bundled with a smaller set of specific privileges a lawyer may invoke in the course of exercising it. These are worth studying together because bar questions like to test whether an examinee can tell a lawyer's *personal* privilege apart from a *client's* right that the lawyer merely asserts on the client's behalf.

## Right to practice before any court in the Philippines

A lawyer validly admitted to the Philippine Bar may appear before **any** court or tribunal in the country — there is no separate state-by-state or region-by-region admission process the way some other jurisdictions require. Admission is national and unitary, flowing directly from the Supreme Court's own constitutional rule-making power over admission to the practice of law.

## Presumption of authority to appear and to continue representing a client

Once a lawyer appears *de parte* for a client before a court, Rule 138, Sec. 22 creates a presumption that the lawyer **continues to represent that client on appeal**, unless the lawyer files a formal withdrawal. This is a genuine privilege: opposing parties and the court itself may rely on the lawyer's continued authority without demanding fresh proof at every stage, and an appearance that turns out to be technically unauthorized at the outset may still be **ratified** by the client's silence or acquiescence, validating the lawyer's acts retroactively. [[Sps. Agbulos v. Gutierrez]] applies exactly this: a lawyer filed a notice of appeal without express prior client confirmation, and the appeal was upheld as validly taken both because Sec. 22's presumption had never been rebutted by a formal withdrawal and because the clients' own silence, once the appeal proceeded, amounted to implied ratification.

## Privileged communication

A lawyer may not, without the client's consent, be examined as to any communication made by the client to the lawyer, or the lawyer's advice given in the course of professional employment, under Rule 130, Sec. 24(b) of the Rules of Court (the rule on disqualification by reason of privileged communication). This is often described as the client's privilege, since the client — not the lawyer — ultimately controls whether to waive it, but it operates as a shield the lawyer invokes and a duty of confidentiality the lawyer independently owes under the CPRA. The privilege protects the communication itself, not the underlying facts; a fact known to the lawyer through some source other than confidential client communication is not shielded merely because the lawyer is also the client's counsel.

## Privileged statements in pleadings and in the course of trial

Statements a lawyer makes in pleadings, motions, or oral argument, if **relevant and pertinent to the case**, enjoy a qualified privilege against actions for libel or slander — advocacy requires candid, sometimes forceful characterizations of the opposing party's conduct, and a lawyer should not have to litigate a defamation suit every time a pleading calls an adversary's claim fraudulent or a witness's testimony false. The privilege is not absolute: it protects only statements germane to the subject of the controversy, not gratuitous personal attacks unconnected to the case.

## Attorney's liens

Under Rule 138, Sec. 37, a lawyer has two distinct liens securing payment of lawful fees and disbursements:

- A **retaining lien** over any funds, documents, and papers of the client that have lawfully come into the lawyer's possession, entitling the lawyer to retain them until the fees are paid — subject to the client's right to demand their surrender upon posting adequate security.
- A **charging lien** over judgments the lawyer has secured for the client, and over the proceeds of any execution, once the lawyer has, at the proper time, recorded a claim for the corresponding fees with the court.

See [[Attorney's Fees]] for the fuller CPRA treatment of fee arrangements and quantum meruit.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "A lawyer admitted in one region of the Philippines must separately qualify to practice in another."

Wrong. Philippine Bar admission is national; there is no regional re-admission requirement.

### Trap 2: "The attorney-client privilege belongs to the lawyer, who may waive it at will."

Wrong. The privilege exists for the client's protection; the client, not the lawyer, controls whether to waive it.

### Trap 3: "A lawyer can be sued for libel for anything said in a pleading, since pleadings are public documents."

Overstated. Statements relevant and pertinent to the case enjoy a qualified privilege against libel or slander actions; only statements unconnected to the controversy lose that protection.

## Recall questions

Q: Must a lawyer separately qualify to practice in each region of the Philippines?  
A: No. Admission to the Philippine Bar is national and unitary.

Q: Who controls whether the attorney-client privilege is waived?  
A: The client, not the lawyer.

Q: What is the difference between a retaining lien and a charging lien?  
A: A retaining lien covers client funds, documents, and papers already in the lawyer's possession; a charging lien covers judgments the lawyer secured for the client and the proceeds of execution.
