# Continuing Requirements for the Practice of Law

## Rule

Admission to the Bar is only the entry point. To **remain** entitled to practice, a lawyer must continue to satisfy a set of standing obligations. Under Rule 138, Section 1, only a member of the Bar who is "in good and regular standing" is entitled to practice law — good standing is not a one-time event but a status a lawyer must keep earning. The continuing requirements are best remembered as seven strands: professional tax, IBP good standing, IBP dues, mandatory continuing legal education, good moral character, legal aid service, and citizenship.

## The seven continuing requirements

### 1. Payment of the Professional Tax Receipt (PTR)

Lawyers engaged in the practice of law must annually pay the professional tax imposed on the practice of a profession, under Section 139 of the Local Government Code (RA 7160). The PTR number is customarily indicated on pleadings together with the IBP and MCLE numbers.

### 2. Membership in good standing in the Integrated Bar of the Philippines (IBP)

Every lawyer admitted to the Bar automatically becomes, and must remain, a member of the IBP (Rule 138, Sec. 1; IBP By-Laws, Art. II). Integration does not create a new professional identity — it simply gives the existing, already-unified body of Philippine lawyers an official national organization. Compulsory IBP membership does not violate a lawyer's freedom of association, because a lawyer does not join any new group by becoming a member; the person was already a lawyer, and integration only organizes lawyers who are already part of one profession (*In re: Edillon*, A.M. No. 1928, 3 August 1978). See [[Edillon, A.C. No. 1928, 84 SCRA 554 (August 3, 1978)]].

### 3. Payment of IBP dues

Annual IBP dues are a regulatory exaction, not a tax — a membership fee collected to fund the profession's self-regulation, distinct from a tax collected for general government revenue (*Report of the Commission on Bar Integration*, A.C. No. 526, 9 January 1973). Non-payment of dues, if allowed to lapse into delinquency, exposes a lawyer to suspension under Rule 139-A.

### 4. Compliance with Mandatory Continuing Legal Education (MCLE)

Under **Bar Matter No. 850**, members of the IBP who are not exempt must complete **36 hours of accredited continuing legal education every three years** (a "compliance period"). The 36 hours are distributed across prescribed subject areas — legal ethics, trial and pretrial skills, alternative dispute resolution, updates on substantive and procedural law and jurisprudence, legal writing and oral advocacy, international law, and an elective component set by the MCLE Committee. The purpose is to keep lawyers current with law and jurisprudence, maintain professional ethics, and raise the standard of practice (Bar Q&A, 2022).

Failure to comply carries escalating consequences: a non-compliance notice, a 60-day cure period, a non-compliance fee, and eventually listing as a **delinquent member** with the possibility of suspension under Rule 139-A. A lawyer who lacks the required MCLE units may still generally practice — because practice of law is broad enough to include any activity requiring legal knowledge, training, and experience (*Cayetano v. Monsod*) — but a non-compliant lawyer **may not sign pleadings**; doing so can draw a fine, discharge from the case, and the client's right to secure new counsel and reclaim fees already paid (OCA Circular No. 79-2014).

Exemptions under B.M. No. 850 include, among others, the President and Vice-President and department secretaries/undersecretaries, Senators and Representatives, the Chief Justice and incumbent or retired members of the Judiciary and the JBC, the Solicitor General and Assistant Solicitors General, the Ombudsman and Deputies, heads of quasi-judicial agencies, full-time law professors and deans of at least 10 years' standing, governors and mayors, those not in law practice at all, and those who have retired from practice with IBP Board of Governors approval.

### 5. Possession of good moral character

Good moral character is not exhausted at admission — it is also a **continuing condition** for remaining a member of the Bar in good standing (*Buenantura v. Atty. Gille*, A.C. No. 7446, 9 December 2020). Loss of good moral character after admission is itself a distinct disciplinary ground. See [[Admission to the Bar]] for the condition-precedent side of this same requirement.

### 6. Legal aid service under the Rules on Unified Legal Aid Service (ULAS)

Effective **1 January 2025**, **A.M. No. 22-11-01-SC** (Rules on Unified Legal Aid Service) requires every "Covered Lawyer" to render **60 hours of pro bono legal aid service** to qualified beneficiaries every 36-month compliance period. At least **25% of the required hours must be rendered personally** by the lawyer; up to **50% of the requirement may instead be satisfied through a financial contribution** to the ULAS Fund, but the remaining half must always be actual service. Covered service includes court and quasi-judicial representation, legal counseling, contract drafting and negotiation assistance, developmental legal assistance (rights awareness, capacity-building, documentation), participation in accredited legal-outreach missions, and mentorship of law students under [[Law Student Practice Rule|Rule 138-A]].

Lawyers **excluded** from ULAS coverage include those in government service or holding elective office who are already absolutely barred from outside practice, Public Attorney's Office lawyers, Shari'ah counselors-at-law under the Shari'ah Public Assistance Office, lawyers whose agency denies authority to render outside pro bono service, lawyers unable to comply due to disability or analogous circumstances, and newly admitted lawyers for the compliance period in which they were admitted. Excluded lawyers must still obtain a Certificate of Exclusion from the ULAS Board.

### 7. Citizenship

The practice of law, like other regulated professions, is constitutionally reserved to Filipino citizens except where a law provides otherwise (1987 Constitution, Art. XII, Sec. 14). A Filipino lawyer who becomes a naturalized citizen of another country and later reacquires Philippine citizenship under **RA 9225** is deemed never to have lost Philippine citizenship or Bar membership — but reacquisition does **not automatically revive the right to practice**; the lawyer must apply to the Supreme Court to resume practice and, ordinarily, must first update MCLE compliance and other continuing requirements (*Petition for Leave to Resume Practice of Law, Benjamin M. Dacanay*, B.M. No. 1678, 17 December 2007).

## Related cases

- [[Edillon, A.C. No. 1928, 84 SCRA 554 (August 3, 1978)]] — mandatory IBP integration does not violate freedom of association; non-payment of dues is a disciplinable ground.
- [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712]] — good moral character as a continuing, rehabilitable standard.
- [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] — the broad definition of practice of law referenced when assessing whether an MCLE-delinquent lawyer may still be considered to be practicing.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "IBP membership is voluntary; a lawyer may opt out."

Wrong. IBP membership is compulsory and automatic upon admission; it does not infringe freedom of association because it does not create a new affiliation — it only organizes lawyers who already share one profession. See [[Edillon, A.C. No. 1928, 84 SCRA 554 (August 3, 1978)]].

### Trap 2: "IBP dues are a tax, so non-payment is merely a civil debt."

Wrong. Dues are a regulatory exaction for the profession's self-governance, not a tax for general revenue — the distinction matters for why the Court, not Congress, may compel payment as a condition of practice.

### Trap 3: "MCLE non-compliance means a lawyer is automatically practicing law illegally."

Wrong. Non-compliance does not, by itself, void the underlying authority to practice — practice of law is broad enough to survive an MCLE lapse — but a non-compliant lawyer cannot validly sign pleadings and risks being listed as delinquent and eventually suspended.

### Trap 4: "Reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 automatically restores the right to practice law."

Wrong. Bar membership is deemed never lost, but the **right to resume practice** requires a separate petition to the Supreme Court and updated compliance with continuing requirements. See *Petition for Leave to Resume Practice of Law, Dacanay*.

### Trap 5: "The 60-hour ULAS requirement can be fully bought out with a donation."

Wrong. At most 50% of the ULAS hours may be satisfied by financial contribution; the remaining half must be actual personal or organizational service, and at least 25% of the total must be rendered personally by the lawyer.

## Recall questions

Q: What does "good and regular standing" under Rule 138, Section 1 actually require?  
A: Continued compliance with PTR payment, IBP membership and dues, MCLE, good moral character, legal aid service, and citizenship — not just a one-time admission.

Q: Why does compulsory IBP membership not violate freedom of association?  
A: Because it does not add a new affiliation; it only organizes lawyers who are already members of one profession by virtue of Bar admission.

Q: How many MCLE hours are required, and over what period?  
A: 36 hours every three years, distributed across prescribed subject areas under B.M. No. 850.

Q: What happens if a lawyer signs a pleading while MCLE-delinquent?  
A: The lawyer may be fined and discharged from the case, and the client may secure new counsel and demand a refund of fees already paid.

Q: How many pro bono hours does ULAS require, and how much of that may be paid off financially?  
A: 60 hours every 36-month compliance period; at most 50% may be satisfied by financial contribution, and at least 25% must always be rendered personally.

Q: Does reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 restore a lawyer's right to practice?  
A: Bar membership is deemed never lost, but resuming practice requires a Supreme Court petition and updated compliance with continuing requirements.
