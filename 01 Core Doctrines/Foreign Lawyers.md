# Foreign Lawyers

## Rule

The Philippines runs one of the more restrictive regimes in the region on this point: the practice of law is reserved for **Filipino citizens** who have been admitted to the Philippine Bar under Rule 138. There is no general reciprocity arrangement and no special examination pathway that lets a foreign-qualified lawyer, as such, gain Philippine Bar admission or a lesser form of local practice authority simply by virtue of being licensed elsewhere. A foreign national wishing to practice law in the Philippines must, like anyone else, independently satisfy Rule 138's requirements — including citizenship, which a foreign national cannot satisfy without naturalization.

## Foreign law graduates distinguished

Do not confuse "foreign lawyers" with "Filipino law graduates of foreign law schools" — the two situations look similar but are governed differently. A **Filipino citizen** who completed a Juris Doctor or equivalent degree at a foreign law school may sit the Philippine Bar Examinations after submitting proof that the degree was validly completed and that the foreign school is recognized or accredited in its own jurisdiction (tracing to Bar Matter No. 1153, now folded into the amended Rule 138). This route is available only because the applicant already satisfies the citizenship requirement; it says nothing about whether an actual foreign national may practice. See [[Admission to the Bar]] for the fuller treatment.

## The narrow exception: international commercial arbitration

The clearest carve-out for genuine foreign lawyers is **international commercial arbitration**. Under RA 9285 (the Alternative Dispute Resolution Act of 2004) and its implementing rules, parties to an international commercial arbitration seated in the Philippines may be represented by counsel of their own choosing, including foreign lawyers, without those lawyers needing to be admitted to the Philippine Bar. This exception is deliberately narrow: it exists to make the Philippines a workable venue for international arbitration, where parties commonly expect to use counsel already familiar with the transaction and, often, a foreign governing law — not to open a general door to foreign law practice. The exception does not extend to litigation in Philippine courts, domestic arbitration, or general legal advisory work in the country.

> **Bar trap:** do not assume the ADR Act's international-arbitration exception extends to mediation generally or to court litigation. [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]] separately covers a non-lawyer's (not specifically a foreign lawyer's) role in ADR Act mediation under Sec. 14 — a different provision, aimed at lay assistance rather than foreign-qualified counsel.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "A lawyer licensed abroad may practice in the Philippines on a reciprocity basis."

Wrong. The Philippines has no general reciprocity arrangement for foreign-qualified lawyers; Bar admission requires satisfying Rule 138 independently, including Philippine citizenship.

### Trap 2: "A Filipino who studied law abroad cannot sit the Philippine Bar."

Wrong. A Filipino citizen with a foreign law degree may sit the Bar Examinations upon proof the degree and school meet the required standards — this is a citizenship-based pathway, not an exception for foreign nationals.

### Trap 3: "Foreign lawyers may appear in any Philippine ADR proceeding."

Overstated. The clear exception is international commercial arbitration seated in the Philippines under RA 9285; it does not extend generally to domestic arbitration, mediation, or litigation.

## Recall questions

Q: May a foreign-licensed lawyer practice law generally in the Philippines on the strength of a reciprocity agreement?  
A: No. The Philippines has no general reciprocity arrangement; Bar admission requires independently satisfying Rule 138, including Philippine citizenship.

Q: In what narrow setting may a genuine foreign lawyer represent a party in the Philippines without Bar admission?  
A: International commercial arbitration seated in the Philippines, under RA 9285.

Q: Does the foreign-law-graduate pathway under Bar Matter No. 1153 apply to foreign nationals?  
A: No. It applies only to Filipino citizens who completed their law degree at a foreign, recognized law school.
