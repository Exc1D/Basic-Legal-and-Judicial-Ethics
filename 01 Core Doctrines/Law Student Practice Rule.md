# Law Student Practice Rule

## Rule

Rule 138-A of the Rules of Court — the **Revised Law Student Practice Rule** — lets a law student render **limited legal services** under supervision, through a law school's **Clinical Legal Education Program (CLEP)**. The current version was adopted by the Supreme Court En Banc on 25 June 2019 as **A.M. No. 19-03-24-SC**, took effect at the start of Academic Year 2020-2021, and was carried forward — and made more consequential — by the 2025 amendments to Rule 138, which now make **CLEP completion a prerequisite to sitting the Bar Examinations** for every law student, not only those who seek to personally appear in court. See [[Admission to the Bar]] for that connection.

The services a certified law student renders are governed by the **CPRA**, the same accountability framework that governs licensed lawyers, and a supervising lawyer's failure to adequately supervise a student is itself a ground for the lawyer's discipline (Circular No. 19, 19 December 1986).

## Certification: the gate to practice

No law student may engage in any CLEP activity without first securing a certification:

- **Level 1 Certification** — for a student who has successfully completed first-year law courses.
- **Level 2 Certification** — for a student currently enrolled in the second semester of third-year law courses. If the student fails to complete all third-year courses, the Level 2 certification is **automatically revoked**.

A certification remains valid until the student completes the law degree, unless sooner revoked for cause. The certification, not mere enrollment or good standing, is what authorizes limited practice — a common bar-trap distinction from simply "being a law student."

## What a certified student may do

### Level 1 practice areas

A student mnemonic worth building for yourself: interview, advise, negotiate, draft, represent, orient, assist.

1. Interview prospective clients.
2. Give legal advice to the client.
3. Negotiate for and on behalf of the client.
4. Draft legal documents — affidavits, compromise agreements, contracts, demand letters, position papers, and similar instruments.
5. Represent eligible parties before quasi-judicial or administrative bodies.
6. Provide public legal orientation.
7. Assist in public-interest advocacies for policy formulation and implementation.

### Level 2 practice areas

A Level 2 student may do everything under Level 1, **plus**:

1. Assist in taking depositions and preparing judicial affidavits of witnesses.
2. Appear on behalf of the client at any stage of the proceedings or trial, before any court, quasi-judicial, or administrative body.
3. In criminal cases, subject to Section 5, Rule 110 of the Rules of Court, appear on behalf of a government agency in the prosecution of criminal actions.
4. In appealed cases, prepare the required pleadings.

### Supervision requirement

Every appearance by a certified student is under the **direct supervision and control** of a member of the IBP duly accredited by the law school. All pleadings, motions, briefs, and memoranda filed must be **signed by the supervising attorney**, for and on behalf of the legal clinic — not by the student.

## Unauthorized practice by a certified student

Under Section 13 of A.M. No. 19-03-24-SC, the following constitute unauthorized practice by a law student:

1. Doing any Level 1/Level 2 act without the necessary certification, or without the supervising lawyer's consent and supervision.
2. Making false representations in the certification application.
3. Using an expired certification.
4. Rendering legal services outside the scope of the authorized practice areas.
5. Asking for or receiving payment or compensation for CLEP services (student practice is, by design, uncompensated).
6. Other analogous circumstances.

## Distinguishing student practice from ordinary self-representation

A recurring bar question tests whether **Rule 138-A** or **Section 34, Rule 138** governs a law student's appearance. The Supreme Court resolved this in **B.M. No. 730** (10 June 1997): the two rules occupy different spaces.

- **Before an inferior (first-level) court**, where issues and procedure are relatively simple, a law student may appear **in a personal capacity, without a supervising lawyer**, but not as counsel — the student instead stands as an **agent or friend of a party litigant** under Section 34, Rule 138, exercising the party's own right to conduct litigation personally with lay assistance.
- **Before any other court or in more complex proceedings**, a student may appear as counsel **only** through the CLEP certification-and-supervision structure of Rule 138-A.

This is why a third-year law student who enters a formal appearance as **private prosecutor** in an inferior court, invoking Section 34 rather than Circular No. 19/Rule 138-A, cannot automatically be turned away for lack of a supervising lawyer — the inferior-court exception is a distinct doctrinal track, not a shortcut around CLEP.

## Related cases

- [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] — the broad, skills-and-training-based definition of practice of law that frames why student practice must be tightly bounded by certification and supervision.
- [[Aguirre v. Rana]] — reinforces that only a defined act of authorization (here, certification, not mere enrollment) permits practice before full admission.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Any law student may appear in court once enrolled in the CLEP."

Wrong. Enrollment alone is not enough — the student must hold a current **Level 1 or Level 2 certification**, and Level 2 is required for appearances at trial.

### Trap 2: "A law student's pleadings should be signed by the student who drafted them."

Wrong. All pleadings, motions, and similar papers must be signed by the **supervising attorney**, for and on behalf of the legal clinic.

### Trap 3: "A law student may never appear in court without a supervising lawyer."

Wrong, in one narrow but heavily tested setting: before **inferior courts**, a student may appear personally as an agent or friend of a party under Section 34, Rule 138 — not as counsel, and not under Rule 138-A supervision. See *B.M. No. 730*.

### Trap 4: "CLEP is only relevant to students who want litigation experience."

Outdated. Under the 2025 amendments to Rule 138, completing CLEP is now a **prerequisite for every applicant to sit the Bar**, regardless of whether the student ever sought Level 1/2 certification to personally appear in a matter.

### Trap 5: "A law student practitioner may accept a fee if the client insists."

Wrong. Receiving payment or compensation for CLEP services is itself listed as unauthorized practice under Section 13 of the Revised Rule.

## Recall questions

Q: What are the two certification levels under the Revised Law Student Practice Rule, and what triggers each?  
A: Level 1, after completing first-year law courses; Level 2, upon enrollment in the second semester of third-year law courses.

Q: Who signs the pleadings filed on behalf of a client represented through CLEP?  
A: The supervising attorney, for and on behalf of the legal clinic — never the student alone.

Q: May a law student appear as private prosecutor before an inferior court without a supervising lawyer?  
A: Yes, but only in a personal capacity as an agent or friend of the party under Section 34, Rule 138 — not as counsel under Rule 138-A.

Q: Can a certified law student accept compensation for CLEP services?  
A: No. Doing so is itself unauthorized practice under Section 13 of the Revised Rule.

Q: Is CLEP completion still optional for a law student who never intends to appear in court?  
A: No. Since the 2025 amendments to Rule 138, CLEP completion is required of every Bar applicant.
