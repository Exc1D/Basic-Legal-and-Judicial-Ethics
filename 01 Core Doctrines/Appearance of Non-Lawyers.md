# Appearance of Non-Lawyers

## Rule

The general rule is that **only members of the Philippine Bar in good standing may appear as counsel** before courts and tribunals. Every instance of a non-lawyer appearing is an **exception**, and each exception is narrow, source-specific, and frequently tested precisely because it looks like an exception to the unauthorized-practice rule but is not — the non-lawyer is either exercising a personal right (self-representation) or acting under a specific statute or tribunal rule that never required Bar membership in the first place.

## When non-lawyers may appear

### 1. Self-representation before first-level courts (MTC/MeTC/MCTC)

Under **Section 34, Rule 138**, in the court of a first-level judge, a party may conduct litigation **in person**, with the aid of an **agent or friend** appointed for the purpose, or with the aid of an attorney. This is the classic setting for the **agent-or-friend** exception, and it is available only at the first level.

### 2. Self-representation before all other courts

In any other court, a party may still conduct litigation **personally**, but any aid the party accepts must come from a **duly authorized member of the Bar** — lay assistance of the "agent or friend" variety is no longer allowed once the case is outside a first-level court.

### 3. Self-defense in criminal cases

Under Section 1(c), Rule 138, and Section 7, Rule 116, an accused may be allowed to defend himself in person when it sufficiently appears to the court that he can properly protect his own rights without counsel. Where no member of the Bar is available in the locality and the gravity of the offense and difficulty of the questions warrant it, the court may appoint **any resident of the province of good repute for probity and ability** to defend the accused — a narrow, availability-driven exception, not a general license for lay defense.

### 4. Officials appearing for the Government

Under Section 33, Rule 138, any official or person appointed or designated by law to appear for the Government of the Philippines has all the rights of a duly authorized member of the Bar to appear in any case in which the Government has a direct or indirect interest — whether or not that official is personally a lawyer.

### 5. Labor proceedings

Non-lawyers may represent parties before the **National Labor Relations Commission or Labor Arbiters**, under the Labor Code provision on appearances (commonly cited as Art. 222, Labor Code), subject to the tribunal's own accreditation and conduct rules for non-lawyer representatives.

### 6. Agrarian proceedings (DARAB)

A non-lawyer may represent a party before the **Department of Agrarian Reform Adjudication Board**, under Section 44, Rule VIII of the DARAB Revised Rules of Procedure. A **DAR Legal Officer** may likewise appear, provided the officer has authority and acts under the direct supervision and control of the Chief Legal Division, and all papers filed are signed by the Chief Legal Division.

### 7. Cadastral proceedings

Under Section 9 of **Act No. 2259 (the Cadastral Act)**, a non-lawyer may represent a claimant before the Cadastral Court.

### 8. Indigenous Peoples' proceedings (NCIP)

Under the Rules on Pleadings, Practice and Procedure before the **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples** (NCIP Administrative Circular No. 1-03), a party may be assisted by a non-lawyer in proceedings before the NCIP or its designated Regional Hearing Officer.

### 9. Mediation under the ADR Act

Under Section 14, RA 9285 (Alternative Dispute Resolution Act of 2004), a party to mediation may designate **a lawyer or any other person** to provide assistance. A waiver of the right to counsel or representation in mediation must be in writing and may be rescinded at any time.

### 10. Certified law students

A law student holding a current Level 1 or Level 2 certification under [[Law Student Practice Rule|Rule 138-A]] may render limited legal services, including — at Level 2 — appearing at trial, under the direct supervision of an accredited supervising lawyer.

## What all of these exceptions have in common — and where they stop

Every exception above rests on either (a) the party's own right to represent themselves, with or without lay assistance, or (b) an express statutory or tribunal-specific authorization that never depended on Bar membership. None of them authorizes a **non-lawyer to hold out generally as someone who represents others for a fee**. A court employee who, outside official duty, drafts pleadings or gives legal advice to litigants for consideration is not exercising any of these exceptions — that is unauthorized practice, full stop. See [[Arienda v. Monilla]].

Self-representation is also conceptually distinct from representing **another** person, even where the "other person" is closely related to the litigant. A public official who appears only for himself, in his own cause, is not thereby engaged in the practice of law for another — but the moment the same person purports to represent someone else's cause, the general rule (Bar membership required) reasserts itself unless a specific exception applies. See [[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]].

## Related cases

- [[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]] — self-representation is not the same as representing another as counsel.
- [[Arienda v. Monilla]] — a non-lawyer court employee's preparation of legal documents for litigants, outside any recognized exception, is not protected appearance.
- [[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva]] — an isolated appearance by a public official/lawyer is analyzed under the habituality-and-compensation test, not the non-lawyer exceptions.
- [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] — supplies the baseline definition of practice of law against which every claimed exception is measured.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "A party may always get help from a friend, in any court, as long as the friend does not charge a fee."

Wrong. The agent-or-friend exception under Section 34, Rule 138 is available only in **first-level courts**. In all other courts, lay assistance is not allowed — a party may still represent themselves, but any assistance must come from a duly authorized member of the Bar.

### Trap 2: "Self-representation by a lawyer-litigant in his own case counts as private practice of law."

Wrong. Representing oneself is the exercise of a party's own right, not practice of law for another — this matters most for public officials who are otherwise barred from private practice. See [[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]].

### Trap 3: "Non-lawyer representation before the NLRC or DARAB is a loophole that also works in regular trial courts."

Wrong. Each exception is tribunal-specific. Authorization to appear before the NLRC, DARAB, NCIP, or a Cadastral Court does not carry over to regular courts.

### Trap 4: "A court employee who is good at drafting pleadings for litigants, off the clock, is just being helpful."

Wrong. Absent a recognized exception, this is unauthorized practice of law, regardless of motive or whether a fee changes hands. See [[Arienda v. Monilla]].

### Trap 5: "An accused may always insist on defending himself without counsel."

Not automatic. The court must be satisfied that the accused can sufficiently protect his own rights before allowing self-representation in a criminal case, under Section 1(c), Rule 138.

## Recall questions

Q: In which courts may a party be assisted by a mere "agent or friend" rather than a lawyer?  
A: Only first-level courts (MTC/MeTC/MCTC), under Section 34, Rule 138.

Q: Does representing yourself in your own case count as private practice of law?  
A: No. It is the exercise of a party's own right, distinct from representing another as counsel.

Q: Name three tribunals where non-lawyer representation is expressly allowed by special rule.  
A: Any three of: NLRC/Labor Arbiters, DARAB, Cadastral Courts, NCIP.

Q: What must a court verify before allowing an accused to defend himself in a criminal case?  
A: That the accused can sufficiently and properly protect his own rights without the assistance of counsel.

Q: Is a non-lawyer's help in mediation proceedings recognized by law?  
A: Yes. RA 9285, Sec. 14 allows a party to designate a lawyer or any other person to assist in mediation.
