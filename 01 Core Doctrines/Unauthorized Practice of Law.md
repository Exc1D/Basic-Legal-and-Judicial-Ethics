# Unauthorized Practice of Law

## Rule

Unauthorized practice of law happens in two distinct shapes, and bar questions like to blur the line between them: **a non-lawyer performing acts exclusive to members of the Bar** — drafting instruments that require legal judgment, giving legal advice, appearing as counsel, or otherwise holding out as authorized to practice — and **a person who was, or could become, a lawyer practicing without current authority** — before taking the oath and signing the Roll, or while suspended, disbarred, or otherwise disqualified. Both shapes threaten the same interest: the public's ability to rely on a class of professionals who are certified competent **and** subject to the Court's disciplinary control. A non-lawyer performing legal work is accountable to no one the way a lawyer is; that is the harm the rule exists to prevent (*Cambaliza v. Cristal-Tenorio*, A.C. No. 6290, 14 July 2004).

## The duty not to assist — old label, current anchor

Generations of case law state this duty as **"Canon 9 of the Code of Professional Responsibility: a lawyer shall not, directly or indirectly, assist in the unauthorized practice of law."** That is still the shorthand the profession uses, and it is how [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|Medado]] (2013), [[Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas]] (2017), and even [[Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Attys. Rivera and Bangoy|Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Rivera and Bangoy]] (2024) frame the violation.

Textually, though, the 1988 Code of Professional Responsibility — Canon 9 included — was repealed outright by the **CPRA's General Provisions, Section 2**, effective 30 May 2023. The CPRA does not reproduce a section labeled "unauthorized practice of law." Its closest express anchor is **Canon II, Section 35 (Non-delegable legal tasks)**, which bars a lawyer from letting a non-lawyer (including a paralegal) accept cases, give legal advice, hold out as a lawyer, appear before a tribunal, negotiate on a client's behalf, sign a legal opinion, or "perform any of the duties that only lawyers may undertake." Read together with the CPRA's definition of practice of law (Canon III, Sec. 1) and its general disciplinary framework (Canon VI), the substance of the old Canon 9 duty continues undiminished — a lawyer's obligation to prevent unauthorized practice is simply folded into the broader duty of fidelity to the profession rather than stated as a stand-alone canon. Treat "Canon 9" as the doctrinal shorthand you will keep seeing in case law, and Canon II, Section 35 as its current textual home.

**Bar trap:** do not cite "Canon 9, CPR" as if it is still the operative rule text — the 1988 Code is repealed. Cite the CPRA provision, but it is fair (and expected) to note that the case law grew up under, and still narrates the doctrine through, Canon 9.

## Shape one: a non-lawyer performing acts exclusive to the Bar

The plainest version of this shape needs no lawyer status claimed, no letterhead, no corporate structure — just a non-lawyer directly standing in for a litigant. **[[Zeta v. Malinao]]** is that plain case: Felicisimo Malinao, a court interpreter, represented litigants in municipal courts and collected fees for it, competing directly with licensed counsel — and to hide the time he spent doing so, he falsified his own daily time records to show he had been at his post when he was actually elsewhere representing private clients. The Supreme Court found him liable for illegal practice of law and grave misconduct and ordered his dismissal from judicial service with a permanent bar against reemployment anywhere in the judiciary, treating the falsified records not as a separate offense but as what made an already serious violation graver still — a sustained, deliberate scheme run from inside the justice system he was supposed to serve.

**[[Arienda v. Monilla]]** shows the same wrong committed without ever appearing in a courtroom at all. A court stenographer, not a lawyer, agreed — off the clock, for a private client, together with her lawyer husband — to prepare and finalize an extrajudicial settlement of an estate for a fee of nearly ₱50,000. Document preparation requiring legal judgment is practice of law under the *Cayetano v. Monsod* functional test regardless of whether the drafter ever sets foot in court, and the Supreme Court found her guilty of simple misconduct on exactly that basis. See [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]] and [[Practice of Law]] for this case's fuller facts.

**[[Cambaliza v. Cristal-Tenorio]]** broadens the picture again, to a case where no one needs to prove any actual appearance or drafting at all. Atty. Ana Luz Cristal-Tenorio allowed her non-lawyer husband, Felicisimo Tenorio, Jr., to be listed as a "partner" on her law office's letterhead and to carry an identification card — one she personally signed as chairperson of the office — reading "Atty. Felicisimo Tenorio, Jr." The Supreme Court suspended her for six months, holding that "holding out" a non-lawyer as a member of the Bar is itself the harm the rule targets: a letterhead or an ID card deceives clients and the public into believing they are dealing with someone accountable to the Court's disciplinary authority, whether or not that person ever personally appeared in a proceeding.

**[[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.]]** takes the same wrong to corporate scale. The Legal Clinic, Inc. — incorporated by Atty. Rogelio P. Nogales, who patterned it after the medical model of specialization — ran newspaper advertisements soliciting clients for "secret" marriages at ₱560, foreign divorces, annulments, immigration and visa remedies, and adoption, with free books on Guam divorce distributed through a Guam-based lawyer. Six bar organizations — the IBP, PBA, PLA, WILOCI, WLAP, and FIDA — were asked for position papers, and all six opposed the clinic's operations. The Supreme Court held that advising on annulment, foreign-divorce recognition under Article 26 of the Family Code, immigration remedies, and estate-tax consequences squarely required legal knowledge and training under the *Cayetano v. Monsod* test, and that a corporation cannot practice law even by employing lawyers to do the work for it — Section 1, Rule 138 reserves the practice to natural persons precisely because the privilege is personal and must remain subject to the Court's disciplinary reach, something a corporate shield defeats. The Court also rejected the clinic's fallback argument that it was merely offering American-style "paralegal" services, since no Philippine judicial rule or legislation had yet established any standards for that occupation. Atty. Nogales was reprimanded for aiding the unauthorized practice and for advertising legal services beyond the narrow exceptions Canon 3 of the old CPR allowed, and the question of the corporation's own franchise was referred to the Solicitor General for quo warranto proceedings — the Court declined to resolve that question itself.

## Shape two: practicing without current authority

The second shape catches a person who has, or once had, a path to lawful practice, but is practicing at a moment when that authority does not yet exist, no longer exists, or has been suspended.

**[[Aguirre v. Rana]]** sits at the very beginning of that timeline. Edwin Rana passed the 2000 Bar Examinations, but the day before the mass oath-taking, a complaint accused him of already appearing as counsel — before he had taken the oath or signed the Roll — for vice-mayoralty candidate George Bunan and mayoralty candidate Emily Estipona-Hao before the Municipal Board of Election Canvassers of Mandaon, Masbate, signing pleadings as "Counsel for... Vice Mayoralty Candidate, GEORGE T. BUNAN." The Supreme Court denied his admission outright. Passing the bar exams does not, by itself, confer any right to practice; the two indispensable acts are the oath and the signing of the Roll, and practicing before either was complete is not a technical foot-fault — it is itself proof of the moral unfitness that the admission process exists to screen for. See [[Signing of the Roll of Attorneys]] for the fuller two-requisites framework this case establishes.

**[[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|In Re: Medado]]** shows the same shape at the opposite end of a career, and answers a question Rana's facts never raised: does the duty not to "assist" unauthorized practice reach a lawyer's own lapse, or only help given to someone else? Michael Medado took the oath in 1980, then misplaced the notice to sign the Roll and mistakenly believed he already had — a belief he carried through over three decades of otherwise ordinary corporate and tax practice, until he rediscovered the misplaced notice while going through old files. The Supreme Court allowed him to finally sign, reasoning that denying the petition outright would function as a disbarment for conduct far short of the Court's most serious sanction, and crediting his own voluntary disclosure and clean record. But it did not excuse him either: applying *ignorantia facti excusat; ignorantia legis neminem excusat*, the Court held that his honest mistake of fact excused him only up to the moment he found the notice — from that point forward, knowing he was not a full-fledged member of the Bar, his continued practice was **willful**, because a mistake of law that follows a discovered mistake of fact is never an excuse. The ruling settles the "assist" question directly: the duty subsumes a lawyer's own unauthorized practice, not only assistance rendered to someone else. Medado was made to sign only after a penalty equivalent to a delayed admission, plus a fine.

**[[Tan, Jr. v. Atty. Gumba]]** shows the shape one more way: authority that existed, then was actively withdrawn. Atty. Haide Gumba, already suspended over an earlier transaction with Tomas Tan, Jr. (a loan secured by a deed of sale over property her own special power of attorney did not actually authorize her to sell), kept filing pleadings and appearing as counsel during the suspension period itself. The Supreme Court treated this not as a continuation of the original case but as a **fresh, separately punishable offense** — willful disobedience of the Court's own order — and stacked an additional six-month suspension on top of the original one. A penalty a lawyer can simply ignore, the Court reasoned, is no penalty at all.

**[[Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas]]** shows how far a suspended lawyer will go to disguise continued practice as something else. Atty. Edgardo Era, two years into a suspension for representing conflicting interests, kept working an illegal-dismissal judgment on behalf of his former clients: he attended the public auction of the judgment debtor's corporate properties, tendered the winning bid, obtained the certificate of sale, negotiated a settlement that climbed from ₱6 million to ₱9 million inside his own law office, and — when talks collapsed — returned with his clients and several men to forcibly open the premises and remove the properties, an episode caught on video. His defense was that he acted only as attorney-in-fact under a Special Power of Attorney, not as a lawyer. The Supreme Court rejected the SPA as a shield: the SPA itself existed only because Era was his clients' counsel, and letting it launder his conduct into something other than legal practice "would cunningly undermine the suspension order" the Court had already imposed. Because this was Era's second infraction, the Court suspended him for three years. His associate, Atty. Diane Bragas, who accompanied him knowing of the suspension, was separately suspended for one month for **assisting** — proof that even a supporting role in a colleague's unauthorized practice carries its own liability.

**[[Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Attys. Rivera and Bangoy|Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Rivera and Bangoy]]** — decided under the CPRA — draws the line between assisting and merely appearing to assist. Atty. Bede Tabalingcos, disbarred in 2012 for bigamy, kept filing motions before the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas under his old firm name. Atty. Cres Dan Bangoy co-signed a Motion for Extension of Time under that firm name two months **after** he admittedly learned of the disbarment — the Supreme Court found him liable for assisting unauthorized practice and suspended him for six months, unmoved by his claim that he had already left the firm, since his own signature postdated his own knowledge. Atty. Socrates Rivera, by contrast, had only signed a Notice of Change of Address as co-counsel of record in the same case — the Court acquitted him of that specific charge, holding that a law firm has a juridical personality distinct from its named partners, and a passive, ministerial filing that merely states a matter of record is not the same as holding a disbarred lawyer out as still authorized to practice. Rivera did not escape discipline altogether, though: his repeated, documented defiance of the Court's own orders to comment and to pay a fine — compounded by a history of three prior administrative cases — drew a separate ₱50,000 fine for gross misconduct and insubordination, a reminder that a lawyer's disciplinary exposure does not end just because the headline charge fails.

## What is *not* unauthorized practice

- **Supervised paralegal work.** A lawyer may employ a paralegal for drafting support, case management, and similar non-advisory tasks; this does not violate the rule as long as the paralegal does not accept cases, give legal advice, act independently, or hold out as a lawyer (CPRA, Canon II, Sec. 34–35).
- **Passive, ministerial association with a firm name.** Being listed as counsel of record for a firm that includes a disbarred partner is not automatically "assisting," if the lawyer did not actively hold the disbarred partner out as authorized to act. See *Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Rivera and Bangoy*, distinguishing Bangoy's active co-signing from Rivera's passive notice.
- **Self-representation.** A party conducting his or her own case is not practicing law "for another" at all — see [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]] and [[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]].

## Fitness to practice reaches beyond acts performed "as counsel"

Two cases round out the picture from a different angle, showing that a lawyer's overall moral fitness — not only conduct performed while formally acting as counsel, and not only the presence or absence of authority to practice — is what the Court ultimately protects. **[[Saladaga v. Astorga]]** involved no client relationship at all: Atty. Arturo Astorga sold Florencio Saladaga a parcel of coconut land under a Deed of Sale with Right to Repurchase, representing that his title was clear, when in truth the certificate had already been cancelled and transferred to his wife's name before the sale — a fact he never disclosed. The Supreme Court suspended him for two years, rejecting the notion that dishonesty in a purely private transaction, done as an individual rather than as counsel, falls outside the Court's reach. **[[Gonzales-Austria v. Abaya]]** reaches the same conclusion from the opposite direction: a government-employed lawyer is not automatically shielded from Bar discipline for misconduct in an official post, if that misconduct is serious enough to reflect on moral fitness. Atty. Ligaya Gonzales-Austria, a clerk of court, was found to have forged a judge's signature on a judicial order in the course of a separate "ghost employee" investigation — conduct the Court treated as a serious breach of legal ethics and public trust, not a mere administrative lapse confined to her government position, and suspended her for one year from the practice of law, separate from whatever administrative penalty attached to her office.

## Penalties

- **Indirect contempt** under Rule 71, Sec. 3(e) of the Rules of Court, for a non-lawyer assuming to be an attorney and acting as such without authority — fine up to ₱30,000, imprisonment up to six months, or both. (Rule 71 is a separate rule from Rule 138 and was not touched by the CPRA's repeal of Rule 138, Secs. 20–37.)
- **Discipline as a lawyer**, under the CPRA's Canon VI offense-and-sanction framework, where the person practicing without authority is or was a member of the Bar — ranging, on the cases above, from a one-month suspension (Bragas, for assisting) to three years (Era, on a second infraction), depending on whether the conduct was active or merely associative, and whether it was a first or repeat offense.
- **Dismissal from judicial service**, for a non-lawyer court employee who practices law for a fee in competition with licensed counsel — see [[Zeta v. Malinao]], where falsifying time records to conceal the practice compounded the offense.
- For a **justice, judge, or court official**, unauthorized practice of law is classified as a **less serious charge** under A.M. No. 21-08-09-SC, carrying suspension without pay for one to three months, or a fine.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Canon 9 assisting-unauthorized-practice is still good rule text."

Not as written. The 1988 CPR is repealed; ground the duty in CPRA Canon II, Sec. 35, while recognizing that the case law still narrates it as a "Canon 9" violation.

### Trap 2: "Assisting unauthorized practice only covers helping someone else — a lawyer's own lapse is a different problem."

Wrong. [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|Medado]] holds the duty subsumes the lawyer's own unauthorized practice.

### Trap 3: "Once a lawyer honestly believes they're authorized, later practice is always excused."

Wrong. A mistake of fact excuses only until the true facts are discovered; from that point on, continued practice is willful, because ignorance of the law that follows is never an excuse.

### Trap 4: "Any lawyer whose name appears alongside a disbarred lawyer's is automatically liable for assisting."

Wrong. Compare active conduct (co-signing a substantive motion under the disbarred lawyer's firm name) against passive, ministerial acts (signing a notice of change of address as counsel of record) — only the former was sanctioned in *Gonzales v. Rivera and Bangoy*.

### Trap 5: "A paralegal who drafts documents for a lawyer is engaged in unauthorized practice."

Wrong, as long as the paralegal works under the lawyer's direction and does not accept cases, advise clients, or hold out as a lawyer.

### Trap 6: "A corporation can practice law as long as every lawyer it employs is properly licensed."

Wrong. [[Ulep v. The Legal Clinic, Inc.]] holds that Section 1, Rule 138 reserves the practice of law to natural persons; a corporation cannot practice law even through licensed lawyer-employees, because the privilege is personal and the corporate form would insulate the practice from the Court's disciplinary reach.

## Recall questions

Q: What are the two distinct shapes unauthorized practice of law can take?  
A: A non-lawyer performing acts exclusive to the Bar, and a lawyer (or bar passer) practicing without current authority — before oath/Roll, or while suspended or disbarred.

Q: Is "Canon 9 of the CPR" still valid rule text to cite?  
A: No — the 1988 CPR is repealed by the CPRA; cite CPRA Canon II, Sec. 35, while noting the case law still calls the duty "Canon 9."

Q: Does the duty not to assist unauthorized practice cover the lawyer's own unauthorized practice?  
A: Yes — *Medado* holds the duty subsumes a lawyer's own lapse, not just assistance to others.

Q: Why was Bangoy liable for assisting unauthorized practice while Rivera was not, in *Gonzales v. Rivera and Bangoy*?  
A: Bangoy actively co-signed a substantive motion under the disbarred lawyer's firm name two months after learning of the disbarment; Rivera only passively signed a notice of change of address as counsel of record.

Q: Does employing a paralegal violate the rule against unauthorized practice?  
A: No, provided the paralegal works under supervision and does not accept cases, advise clients, or hold out as a lawyer.

Q: Why could The Legal Clinic, Inc. not simply call its staff "paralegals" to avoid the unauthorized-practice finding in *Ulep*?  
A: Because Philippine law had no judicial rule or legislation establishing standards for a paralegal occupation; a private entity cannot unilaterally import that model to justify offering legal services to the public.
