# Revised Lawyer's Oath

## Rule

Subscribing to the Lawyer's Oath before the Supreme Court is one of two indispensable acts — alongside signing the Roll of Attorneys — that a successful Bar examinee must complete before practicing law. See [[Signing of the Roll of Attorneys]] for the companion requirement and the leading case on what happens when the two acts are separated in time. Rule 138, Sec. 3 prescribes the form of the oath; Rule 138, Sec. 17 requires it as a condition to receiving the attorney's certificate. Neither step is optional, and neither substitutes for the other — a bar passer who has taken the oath but not signed the Roll, or vice versa, is not yet authorized to practice.

## The original oath

Since long before the CPRA, the Lawyer's Oath committed every admittee to allegiance to the Republic, support for the Constitution, and a set of specific promises still worth knowing because they remain embedded, in substance, in the revised text: to "do no falsehood, nor consent to the doing of any in court," to not "wittingly or willingly promote or sue any groundless, false, or unlawful suit," to "delay no man for money or malice," and to conduct oneself "with all good fidelity as well to the courts as to my clients." These lines are the source of some of the most frequently cited oath violations in disciplinary case law — falsehood before the court, groundless suits, and delay for money or malice remain live bar-exam themes even after the 2023 revision.

## The 2023 revision

Alongside the promulgation of the CPRA (A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC, 11 April 2023), the Supreme Court promulgated a **Revised Lawyer's Oath**, applied to every examinee admitted from the 2023 Bar Examinations onward. The revision was not merely stylistic — reviewers and commentators consistently flag it as the CPRA's most symbolically significant change, because the older oath never used the word "justice" at all. As quoted in full by the Court itself in [[Pante v. Atty. Tebelin]], the revised oath reads:

> I, (name), do solemnly swear (affirm) that I accept the honor, privilege, duty and responsibility of practicing law in the Philippines as an Officer of the Court in the interest of our people. I declare fealty to the Constitution of the Republic of the Philippines. In doing so, I shall work towards promoting the rule of law and a regime of truth, justice, freedom, love, equality, and peace. I shall conscientiously and courageously work for justice, as well as safeguard the rights and meaningful freedoms of all persons, identities, and communities. I shall ensure greater and equitable access to justice. I shall do no falsehood nor shall I pervert the law to unjustly favor nor prejudice anyone. I shall faithfully discharge these duties and responsibilities to the best of my ability, with integrity, and utmost civility. I impose all these upon myself without mental reservation nor purpose of evasion. So help me, God.

The revised oath commits the new lawyer to accepting the practice of law as an "honor, privilege, duty, and responsibility" exercised "as an officer of the court, in the interest of our people," and adds express commitments to the rule of law, truth, justice, freedom, love, equality, and peace; to working "conscientiously and courageously" for justice; to safeguarding the rights and meaningful freedoms of all persons, identities, and communities; and to ensuring **greater and equitable access to justice** — language with no real counterpart in the pre-2023 text. It retains the older oath's core promise to do no falsehood, restated as a duty not to "pervert the law to unjustly favor or prejudice anyone," and closes with a commitment to integrity and "utmost civility."

> **Bar trap:** pre-2023 review materials — and older mnemonic devices built around the classic oath — will not reflect the revised text's justice-and-access-to-justice language. The Supreme Court has also required that the **full oath be read aloud** by each Bar passer during the oath-taking ceremony, not merely assented to with "I do," underscoring that the oath is meant to be a deliberate, individually spoken commitment rather than a ceremonial formality.

## The oath as a perpetual ethical touchstone

A common misconception is that the Lawyer's Oath matters only at admission — a box checked once, on oath-taking day, and rarely relevant again. Case law treats it very differently: disciplinary decisions across almost every kind of misconduct routinely cite a **violation of the Lawyer's Oath** as an independent or aggravating ground, layered on top of whatever specific CPRA canon or CPR rule was also broken. [[Katipunan v. Atty. Carrera]] cites the oath in an ordinary case of neglect and poor client communication — conduct that has nothing to do with admission — because the oath's promise of fidelity and diligence to a client is treated as binding for the whole of a lawyer's career, not only at its outset. [[Santos v. Atty. Paña]] and [[Pante v. Atty. Tebelin]] both frame outright falsification and fiduciary betrayal as direct contradictions of the oath, serious enough on their own to justify disbarment. The lesson for exam purposes: whenever a fact pattern describes a lawyer behaving dishonestly, negligently, or disloyally toward a client or the court, expect the Lawyer's Oath to be cited **in addition to** the specific rule violated, not instead of it.

## Related cases

- [[Katipunan v. Atty. Carrera]] — the oath invoked as an umbrella violation in an ordinary diligence/client-communication case.
- [[Santos v. Atty. Paña]] — falsification of court documents as a direct contradiction of the oath; disbarment.
- [[Pante v. Atty. Tebelin]] — fiduciary betrayal framed expressly as a Revised Lawyer's Oath violation; disbarment.
- [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|In Re: Medado]] — the oath's obligations begin only once the oath is validly taken and the Roll is signed, but bind absolutely from that point.
- [[Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Attys. Rivera and Bangoy]] — a lawyer's "propensity to violate the Lawyer's Oath," shown by a pattern of prior cases, treated as an aggravating circumstance.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "The Lawyer's Oath is a ceremonial formality with no independent legal weight."

Wrong. Courts cite oath violations as an independent or aggravating disciplinary ground in cases spanning falsification, neglect, dishonesty, and fiduciary breach — not only in admission-related disputes.

### Trap 2: "The old oath and the revised oath say essentially the same thing in different words."

Not quite. The revised oath adds substantive commitments that had no counterpart before — most notably the express pledges to "justice" and to "greater and equitable access to justice" — alongside retaining the older oath's core promises against falsehood, groundless suits, and delay for money or malice.

### Trap 3: "Taking the oath is enough to begin practicing law."

Wrong. The oath and the signing of the Roll of Attorneys are two separate, both-indispensable steps. See [[Aguirre v. Rana]] and [[Signing of the Roll of Attorneys]].

## Recall questions

Q: What word did the pre-2023 Lawyer's Oath never use, which the 2023 revision made central?  
A: "Justice" — the revised oath expressly commits the lawyer to working for justice and ensuring greater and equitable access to justice.

Q: Is taking the Lawyer's Oath, by itself, enough to authorize the practice of law?  
A: No. The oath and signing the Roll of Attorneys are both required.

Q: Does the Lawyer's Oath only matter at the moment of admission?  
A: No. Courts routinely cite oath violations as an independent or aggravating ground in later disciplinary cases spanning falsification, neglect, and dishonesty.

Q: What promises from the original oath survive, in substance, in the revised text?  
A: To do no falsehood (now phrased as not perverting the law to unjustly favor or prejudice anyone), to avoid groundless or unlawful suits, and to delay no one for money or malice.
