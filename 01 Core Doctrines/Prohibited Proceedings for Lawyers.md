# Prohibited Proceedings for Lawyers

## Rule

Where [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]] asks when a **non-lawyer** may stand in for a party, this note asks the mirror question: when is a **lawyer** kept out, or cut down to a lesser role, precisely because the proceeding is designed to run without counsel? Three settings recur on the Bar: **Katarungang Pambarangay**, **Small Claims**, and **customary proceedings under the IPRA**. Each is built around the same policy — cheap, fast, party-driven dispute resolution — and each treats the presence of lawyers as something that would defeat that purpose by re-introducing formality, cost, and imbalance between the parties.

## Katarungang Pambarangay

Under **Section 415 of the Local Government Code (RA 7160)**, in all Katarungang Pambarangay proceedings, the parties must appear **in person**, without the assistance of counsel or representative, **except** for minors and incompetents, who may be assisted by a next-of-kin who is **not himself a lawyer**.

- **Exception:** a lawyer may still take part if the lawyer is **appearing as a party** in his or her own right, not as counsel for someone else.
- The prohibition is aimed at preserving the barangay conciliation process's informal, face-to-face, conciliatory character; introducing counsel risks turning conciliation into adversarial litigation in miniature.

## Small Claims Cases

Under **Section 17, A.M. No. 08-8-7-SC** (Rules of Procedure for Small Claims Cases, part of the Rules on Expedited Procedures in the First Level Courts), **no lawyer shall appear on behalf of or represent a party at the hearing**, unless the lawyer is **himself or herself the plaintiff or defendant**.

- Parties are expected to **personally** present their own claims or defenses.
- Under Section 18 (as reflected in OCA Circular No. 69-2022), a **non-lawyer may be allowed to assist** a party if the court, in its discretion, determines that the party cannot properly present the claim or defense and needs assistance — this is deliberately **not** a lawyer's role; it is lay assistance analogous to the agent-or-friend concept in [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]].
- The policy goal is the same as Katarungang Pambarangay: keep small, low-value disputes fast, inexpensive, and free of the cost and formality lawyers bring.

## Customary proceedings under the IPRA

Under **RA 8371** (the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act), the general rule is that lawyers are **prohibited from appearing as counsel for any party** in customary proceedings before indigenous dispute-resolution bodies.

- **Exception:** a lawyer may still take part if appearing in a **non-counsel capacity** — as a member of the **Council of Elders/Leaders**, or by virtue of an obligation arising from membership in the indigenous cultural community or indigenous peoples' group itself.
- The exception tracks the same logic as the Katarungang Pambarangay exception: what is barred is appearing **as counsel**; participating in another, community-based capacity is not barred.

## The common thread

Across all three settings, notice the recurring structure that examiners like to test:

1. A **blanket prohibition** on lawyers appearing **as counsel**.
2. A **narrow carve-out** for the lawyer appearing **as a party**, or in some **other, non-representational capacity** tied to the forum itself (family member, community elder).
3. Room, in some of these fora (small claims), for **lay, non-lawyer assistance** to a party who genuinely cannot cope alone — which is not the same thing as reintroducing counsel.

## Related cases

- [[Maderada v. Judge Mediodea]] — self-representation versus representation of another, the same conceptual line that makes the "appearing as a party" exception work in Katarungang Pambarangay and IPRA proceedings.
- [[Cayetano v. Monsod]] — baseline definition of practice of law, useful for testing whether a lawyer's conduct in one of these fora crosses from permitted self-interest into prohibited representation of another.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "A lawyer can never take part in Katarungang Pambarangay proceedings."

Wrong. A lawyer may appear **as a party** in his or her own case. What is barred is appearing **as counsel** for someone else, or as a representative other than next-of-kin assisting a minor or incompetent.

### Trap 2: "In small claims, a party may hire a lawyer as long as the lawyer stays quiet and just sits beside the client."

Wrong. Section 17 bars the lawyer from appearing on behalf of or representing a party at the hearing at all, unless the lawyer is the plaintiff or defendant. Silent presence as counsel is still "appearing... on behalf of" the party in substance.

### Trap 3: "Small claims never allows any assistance to a party."

Wrong. The court may, in its discretion, allow a **non-lawyer** to assist a party who cannot properly present a claim or defense — assistance is allowed; lawyer-as-counsel is not.

### Trap 4: "IPRA bars lawyers from customary dispute resolution, full stop."

Wrong. A lawyer may still participate as a member of the Council of Elders/Leaders or by virtue of an obligation as a member of the indigenous community — what is barred is appearing **as counsel** for a party.

### Trap 5: "These prohibitions apply to any proceeding involving indigent or low-value claims."

Wrong. Each prohibition is specific to its own statute or rule (LGC Sec. 415; A.M. No. 08-8-7-SC; RA 8371) — it does not generalize to every summary or expedited procedure.

## Recall questions

Q: May a lawyer ever appear in a Katarungang Pambarangay proceeding?  
A: Yes, but only as a party in his own case, or as a non-lawyer next-of-kin assisting a minor or incompetent — never as counsel for another.

Q: Under the Small Claims rule, who is barred from appearing at the hearing?  
A: Lawyers, unless the lawyer is personally the plaintiff or defendant.

Q: Can a small-claims party get help presenting a claim if they are struggling?  
A: Yes — the court may allow a non-lawyer to assist, in its discretion, if the party cannot properly present the claim or defense.

Q: What is the general rule and exception for lawyers in IPRA customary proceedings?  
A: General rule: lawyers cannot appear as counsel. Exception: a lawyer may take part as a Council of Elders/Leaders member or due to an obligation as an IP community member.

Q: What policy do all three prohibitions share?  
A: Keeping the proceeding fast, inexpensive, informal, and free of the imbalance that professional representation can introduce.
