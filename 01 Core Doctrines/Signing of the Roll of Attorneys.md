# Signing of the Roll of Attorneys

## Rule

Passing the Bar Examinations does not make a person a lawyer. Under Rule 138, two further, distinct acts are required:

1. **Section 17 — the Lawyer's Oath.** A successful applicant "shall take and subscribe before the Supreme Court the corresponding oath of office." See [[Revised Lawyer's Oath]].
2. **Section 19 — signing the Roll of Attorneys.** "The clerk of the Supreme Court shall keep a roll of all attorneys admitted to practice, which roll shall be signed by the person admitted when he receives his certificate."

Only after both steps does Section 18 give effect to the clerk's certificate as the successful applicant's "authority to practice." These three sections sit outside the range the CPRA's General Provisions repealed (Rule 138, Secs. 20–37), so they remain the direct, controlling text — unlike some neighboring Rule 138 provisions on attorney discipline that the CPRA has since superseded.

**The oath and the signature are not interchangeable, and neither substitutes for the other.** [[Aguirre v. Rana]] identifies them as the "two essential requisites to become a full-fledged lawyer": taking the oath, and signing the Roll. A person who has done only one is not yet a lawyer, and any act of practice performed in that gap is unauthorized practice — see [[Unauthorized Practice of Law]].

Both steps presuppose that the Court is satisfied the candidate deserves to take them at all — and the oath itself can be withheld even from someone who has already passed the Bar Examinations. **[[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712|In re: Argosino]]** shows the sequence stalling at its very first step. Al Argosino passed the 1993 Bar Examinations, but the Court deferred his oath-taking altogether because of his conviction for reckless imprudence resulting in homicide, arising from the 1991 fraternity hazing death of neophyte Raul Camaligan. Argosino was allowed to take the oath — and only then proceed toward signing the Roll — but only after he produced more than fifteen certifications, including from two senators, five trial court judges, and six members of religious orders, and evidence that a scholarship foundation had been established in the victim's honor through the joint efforts of his family and the eight accused. Even the victim's own father, himself a lawyer, declined to say whether Argosino was now fit to practice and left the question to the Court's discretion. The lesson for this note specifically: "signing the Roll" is the last stop in a sequence, not the first gate, and a candidate can be stopped well before ever reaching it if the oath itself is withheld pending proof of reformed character.

## Why the signature is not a formality

It is tempting to treat the Roll as a clerical record-keeping step, and that temptation is exactly what [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|In Re: Medado]] (B.M. No. 2540, 24 Sept. 2013) is built to correct. Medado took the oath in 1980, mistakenly believed that a document he signed at the venue entrance was the Roll itself, and practiced law competently for more than three decades before discovering the mistake. Even so, the moment he found the misplaced notice and realized he had never actually signed, his authority to keep practicing evaporated — not because his decades of work were deficient, but because **signing the Roll, not merely taking the oath or behaving like a lawyer, is the act that completes admission.**

## Two case outcomes worth contrasting

| | [[Aguirre v. Rana]] (2003) | [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|In Re: Medado]] (2013) |
|---|---|---|
| Timing of the unauthorized practice | **Before** the oath and before the Roll — Rana signed pleadings as "counsel" the week before his own scheduled oath-taking | **After** the oath, continuing for decades due to a genuine mix-up about whether the Roll had been signed |
| State of mind | Knew he was not yet a lawyer; practiced anyway, apparently to accommodate an actual client's request | Honest mistake of fact at first; became willful the moment he discovered the unsigned notice |
| Outcome | **Admission denied outright** — unauthorized practice before admission is itself proof of the moral unfitness the admission process screens for | **Allowed to sign**, but only after a penalty functioning like a delayed admission, plus a fine — outright denial after 30 years of otherwise good practice would have been tantamount to disbarment, a penalty reserved for the most serious transgressions |

The contrast is the exam-ready takeaway: the *same* underlying problem — practicing without having signed the Roll — can lead to permanent denial or to a lenient, penalized cure, depending on **when** the unauthorized practice happened relative to admission, **why** it happened, and how the candidate responded once the truth was known. Voluntary disclosure, decades of otherwise-clean practice, and the absence of any prior disciplinary action all cut in Medado's favor; deliberate practice on the eve of one's own admission, for an actual client's benefit, cut against Rana.

## Mistake of fact versus mistake of law

Medado's digest is the vault's cleanest illustration of a recurring bar-exam Latin maxim pairing: ***ignorantia facti excusat; ignorantia legis neminem excusat*** — a mistake of fact may excuse, but a mistake of law excuses no one. Medado's belief that he had already signed was a mistake of fact, and it excused his conduct only for as long as it lasted. Once he found the notice, the mistake of fact ended; continuing to practice from that point was, in the SC's words, a **willful** engagement in unauthorized practice, because everyone is conclusively presumed to know that practicing law requires being on the Roll.

## Reinstatement after disbarment: a related but distinct track

Signing the Roll for the first time is different from being restored to it after disbarment. Under CPRA, Canon VI, Secs. 47–51, a disbarred lawyer may petition for **judicial clemency** only after five years from the disbarment decision, must show full compliance with prior disciplinary orders, genuine recognition of wrongdoing, reconciliation efforts (or a public apology where there is no private offended party), and continuing good moral character and competence — decided on **clear and convincing evidence**. See [[Edillon, A.C. No. 1928, 84 SCRA 554 (August 3, 1978)]] for the foundational "preservative, not vindictive" discipline principle this framework still rests on, even though Edillon predates the CPRA's codified clemency procedure.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Taking the oath is the moment a bar passer becomes a lawyer."

Wrong. The oath and the Roll signature are both required; a bar passer who has only taken the oath is not yet authorized to practice.

### Trap 2: "Medado's 30 years of competent, uneventful practice should have cured the defect on its own."

Wrong. Competent practice is not a substitute for signing the Roll — the SC still required a formal (if lenient) penalty before allowing him to sign, precisely because the underlying unauthorized practice was real once he knew of the omission.

### Trap 3: "Rana and Medado should have received the same outcome since both practiced without having signed the Roll."

Wrong. Timing and conduct matter: Rana practiced deliberately before his own admission was even complete; Medado's lapse began as an honest mistake and he voluntarily came forward decades later. Outright denial fits one fact pattern; a penalized cure fits the other.

### Trap 4: "Reinstatement after disbarment just requires waiting out a suspension period."

Wrong. It requires a verified petition for judicial clemency filed only after five years, supported by clear and convincing evidence of compliance, reformation, and reconciliation — not a fixed suspension term that lapses automatically.

### Trap 5: "Passing the Bar guarantees the oath will be administered as scheduled."

Wrong. [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712|In re: Argosino]] shows the Court can defer the oath itself, before the candidate ever reaches the Roll, pending proof that a disqualifying moral-character problem has been overcome.

## Recall questions

Q: What two acts, besides passing the Bar Examinations, are required before a person may practice law?  
A: Taking the Lawyer's Oath (Sec. 17, Rule 138) and signing the Roll of Attorneys (Sec. 19, Rule 138).

Q: In *Medado*, why did his initial mistake stop excusing his conduct?  
A: Because it was only a mistake of fact — once he discovered the misplaced notice and knew he had not signed the Roll, continuing to practice became a willful, unexcused violation.

Q: Why was Rana denied admission outright while Medado was allowed to belatedly sign, despite both having practiced without a signed Roll?  
A: Rana practiced deliberately before his own admission was complete, for an actual client's benefit; Medado's lapse began as a genuine mistake, and he voluntarily disclosed it decades later, showing good faith and reformation.

Q: How soon after disbarment may a lawyer petition for judicial clemency to be reinstated?  
A: Not less than five years from receipt of the disbarment order, decision, or resolution.

Q: Can the Supreme Court stop a successful bar examinee before the oath-and-Roll sequence even starts?  
A: Yes — [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712|In re: Argosino]] shows the Court deferring the oath itself pending proof that a disqualifying moral-character problem (there, a hazing-related homicide conviction) had been overcome.
