# Merit-Based Practice

## Rule

> "A lawyer shall rely solely on the merits of a cause and not exert, or give the appearance of, any influence on, nor undermine the authority of, the court, tribunal or other government agency, or its proceedings." — CPRA, Canon I, Sec. 2

This is the CPRA's direct successor to the 1988 CPR's **Canon 13** ("A lawyer shall rely upon the merits of his cause and refrain from any impropriety which tends to influence, or gives the appearance of influencing the court") and **Rule 13.01** (no extending hospitality to, or cultivating familiarity with, judges). Every case in this vault's Canon I set that discusses merit-based practice was in fact decided under Canon 13, not the CPRA — the CPRA simply codifies and relocates the same duty.

Two distinct wrongs are packed into the one section: **actual influence** — bribery, offers of pecuniary benefit, or genuine attempts to sway a decision-maker — and **the mere appearance of influence** — namedropping connections, fraternizing with judges, or claiming access a lawyer may not even actually have. The rule punishes the appearance independently of whether real influence existed, because the harm is the erosion of public confidence that decisions are won on the law and the facts, not on who a lawyer knows. Three cases, of escalating severity, show exactly where the line sits and how far past it a lawyer can go before the penalty stops being a suspension at all.

At the mildest end, **[[Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.]]** shows that the duty is not even limited to courts. Atty. Felisberto Verano, Jr. represented Richard Brodett and Joseph Tecson — the so-called "Alabang Boys," accused by the PDEA of illegal drug sale and use in a case that drew heavy media and congressional scrutiny. After a Joint Inquest Resolution dropped the charges, Verano wanted his clients released faster than the process allowed, so he personally went to see then-Justice Secretary Raul Gonzales, spent roughly two hours discussing the case with him, and handed over a release order he had drafted himself on Department of Justice letterhead — pre-written for the Secretary's signature, to make it "easy" and "convenient" for Gonzales to sign if he agreed. The Secretary never signed it, and Verano argued the draft was therefore "a mere scrap of paper with no effect at all." The Supreme Court rejected that defense and suspended him for six months, holding that Canon 13's ban on influence-peddling is not textually confined to judicial proceedings — it reaches **"all other venues in the justice sector,"** including a prosecutorial and executive authority like the DOJ Secretary, "where respect for the rule of law is at all times demanded from a member of the bar." No money changed hands and no threat was made; the act of personally lobbying an official outside the normal channel, with a document designed to make compliance effortless, was enough by itself.

One step further, **[[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]]** adds an actual bribery offer and direct threats to court personnel on top of the same namedropping-and-lobbying pattern. Atty. Manuel Camacho, counsel in a ₱16 million collection case pending before Judge Ariel Dumlao, Jr. of RTC Dagupan City, spent the litigation dropping the names of then-Chief Justice Maria Lourdes Sereno and Associate Justice Marvic Leonen as his close friends and touting his UP Law connections. After winning summary judgment, he called the judge directly, asked him to deny the opposing side's appeal and issue a writ of execution, and **offered to share a cut of his own attorney's fees with the judge** in exchange — adding that the case was being "closely monitored" by the named Justices. When the judge did not bite, Camacho escalated: he barged into the judge's chambers and pressured the sheriff to sign a garnishment order Camacho had drafted himself, seeking release of an inflated ₱18.69 **billion** against an actual ₱16 million judgment, and when the sheriff correctly refused, warned him in Filipino that he would have him removed and that "the two Justices" already knew about it. The Supreme Court found this influence peddling and attempted bribery and imposed a two-year suspension — though the penalty could no longer actually run, because Camacho had already been disbarred in an unrelated, earlier case (*Sison, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho*) for mishandling a different client's funds. Philippine practice recognizes no double or multiple disbarment, so the suspension was imposed solely to be entered in his personal file with the Office of the Bar Confidant, relevant only if he ever petitions to have the original disbarment lifted.

At the far end, **[[Vantage Lighting Philippines v. Atty. Diño, Jr.]]** shows what happens when a lawyer stops implying access and starts explicitly selling it. Atty. Jose Diño, Jr., retained by Vantage Lighting Philippines to sue two companies, told Vantage's finance officer that the company needed to pay **₱150,000 "to the judge"** to secure a temporary restraining order, and that he would personally advance ₱20,000 of it if Vantage could not raise the cash by 2:00 p.m. that day. Vantage paid. The TRO never materialized; Diño's story shifted from "the deal with the judge is no longer valid" to a "fixer" who could still make it happen, and when Vantage asked for its money back, he refused, threatened that the company would "be sorry," and then withdrew as counsel and filed **five separate cases** against Vantage and its officers within two months — estafa, a fee-collection suit, grave oral defamation, libel, and falsification, the last one dragging in Vantage's new lawyers on an unproven fabrication accusation. The Supreme Court found even the IBP's recommended one-year suspension too light and imposed **disbarment**, writing the sharpest line in this vault's Canon I case set: a lawyer "should not espouse a belief that the judicial system can be bought, much less contribute to the perpetuation of such belief," and doing so is "inexcusable, shameful and unlawful" misconduct in itself, proof of actual influence aside. The five retaliatory suits were an independent aggravator — proper recourse for an unpaid fee is a retaining lien under Rule 16.03 or a good-faith collection action, not a harassment campaign.

Read together, the three cases form a penalty spectrum worth holding in mind for calibration questions: **lobbying an agency outside the courts, with no money and no threats (six months) → namedropping plus a direct bribery offer and threats to court personnel, already mooted by a prior disbarment (two years, nominal) → affirmatively claiming the judiciary itself is for sale, compounded by retaliatory harassment of a former client (disbarment)**. What separates the three is not whether influence-peddling occurred — all three lawyers engaged in it — but how far each went: implying access, versus offering money and threatening staff, versus explicitly pricing a judge's decision and then punishing the client who balked.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Influence peddling requires proof that the judge or official actually was influenced."

Wrong. Sec. 2 (and its ancestor, Canon 13) punishes **the appearance of influence** independently of actual effect. Camacho's namedropping and Verano's DOJ visit were sanctioned regardless of whether the judge or Secretary was actually swayed.

### Trap 2: "Merit-based practice only concerns conduct before courts."

Wrong. *Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.* squarely extends the duty to "all other venues in the justice sector" — prosecutorial and executive agencies included.

### Trap 3: "A lawyer who has already been disbarred in another case cannot be further disciplined, so a second complaint against them is pointless."

Not quite. Philippine jurisdiction has no double or multiple disbarment, but the Court will still adjudicate the second case on the merits and impose a nominal penalty — as in *Camacho* — for the record, relevant if the lawyer ever petitions to lift the original disbarment.

### Trap 4: "Filing cases to collect unpaid attorney's fees from a difficult former client is always proper self-help."

Wrong, where the suits are filed as retaliation rather than through the proper fee-collection mechanism (a lien under Rule 16.03, or a dedicated collection action pursued in good faith). *Vantage Lighting* treated five suits filed within two months of a fee dispute as harassment aggravating the underlying merit-based-practice violation.

## Recall questions

Q: What two distinct wrongs does CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2 cover?  
A: Actual influence over a court, tribunal, or agency, and the mere *appearance* of influence — both are independently sanctionable.

Q: Is merit-based practice limited to conduct before courts?  
A: No — *Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.* extends it to any venue in the justice sector, including executive agencies like the DOJ.

Q: Why was Atty. Camacho's two-year suspension in the *Dumlao* case only nominal?  
A: He had already been disbarred in a separate, earlier case (*Sison, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho*), and Philippine jurisdiction recognizes no double disbarment — the penalty was imposed only for the OBC record.

Q: Why was disbarment, rather than suspension, imposed in *Vantage Lighting v. Atty. Diño, Jr.*?  
A: Because counsel did not merely imply influence but affirmatively claimed a judge could be bought, and then compounded it with five retaliatory suits against his former client.
