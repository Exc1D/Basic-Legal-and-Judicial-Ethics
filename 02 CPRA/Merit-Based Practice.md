# Merit-Based Practice

## Rule

> "A lawyer shall rely solely on the merits of a cause and not exert, or give the appearance of, any influence on, nor undermine the authority of, the court, tribunal or other government agency, or its proceedings." — CPRA, Canon I, Sec. 2

This is the CPRA's direct successor to the 1988 CPR's **Canon 13** ("A lawyer shall rely upon the merits of his cause and refrain from any impropriety which tends to influence, or gives the appearance of influencing the court") and **Rule 13.01** (no extending hospitality to, or cultivating familiarity with, judges). Every case in this vault's Canon I set that discusses merit-based practice was in fact decided under Canon 13, not the CPRA — the CPRA simply codifies and relocates the same duty.

Two distinct wrongs are packed into the one section, and the case law shows both:

1. **Actual influence** — bribery, offers of pecuniary benefit, or genuine attempts to sway a decision-maker.
2. **The mere appearance of influence** — namedropping connections, fraternizing with judges, or claiming access that a lawyer may not even actually have. The rule punishes the appearance independently of whether real influence existed, because the harm is the erosion of public confidence that decisions are won on the law and the facts, not on who a lawyer knows.

The duty is not limited to courts. It reaches **"all other venues in the justice sector"** — prosecutorial offices, executive agencies, and any other body where "respect for the rule of law is at all times demanded from a member of the bar" (*Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.*).

## Illustrations already in this vault

- **[[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]]** (A.C. No. 10498, 4 Sept. 2018) — counsel namedropped Supreme Court Justices as close friends, offered to share his attorney's fees with the trial judge for a favorable ruling, and threatened a judge and a sheriff who would not sign a self-prepared, inflated garnishment order. Suspended two years — though the suspension could no longer actually run, since respondent had already been disbarred in an unrelated case, *Sison, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho*. The penalty was imposed solely to be recorded in his personal file with the Office of the Bar Confidant.
- **[[Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.]]** (Adm. Case No. 8108, 15 July 2014) — counsel personally lobbied the DOJ Secretary and drafted an unauthorized release order on DOJ letterhead to expedite his clients' release from a drug case. Suspended six months; the Court held merit-based practice reaches beyond the regular courts into any justice-sector venue.
- **[[Vantage Lighting Philippines v. Atty. Diño, Jr.]]** (A.C. No. 7389, 2 July 2019) — counsel told his client it had to pay ₱150,000.00 "to the judge" for a TRO, then filed five retaliatory suits against the client when the relationship soured. **Disbarred** — the gravest penalty of the three, because counsel did not merely imply influence but affirmatively claimed the judiciary itself was for sale, compounded by a pattern of harassment against a former client.

Read together, these three cases form a penalty spectrum useful for calibration questions: **appearance/lobbying an agency (6 months) → namedropping plus a bribery offer, already mooted by a prior disbarment (2 years, nominal) → affirmatively claiming the court is purchasable, plus retaliation (disbarment)**. The differentiator across all three is not the existence of influence-peddling — all three lawyers engaged in it — but its severity and whether it was compounded by a direct bribery claim, threats to court personnel, or harassment of a former client.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Influence peddling requires proof that the judge or official actually was influenced."

Wrong. Sec. 2 (and its ancestor, Canon 13) punishes **the appearance of influence** independently of actual effect. *Camacho*'s namedropping and *Verano*'s DOJ visit were sanctioned regardless of whether the judge or Secretary was actually swayed.

### Trap 2: "Merit-based practice only concerns conduct before courts."

Wrong. *Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.* squarely extends the duty to "all other venues in the justice sector" — prosecutorial and executive agencies included.

### Trap 3: "A lawyer who has already been disbarred in another case cannot be further disciplined, so a second complaint against them is pointless."

Not quite. Philippine jurisdiction has no double or multiple disbarment, but the Court will still adjudicate the second case on the merits and impose a nominal penalty — as in *Camacho* — for the record, relevant if the lawyer ever petitions to lift the original disbarment.

### Trap 4: "Filing cases to collect unpaid attorney's fees from a difficult former client is always proper self-help."

Wrong, where the suits are filed as retaliation rather than through the proper fee-collection mechanism (a lien under Rule 16.03, or a dedicated collection action pursued in good faith). *Vantage Lighting* treated five suits filed within two months of a fee dispute as harassment aggravating the underlying merit-based-practice violation.

## Recall questions

Q: What two distinct wrongs does CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2 cover?  
A: Actual influence over a court, tribunal, or agency, and the mere *appearance* of influence — both are independently sanctionable.

Q: Is merit-based practice limited to conduct before courts?  
A: No — *Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.* extends it to any venue in the justice sector, including executive agencies like the DOJ.

Q: Why was Atty. Camacho's two-year suspension in the *Dumlao* case only nominal?  
A: He had already been disbarred in a separate, earlier case (*Sison, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho*), and Philippine jurisdiction recognizes no double disbarment — the penalty was imposed only for the OBC record.

Q: Why was disbarment, rather than suspension, imposed in *Vantage Lighting v. Atty. Diño, Jr.*?  
A: Because counsel did not merely imply influence but affirmatively claimed a judge could be bought, and then compounded it with five retaliatory suits against his former client.
