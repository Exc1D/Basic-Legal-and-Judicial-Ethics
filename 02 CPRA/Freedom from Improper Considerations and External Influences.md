# Freedom from Improper Considerations and External Influences

## Rule

> "A lawyer shall not, in advocating a client's cause, be influenced by dishonest or immoral considerations, external influences, or pressure." — CPRA, Canon I, Sec. 3

**No case is assigned to this section in the syllabus**, and none of this vault's Canon I case digests turns on it. This note is built from the rule text itself and its standard commentary rather than case law — treat it as a rule to know cold on its wording, not one to expect a landmark case for.

### The direction of the duty — contrast with Sec. 2

Sec. 3 is easy to blur with Sec. 2 ([[Merit-Based Practice]]) because both concern "influence," but they run in opposite directions:

- **Sec. 2** governs the lawyer's conduct **toward the tribunal** — a lawyer must not exert, or appear to exert, influence *on* the court, tribunal, or agency deciding the matter.
- **Sec. 3** governs what may influence **the lawyer's own advocacy** — a lawyer must not let dishonest or immoral considerations, external influences, or pressure shape how they advocate the client's cause in the first place.

Put simply: Sec. 2 is about corrupting the decision-maker; Sec. 3 is about the lawyer *themselves* being corrupted, whether by a bribe from a third party, a threat, or an improper personal inducement, before the case ever reaches the tribunal.

### What counts as a "dishonest" consideration

An inducement that is untruthful or untrustworthy — the standard illustration is a lawyer agreeing to advocate a cause in exchange for a share for the proceeds of a criminal activity (for example, taking a cut of stolen or laundered funds as a condition for representation).

### What counts as an "immoral" consideration

An inducement contrary to prevailing morals — for example, taking on a matter in exchange for something immoral such as sexual favors, or agreeing to represent a cause purely as an act of vengeance or retribution against another person, rather than because the cause has merit.

### "External influences" and "pressure"

This third category is broader and catches non-monetary, non-sexual forms of improper leverage — threats, intimidation, or coercion (by a client, a third party, or even a colleague) that push a lawyer to advocate a cause, or to advocate it in a particular way, for reasons unrelated to the client's actual legal interest or the merits of the case.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Sec. 2 and Sec. 3 both punish the same conduct, so any influence-peddling case can be cited for either."

Wrong. Sec. 2 is about the lawyer influencing the tribunal; Sec. 3 is about something else influencing the lawyer. A case like [[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]] is squarely Sec. 2 (he tried to influence the judge) — it is not a Sec. 3 case, because nothing shows *Camacho himself* was corrupted by a third party into taking or handling the case a certain way.

### Trap 2: "Immoral considerations" only means sexual exchanges.

Incomplete. The standard gloss includes sexual favors as one example, but also covers taking a case purely for vengeance or retribution against a third party, unmoored from the client's actual legal interest.

### Trap 3: "External influences and pressure require a threat of physical harm."

Wrong. The category is broader than physical coercion — it reaches any improper leverage (professional, financial, social, or reputational) that pushes a lawyer's advocacy off the merits.

## Recall questions

Q: How does CPRA Canon I, Sec. 3 differ in direction from Sec. 2?  
A: Sec. 2 bars the lawyer from influencing the tribunal; Sec. 3 bars outside considerations, influences, or pressure from improperly shaping the lawyer's own advocacy.

Q: Give an example of a "dishonest consideration" under Sec. 3.  
A: Agreeing to advocate a cause in exchange for a share of the proceeds of a criminal activity.

Q: Give an example of an "immoral consideration" under Sec. 3.  
A: Taking on a matter in exchange for sexual favors, or purely to exact vengeance or retribution against another person.

Q: Is there a landmark Supreme Court case specifically decided under Sec. 3?  
A: None is assigned in this vault or the syllabus — the section is tested on its text and standard commentary rather than a named case.
