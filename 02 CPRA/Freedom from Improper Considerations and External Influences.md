# Freedom from Improper Considerations and External Influences

## Rule

> "A lawyer shall not, in advocating a client's cause, be influenced by dishonest or immoral considerations, external influences, or pressure." — CPRA, Canon I, Sec. 3

### The direction of the duty — contrast with Sec. 2

Sec. 3 is easy to blur with Sec. 2 ([[Merit-Based Practice]]) because both concern "influence," but they run in opposite directions:

- **Sec. 2** governs the lawyer's conduct **toward the tribunal** — a lawyer must not exert, or appear to exert, influence *on* the court, tribunal, or agency deciding the matter.
- **Sec. 3** governs what may influence **the lawyer's own advocacy** — a lawyer must not let dishonest or immoral considerations, external influences, or pressure shape how they advocate the client's cause in the first place.

Put simply: Sec. 2 is about corrupting the decision-maker; Sec. 3 is about the lawyer *themselves* being corrupted, whether by a bribe from a third party, a threat, or an improper personal inducement, before the case ever reaches the tribunal.

### What counts as a "dishonest" consideration

An inducement that is untruthful or untrustworthy — the standard illustration is a lawyer agreeing to advocate a cause in exchange for a share of the proceeds of a criminal activity. Picture a lawyer approached by someone who wants to contest a relative's will, who offers the lawyer a cut of the estate's undeclared, untaxed cash on the side if the will is successfully broken — the lawyer's fee is no longer tied to a lawful, disclosed engagement but to a share of something the client is trying to conceal from the tax authorities. Accepting that arrangement is a Sec. 3 violation the moment it is agreed to, regardless of how the underlying case turns out.

### What counts as an "immoral" consideration

An inducement contrary to prevailing morals — for example, taking on a matter in exchange for something immoral such as sexual favors, or agreeing to represent a cause purely as an act of vengeance or retribution against another person, rather than because the cause has merit. Picture a lawyer who agrees to file a harassment suit against a former business partner not because the client has any real cause of action, but because the client is a close friend seeking to make an ex-partner's life difficult over an unrelated personal falling-out, and the lawyer goes along with it as a personal favor. The suit may even be dressed up to look colorable on paper — the violation is in the lawyer's real motive for taking it up.

### "External influences" and "pressure"

This third category is broader and catches non-monetary, non-sexual forms of improper leverage — threats, intimidation, or coercion (by a client, a third party, or even a colleague) that push a lawyer to advocate a cause, or to advocate it in a particular way, for reasons unrelated to the client's actual legal interest or the merits of the case. Picture a lawyer who is told by a senior partner, on whom the lawyer's continued employment depends, to take a hardline, scorched-earth negotiating posture on a case where a more measured approach would actually serve the client better — if the lawyer follows that instruction purely to protect standing within the firm rather than because it serves the client's cause, the advocacy has been shaped by exactly the kind of internal pressure Sec. 3 is written to reach, even though no outsider or opposing party is involved at all.

## Why this note runs light on case law

**No case is assigned to this section in the syllabus, and none of this vault's Canon I case digests turns on it.** That is worth stating plainly rather than manufacturing a citation to fill the gap: Sec. 3 punishes what happens to the *lawyer's own motives* before a case ever reaches a tribunal, which rarely produces the kind of paper trail — pleadings, court transcripts, a judge's own account — that makes for a reported disciplinary decision. Contrast [[Merit-Based Practice]], where the misconduct plays out visibly in front of a judge or official and is easy to document; a corrupted motive for taking a case in the first place is comparatively invisible unless the lawyer is careless enough to admit it, as in the dishonest-consideration hypothetical above. If a genuinely on-point case surfaces in future research, it belongs here. Until then, know this section cold on its text and be ready to apply it to a fact pattern the exam invents — that is precisely how this section tends to be tested.

## Bar traps

### Trap 1: "Sec. 2 and Sec. 3 both punish the same conduct, so any influence-peddling case can be cited for either."

Wrong. Sec. 2 is about the lawyer influencing the tribunal; Sec. 3 is about something else influencing the lawyer. A case like [[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]] is squarely Sec. 2 (he tried to influence the judge) — it is not a Sec. 3 case, because nothing shows *Camacho himself* was corrupted by a third party into taking or handling the case a certain way.

### Trap 2: "Immoral considerations" only means sexual exchanges.

Incomplete. The standard gloss includes sexual favors as one example, but also covers taking a case purely for vengeance or retribution against a third party, unmoored from the client's actual legal interest.

### Trap 3: "External influences and pressure require a threat of physical harm."

Wrong. The category is broader than physical coercion — it reaches any improper leverage (professional, financial, social, or reputational) that pushes a lawyer's advocacy off the merits.

## Recall questions

Q: How does CPRA Canon I, Sec. 3 differ in direction from Sec. 2?  
A: Sec. 2 bars the lawyer from influencing the tribunal; Sec. 3 bars outside considerations, influences, or pressure from improperly shaping the lawyer's own advocacy.

Q: Give an example of a "dishonest consideration" under Sec. 3.  
A: Agreeing to advocate a cause in exchange for a share of the proceeds of a criminal activity.

Q: Give an example of an "immoral consideration" under Sec. 3.  
A: Taking on a matter in exchange for sexual favors, or purely to exact vengeance or retribution against another person.

Q: Is there a landmark Supreme Court case specifically decided under Sec. 3?  
A: None is assigned in this vault or the syllabus — the section is tested on its text and standard commentary rather than a named case.
