
## Gist

- In Philippine law it is settled doctrine that the practice of law is **not** a natural, vested, or constitutional right but a **privilege** granted and continually supervised by the Supreme Court under Article VIII, Section 5(5) of the 1987 Constitution — a privilege "burdened with conditions" and "imbued with public interest."
- The doctrine is built on a line of Supreme Court rulings — _In re Cunanan_ (1954), _In re Almacen_ (1970), _In re Edillon_ (1978), _[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]_ (1991), _In re Argosino_ (1995/1997), _[[People of the Philippines v. Hon. Bonifacio Sanz Maceda and Avelino T. Javellana|People v. Maceda]]_ (2000), and _Aguirre v. Rana_ (2003) — and means the privilege is conditioned on continuing good moral character and compliance with the Rules of Court and the CPRA, and may be withdrawn through suspension or disbarment.
- The 2023 Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (CPRA, A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC) carries the doctrine forward: it defines the practice of law, frames the lawyer as a "guardian of the rule of law" and "officer of the court," and ties continued membership to conditions (good standing, IBP dues, MCLE) enforced by the Court's plenary disciplinary power.

## Key Findings

1. The foundational proposition is that "the practice of law is not a natural, absolute or constitutional right to be granted to everyone who demands it. Rather, it is a high personal privilege limited to citizens of good moral character, with special educational qualifications, duly ascertained and certified" (_In re Argosino_).
2. The legal basis is the Supreme Court's exclusive constitutional power to regulate admission to and the practice of law (Art. VIII, Sec. 5(5), 1987 Constitution), implemented through Rule 138 of the Rules of Court.
3. The privilege is conditional and revocable: continued good moral character is a continuing requirement, and breach of the conditions (ethics, lawful orders, dues, MCLE) can lead to suspension or disbarment.
4. The CPRA (2023) modernizes but does not displace the doctrine; the "privilege/public interest/burdened with conditions" language is jurisprudential, while the CPRA supplies the operative definitions and disciplinary machinery.
5. A textbook treatment titled "A Mere Privilege, not a Right" would cover the doctrine, its constitutional/statutory basis, the leading cases, the conditional/continuing nature of the privilege, and the consequences of breach.

## Details

### 1. The doctrine and its meaning

Philippine legal ethics begins from the premise that [no person has an inherent or vested right to practice law](https://lawphil.net/judjuris/juri1973/jan1973/ibp_1973.html). The practice of law is a **privilege** conferred by the State, acting through the Supreme Court, on individuals who satisfy and continue to satisfy exacting standards of competence and character. The classic formulation appears in _In re Argosino_ (B.M. No. 712), which describes the practice of law as "a high personal privilege limited to citizens of good moral character, with special educational qualifications, duly ascertained and certified," and adds that it is "a privilege burdened with conditions" that is "so delicately affected with public interest that it is both a power and a duty of the State (through this Court) to control and regulate it in order to protect and promote the public welfare."

Because the privilege is not a property or vested right, it must "bow to the inherent regulatory power of the Court," and "no lawyer has a vested right" to continue practicing if he fails to maintain the required standards. The practical consequences are that the privilege can be conditioned, regulated, and withdrawn for cause.

### 2. The leading Supreme Court cases

**In re Cunanan, 94 Phil. 534 (March 18, 1954).** The Court struck down Republic Act No. 972 ("Bar Flunkers' Act of 1953"), which retroactively lowered passing averages (70% for 1946–1951, scaling to 74% by 1955) to benefit 1,094 unsuccessful candidates from 1946–1953, of which only 604 had filed petitions. The Court held that admission, suspension, disbarment, and reinstatement of attorneys is a judicial function vested exclusively in the Supreme Court, and that Congress had encroached on that power. As the resolution put it, "the ultimate power to grant license for the practice of law belongs exclusively to this Court, and the law passed by Congress on the matter is of permissive character... merely to fix the minimum conditions for the license." Cunanan is the bedrock authority that the power to grant the license to practice law belongs exclusively to the judiciary, and that membership in the bar is a privilege burdened with conditions imbued with public interest.

**In re [[Almacen, 31 SCRA 562 (G.R. No. L-27654, February 18, 1970)]].** Atty. Almacen, frustrated by an adverse ruling, filed a "Petition to Surrender Lawyer's Certificate of Title" with intemperate attacks on the Court. The Court suspended him, holding that disbarment/suspension proceedings are _sui generis_ — neither purely civil nor criminal — and are designed not to punish but to protect the courts and the public and to determine the lawyer's continuing fitness to enjoy the privilege.

**In re [[Edillon, A.C. No. 1928, 84 SCRA 554 (August 3, 1978)]].** Atty. Marcial Edillon was disbarred for stubborn refusal to pay IBP membership dues. The Court held that compulsory IBP membership and dues are a valid exercise of the State's police power and the Court's regulatory authority, and famously reasoned that "the practice of law is not a property right but a mere privilege" that "must bow to the inherent regulatory power of the Court to exact compliance with the lawyer's public responsibilities." (Edillon was later reinstated after showing contrition.)

**[[Cayetano v. Monsod]], 201 SCRA 210 (G.R. No. 100113, September 3, 1991).** While primarily about whether Christian Monsod met the "engaged in the practice of law for at least ten years" qualification for COMELEC Chairman, this case supplies the canonical broad definition: "Practice of law means any activity, in or out of court, which requires the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training and experience." It is cited for the Court's authority to define and regulate what constitutes the practice of law.

**[[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712]] (July 13, 1995; resolution March 19, 1997).** Al Argosino passed the bar but was initially refused the lawyer's oath because of a homicide conviction arising from fraternity hazing. The Court reiterated that good moral character is a condition precedent and a continuing requirement, and is "of greater importance so far as the general public and the proper administration of justice are concerned, than the possession of legal learning."

**[[People of the Philippines v. Hon. Bonifacio Sanz Maceda and Avelino T. Javellana|People v. Maceda]], G.R. Nos. 89591-96 (January 24, 2000).** This resolution held that a detention prisoner — including a lawyer, here Atty. Avelino Javellana — cannot practice his profession as a necessary consequence of detention, illustrating that the privilege yields to lawful restraints and the Court's supervisory authority.

**[[Aguirre v. Rana]], B.M. No. 1036 (June 10, 2003).** The Court held that one who passed the bar but engaged in the practice of law before taking the oath and signing the Roll of Attorneys could be denied admission. It stated: "The right to practice law is not a natural or constitutional right but is a privilege... A bar candidate does not acquire the right to practice law simply by passing the bar examinations." This is the case most precisely on point for "privilege, not a right."

A closely related disciplinary case, _Ui v. Bonifacio_ (A.C. No. 3319, June 8, 2000), similarly states that "the practice of law is a privilege" that "can be revoked, subject to the mandate of due process, once a lawyer violates his [duties]."

### 3. Implications of the doctrine

- **No vested right; revocable for cause.** Because the practice of law is a privilege, it may be suspended or withdrawn through disbarment when the lawyer ceases to meet the conditions. Disbarment is not punishment but protection of courts, clients, and the public (_Almacen_).
- **Continuing good moral character.** Good moral character is required not only at admission but throughout the lawyer's career; loss of it is ground for discipline (_Argosino_; and reiterated in numerous disbarment cases).
- **Plenary regulatory and disciplinary power of the Supreme Court.** Admission, suspension, disbarment, and reinstatement are inherent judicial functions vested exclusively in the Court (_Cunanan_; _Edillon_). Discipline is governed by Rule 139-B of the Rules of Court and, now, Canon VI of the CPRA.
- **Imbued with public interest.** The lawyer's duties run not only to the client but to the courts, the profession, and the public; the privilege exists to serve the administration of justice.

### 4. How the CPRA (2023) frames the privilege

The Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC) was approved by the Supreme Court En Banc on April 11, 2023 and took effect on May 29, 2023 — 15 days after its publication in the Philippine Star and Manila Bulletin on May 14, 2023. It replaced the 34-year-old 1988 Code of Professional Responsibility, and was crafted after a five-leg "Ethics Caravan" in which over 2,000 legal practitioners nationwide took part from September 2022 to January 2023.

Its Preamble frames the lawyer, "as a guardian of the rule of law," as one who "owes allegiance to the Constitution," is bound by the profession's ethical standards, and "as an officer of the court, assists in the administration of justice." Canon III (Fidelity), Section 1 defines the practice of law as "the rendition of legal service or performance of acts or the application of law, legal principles, and judgment, in or out of court... and requires membership in the Philippine bar as qualification." Section 2 reiterates that "as an officer of the court, a lawyer shall uphold the rule of law and conscientiously assist in the speedy and efficient administration of justice." Canon VI (Accountability) provides that failure to honor the lawyer's covenant "makes the lawyer unfit to continue in the practice of law." Although the precise phrases "privilege," "imbued with public interest," and "burdened with conditions" are jurisprudential rather than CPRA text, the CPRA's structure — conditions of good standing plus a robust disciplinary regime culminating in suspension or disbarment — embodies the privilege doctrine.

### 5. Qualifications and continuing requirements for the privilege

- **Admission to the bar (Rule 138, Rules of Court):** Filipino citizenship, minimum age, residence, completion of prescribed law studies, good moral character with no pending charges involving moral turpitude, passing the bar examinations, taking the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]], and signing the Roll of Attorneys.
- **IBP membership and dues:** Mandatory membership in the Integrated Bar of the Philippines; payment of annual dues is a continuing condition, the non-payment of which can lead to delinquency and ultimately disbarment (_Edillon_). The Revised IBP By-Laws (adopted by Supreme Court Resolution dated March 8, 2023) set annual dues at P4,000, and prolonged delinquency — failure to pay beyond one year from the due date — can result in striking from the Roll.
- **MCLE (Bar Matter No. 850, issued October 2, 2001):** Members "shall complete every three (3) years at least thirty-six (36) hours" of continuing legal education, of which "at least six (6) hours shall be devoted to legal ethics... four (4) hours [to] trial and pretrial skills... five (5) hours [to] alternative dispute resolution... nine (9) hours [to] updates on substantive and procedural laws, and jurisprudence... four (4) hours [to] legal writing and oral advocacy... [and] two (2) hours [to] international law." Non-compliance leads to listing as a delinquent member and bars practice until cured, and pleadings must indicate the MCLE compliance number (Bar Matter No. 1922).
- **Continuing good moral character and ethical compliance:** Ongoing adherence to the CPRA, the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]], and lawful court orders.

## Caveats

- The popular composite quotation often misattributed to _[[People of the Philippines v. Hon. Bonifacio Sanz Maceda and Avelino T. Javellana|People v. Maceda]]_ ("a bar candidate does not have the right to enjoy the practice of law simply by passing the bar examinations") in fact derives from _Aguirre v. Rana_ and _Ui v. Bonifacio_; users should confirm attributions.
- Some parallel citations (e.g., SCRA vs. Phil. Reports) vary across secondary sources; the official Supreme Court E-Library is controlling.
- The CPRA text itself does not use the words "privilege" or "imbued with public interest"; those remain jurisprudential characterizations applied alongside the CPRA.
