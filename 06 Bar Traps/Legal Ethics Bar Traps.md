# Legal Ethics Bar Traps

Use this note for short, exam-facing traps. Each entry should point back to a doctrine note and at least one case.

## Practice of law traps

- Practice of law is not limited to court appearances. See [[Practice of Law]].
- Passing the bar is not enough; oath-taking and signing the Roll matter. See [[Admission to the Bar]] and [[Signing of the Roll of Attorneys]].
- Self-representation is not the same as representing another as counsel. See [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]].
- Notarial acts are not empty clerical acts. See [[Notarial Practice]].
- A corporation cannot practice law by hiring lawyers as a business front. See [[Unauthorized Practice of Law]].
- A public official who is also a lawyer may be subject to special limits. See [[Public Officials and Private Practice]].

## Admission and continuing-requirement traps

- The amended Rule 138 (A.M. No. 24-10-05-SC, effective 3 Nov. 2025) dropped the stand-alone "resident of the Philippines" requirement and replaced "21 years of age" with "legal age" — pre-2025 reviewers still show the old text. See [[Admission to the Bar]].
- A pending, undecided moral-turpitude charge no longer automatically disqualifies an applicant; only a charge **decided with finality** does, under the amended rule. See [[Admission to the Bar]].
- Completing the Clinical Legal Education Program (CLEP) is now a Bar Examination prerequisite for every applicant, not just students who want Level 1/2 court practice. See [[Admission to the Bar]] and [[Law Student Practice Rule]].
- An MCLE-delinquent lawyer does not lose the underlying authority to practice law, but may not validly sign pleadings. See [[Continuing Requirements for the Practice of Law]].
- Reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 does not automatically restore the right to resume law practice; a Supreme Court petition is still required. See [[Continuing Requirements for the Practice of Law]].
- The ULAS 60-hour legal-aid requirement can be only half-satisfied by a financial contribution — at least 25% of the hours must always be rendered personally. See [[Continuing Requirements for the Practice of Law]].
- Falsely claiming MCLE exemption in a pleading is a far graver wrong than simply failing to complete MCLE units — the first is a knowing misrepresentation to the court, the second is cured through the rule's own non-compliance fee. See [[Continuing Requirements for the Practice of Law]] and [[Turla v. Atty. Caringal]].

## Citizenship and good moral character traps

- A legitimate child born under the 1935 Constitution to a Filipino mother and an alien father must **elect** Philippine citizenship within a reasonable time (roughly three years) of reaching majority — passing the Bar Examinations does not cure an untimely election. See [[Admission to the Bar]] and [[In Re Application for Admission to the Philippine Bar of Vicente D. Ching]].
- Reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 does **not** automatically restore a lawyer's privilege to practice — a Supreme Court petition and a fresh Lawyer's Oath are still required. See [[In Re Petition to Re-acquire the Privilege to Practice Law, B.M. No. 2112]].
- Good moral character is a **continuing** requirement, not a one-time admission check — the lapse of time between misconduct and a disbarment complaint does not excuse it. See [[Admission to the Bar]] and [[Garrido v. Attys. Garrido and Valencia]].
- The LEB's power over legal education and the Supreme Court's power over admission to practice are **distinct** — LEB may regulate law schools but may not gatekeep who becomes a lawyer (the basis for striking down PhiLSAT). See [[Pimentel v. Legal Education Board]].

## Lawyer's Oath traps

- The Lawyer's Oath is not a ceremonial formality confined to admission day — courts cite oath violations as an independent or aggravating ground across nearly every kind of disciplinary case, including ordinary negligence. See [[Revised Lawyer's Oath]] and [[Katipunan v. Atty. Carrera]].
- The 2023-revised oath is not merely a stylistic update — it adds substantive commitments (justice, equitable access to justice) with no real counterpart in the pre-2023 text. See [[Revised Lawyer's Oath]].

## Non-lawyer appearance and prohibited-proceeding traps

- The "agent or friend" exception under Sec. 34, Rule 138 works only in first-level courts; it does not carry over to the RTC or higher. See [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]].
- A lawyer may always appear **as a party** in Katarungang Pambarangay, small claims, or IPRA customary proceedings — what is barred is appearing **as counsel** for someone else. See [[Prohibited Proceedings for Lawyers]].
- A law student may appear personally as an agent or friend of a party before an **inferior court** under Sec. 34, Rule 138, without a supervising lawyer — a distinct track from Rule 138-A practice. See [[Law Student Practice Rule]] and [[Cruz v. Mina]].
- The Rules on Expedited Procedures in the First Level Courts (A.M. No. 08-8-7-SC) are **not** a lawyer-free zone generally — only its small-claims track restricts lawyer appearance; the expedited-procedure civil track proper allows counsel exactly as ordinary litigation does. See [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]].
- Party representation in **arbitration** is broader than in litigation — a party may be represented by counsel or any other authorized representative — but the **foreign-lawyer** carve-out is narrower still, limited to international commercial arbitration seated in the Philippines. See [[Appearance of Non-Lawyers]] and [[Foreign Lawyers]].

## Unauthorized-practice traps

- "Canon 9, Code of Professional Responsibility" is not current rule text — the 1988 CPR is repealed. The duty not to assist unauthorized practice now lives in CPRA, Canon II, Sec. 35, though case law still calls it a "Canon 9" violation. See [[Unauthorized Practice of Law]].
- The duty not to assist unauthorized practice covers a lawyer's **own** unauthorized practice, not only assistance given to someone else. See [[Unauthorized Practice of Law]].
- A mistake of fact (believing the Roll was already signed) excuses only until the truth is discovered; continuing to practice afterward is willful, because a mistake of law never excuses. See [[Signing of the Roll of Attorneys]].
- Co-signing a substantive pleading under a disbarred lawyer's firm name is active, sanctionable assistance; a passive filing as counsel of record may not be — a law firm has a juridical personality distinct from its named partners. See [[Unauthorized Practice of Law]].
- **Holding out** a non-lawyer as authorized to practice — through letterhead, an ID, or any public-facing device — is itself the violation; no actual court appearance by the non-lawyer needs to be proven. See [[Cambaliza v. Cristal-Tenorio]].
- Practicing law **during an active suspension** is a fresh, separately punishable offense (disobedience of the Court's own order), not merely a continuation of the original case. See [[Tan, Jr. v. Atty. Gumba]].
- Bar discipline is not confined to conduct performed "as counsel" — dishonesty in a purely personal transaction, or serious misconduct in a government post, can each independently support suspension. See [[Saladaga v. Astorga]] and [[Gonzales-Austria v. Abaya]].

## Public-official practice traps

- Governors and city/municipal mayors are **absolutely** barred from private practice; ordinary Sanggunian members — including Vice-Governors and Vice-Mayors, who merely preside over the Sanggunian — are only **qualifiedly** restricted. Do not treat all local elective officials alike. See [[Public Officials and Private Practice]].
- The constitutional bar on Senators and Representatives covers only **personal appearance** as counsel (including signing/filing pleadings) — not all law practice. See [[Public Officials and Private Practice]].
- The retired-Justice/Judge counsel-appearance restriction is in the **proviso to RA 910, Sec. 1**, not Sec. 5 as many reviewers state. See [[Public Officials and Private Practice]].
- A general agency policy allowing government lawyers to practice privately does not excuse the need for case-specific superior authorization, a stated purpose, and approved leave. See [[Public Officials and Private Practice]].
- Rule 138, Sec. 35's absolute bar on judges reaches even a case begun **before** appointment and involving the judge's own **family** — there is no exception for either. See [[Ziga v. Judge Arejola]].
- Resigning or retiring from government service does **not** immediately clear a lawyer to practice before their own former office — RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) imposes a **one-year** post-employment restriction. See [[In Re Query of Atty. Karen M. Silverio-Buffe]].
