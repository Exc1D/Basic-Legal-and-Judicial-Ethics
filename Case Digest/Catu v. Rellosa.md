
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 5738, February 19, 2008

**Ponente:** Corona, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Public Officials and Private Practice; Scope of Rule 6.03, CPR; Civil Service Permission Requirement

> **Gist:** A punong barangay who presided over barangay conciliation between two disputants, then appeared as counsel for one side once the dispute reached court, was suspended for six months — but not for the conflict-of-interest theory the complainant argued. The Supreme Court held Rule 6.03 of the CPR inapplicable to an incumbent official and disciplined him instead for practicing law without the prior written departmental permission civil service rules require.

## FACTS

Wilfredo Catu was a co-owner of a lot and building on San Andres Street, Malate, Manila. His mother and brother, Regina and Antonio Catu, contested the possession of one unit against Elizabeth Diaz-Catu (Wilfredo's sister-in-law) and Antonio Pastor, who ignored demands to vacate. A complaint was filed before the Lupong Tagapamayapa of Barangay 723, where respondent Atty. Vicente Rellosa was punong barangay. Rellosa summoned the parties to conciliation meetings; when they failed to settle, he issued, in his official capacity, the certification allowing the appropriate action to be filed in court.

Regina and Antonio then filed an ejectment case against Elizabeth and Pastor before the Metropolitan Trial Court of Manila. Rellosa entered his appearance as counsel **for the defendants** — Elizabeth and Pastor — the very parties on the opposite side of the dispute he had just tried to conciliate as punong barangay. He prepared and signed the answer with counterclaim, the pre-trial brief, the position paper, and the notice of appeal. Wilfredo filed the administrative complaint, arguing that Rellosa committed impropriety as both lawyer and public officer by representing one side of a dispute in which he had earlier intervened as the presiding barangay official.

Rellosa's defense was that he heard the case with utmost objectivity as head of the Lupon, that the parties simply failed to settle, and that he only agreed to help Elizabeth — for free, given her financial distress — after the case had already moved to court.

The IBP-CBD found grounds to discipline him, reasoning that he had violated **Rule 6.03 of the Code of Professional Responsibility** (barring a lawyer from accepting engagement, after leaving government service, on a matter handled while in that service) and, as an elective official, the private-practice authorization requirement of **RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2)**, which in turn breached **Canon 1** of the CPR. It recommended a one-month suspension, adopted by the IBP Board of Governors.

## ISSUES

1. Whether respondent violated Rule 6.03 of the CPR by appearing as counsel for a party in a dispute he had earlier presided over as punong barangay.
2. Whether, as an elective barangay official, respondent's private practice was governed by RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) or by RA 7160.
3. Whether respondent's appearance as counsel without prior written permission from the head of his department was itself a disciplinable violation.

## RULING

The Supreme Court **modified** the IBP's findings and increased the penalty. On the first issue, Rule 6.03 could not apply: by its own text, it binds only a lawyer who has **left** government service, in connection with a matter handled while still in that service. Rellosa was still an **incumbent** punong barangay when he took Elizabeth's case, so the rule never came into play, citing *PCGG v. Sandiganbayan*.

On the second issue, the Court held that **RA 7160, Sec. 90** — not RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) — governs the practice of profession by elective local officials, since a special law prevails over a general law on the same subject (*lex specialibus derogat generalibus*). Section 90 absolutely bars governors and city/municipal mayors from practicing their profession, and Sec. 91 qualifiedly restricts Sanggunian members (province/city/municipality level). Neither section says anything about the **punong barangay** or members of the **sangguniang barangay**. Applying *expressio unius est exclusio alterius*, the Court held barangay officials are excluded from RA 7160's restrictions altogether and are presumptively free to practice their profession — consistent with barangay officials not being required to render full-time service (the sangguniang barangay meets only twice a month).

That did not end the inquiry. On the third issue, the Court held that a civil service officer or employee whose duties do not require full-time availability to government — which includes a punong barangay — may still engage in private practice **only with the written permission of the head of the department concerned**, under **Section 12, Rule XVIII of the Revised Civil Service Rules**. For a punong barangay, that head is the Secretary of the Interior and Local Government. Rellosa never secured this permission before appearing as counsel for Elizabeth and Pastor. That omission — not the conflict-of-interest theory originally argued — was the actual violation: failing to comply with a civil service rule breached his oath to obey the laws (**Canon 1**), constituted unlawful conduct (**Rule 1.01**), and fell short of the integrity **Canon 7** demands of every lawyer.

The Court found Rellosa **GUILTY** of professional misconduct and **SUSPENDED** him from the practice of law for **six months** — increased from the IBP's recommended one month — with a stern warning. It closed by advising him to "look up and take to heart the meaning of the word *delicadeza*."

## DOCTRINE

**Rule 6.03 of the CPR binds only lawyers who have already left government service, on a matter they intervened in while still there — it does not reach an incumbent official's conduct.** Separately, **RA 7160, Sec. 90, as a special law, governs the practice of profession by elective local officials and displaces RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2)'s general authorization rule for that class**; because neither Sec. 90 nor Sec. 91 restricts barangay officials, punong barangay and sangguniang barangay members are not restricted by RA 7160 at all. But **a civil service officer or employee whose full time is not required to be at the government's disposal must still secure prior written permission from the head of the department before engaging in private practice**, under Sec. 12, Rule XVIII of the Revised Civil Service Rules — and failing to do so is itself actionable under Canons 1 and 7 and Rule 1.01 of the CPR.

## NOTES

This case is a favorite reviewer trap precisely because the "obvious" answer is wrong. It is tempting to read the facts — barangay official conciliates a dispute, then represents one side of it in court — as a textbook Rule 6.03 conflict-of-interest violation. The Supreme Court expressly rejected that framing because Rellosa never left government service; Rule 6.03 has no application to a sitting official. Compare [[In Re Query of Atty. Karen M. Silverio-Buffe]], which is the genuine Rule 6.03/RA 6713, Sec. 7(b)(2) fact pattern — a *former* Clerk of Court appearing before her own old branch within the one-year post-employment window. Reading the two cases together fixes the incumbent-versus-former line that examiners like to blur.

Decided under the pre-2023 Code of Professional Responsibility; the specific provisions cited (Canon 1, Canon 7, Rule 1.01) map onto the CPRA's Canon II (Propriety) and Canon I (Independence) framework today, but the citations above are given as the Court actually wrote them.
