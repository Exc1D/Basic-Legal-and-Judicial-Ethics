
**A.C. No. 10738, September 14, 2020**

**Ponente:** Perlas-Bernabe, J.

**Subject:** Legal Ethics — Code of Professional Responsibility

> **Gist:** The SC held that a lawyer-client relationship may be established even without a formal contract, retainer, or payment, and found respondent Atty. Gallanosa guilty of soliciting legal business, encroaching on another lawyer's professional employment, neglecting a legal matter, and breaching the trust reposed by her client, warranting a six-month suspension from the practice of law.

## FACTS

Marcelina Zamora filed a complaint for disciplinary action against Atty. Marilyn V. Gallanosa arising from the latter's conduct in connection with an illegal dismissal case filed by Zamora's husband against DM Consunji, Inc. before the NLRC, where the husband was being represented by the Public Attorney's Office (PAO). Sometime in June 2012, while Zamora was outside the office of Labor Arbiter Virginia T. Luyas-Azarraga, respondent approached her and inquired about the case. Upon reviewing the PAO-drafted Position Paper, respondent disparaged the work of the PAO lawyer, telling Zamora that the PAO's efforts were useless and that she would lose the case. Respondent went further by making baseless accusations against the Labor Arbiter and the opposing counsel, insinuating collusion between them, before advising Zamora to have a new Position Paper prepared instead.

Zamora subsequently visited respondent's office in Quezon City, where respondent told her that replacing the Position Paper was possible and quoted a professional fee of twenty percent (20%) of the judgment award on a contingent basis — meaning no payment would be required until the case was won. Respondent drafted the new Position Paper and instructed Zamora to submit it to LA Azarraga. Although the opposing counsel did not object to the substitution, LA Azarraga asked whether respondent would be attending the next hearing. Respondent confirmed her attendance but failed to appear, causing the case to be submitted for resolution without hearing.

When the labor case was decided, Zamora informed respondent and was assured that an appeal would be filed. However, the reglementary period lapsed without any appeal being perfected. Confronted about the lapse, respondent denied being Zamora's lawyer, claiming she had not signed the Position Paper and had not received any fees. Despite eventually being prevailed upon to assist, respondent informed Zamora upon her return from Bicol that the appeal period had already expired. Respondent then offered to negotiate for a higher financial aid package from the opposing party but reneged on this promise and eventually ignored Zamora's follow-ups altogether. Zamora thus charged respondent with violating several rules of the Code of Professional Responsibility (CPR), including solicitation of legal business, encroachment on another lawyer's professional employment, neglect of legal matter, breach of trust, and conduct unbecoming of a lawyer.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer-client relationship was established between respondent and complainant such that respondent may be held administratively liable for the acts complained of, despite the absence of a formal retainer, payment of fees, or written contract.

Whether respondent violated the CPR provisions on solicitation of legal business, encroachment upon another lawyer's professional employment, and neglect of legal matter entrusted to her.

## RULING

The SC adopted the findings of the IBP and found respondent guilty of violating **Rules 2.03, 8.02, and 18.03**, as well as **Canon 17** of the CPR. The pivotal question was whether a lawyer-client relationship existed, and the SC answered this in the affirmative. Canon 3 of the CPR provides that "a lawyer in making known his legal services shall use only true, honest, fair, dignified and objective information or statement of facts," and the SC has consistently reminded the Bench and Bar that the **practice of law is a profession, not a business**. Lawyers are prohibited from advertising their services like merchants peddling wares, and any form of solicitation that commercializes the profession is condemned. Rule 2.03 of the CPR explicitly proscribes "ambulance chasing" — the solicitation of almost any kind of business by an attorney, personally or through an agent, in order to gain employment.

The SC rejected respondent's defense that she was never Zamora's lawyer. **Practice of law** encompasses any activity, in or out of court, that requires the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training, and experience. It includes, among others, the preparation of pleadings and other papers incident to actions; the management of proceedings on behalf of clients before courts; and advising clients on matters involving the determination by a trained legal mind of the legal effects of facts and conditions. Respondent herself admitted that she met with Zamora, advised her on what legal course to pursue in the labor case, and prepared the new Position Paper that was eventually submitted — all of which constitute the practice of law. 

Critically, the SC held that to constitute professional employment, **it is not essential that the client paid a retainer, employed the attorney on any previous occasion, executed a written contract, or that a formal engagement followed the consultation**. What matters is that the advice and assistance of an attorney was sought and received on a matter pertinent to the legal profession. Applied here, the consultation that produced the new Position Paper, combined with respondent's actual drafting and submission of that pleading, plainly established the lawyer-client relationship — regardless of respondent's later attempt to disclaim it.

Given this relationship, respondent's preparation of a new Position Paper to replace the one filed by the PAO violated **Rule 8.02** of the CPR, which prohibits a lawyer from directly or indirectly encroaching upon the professional employment of another lawyer. Settled jurisprudence bars an attorney from "stealing" another lawyer's client or inducing the latter's retention through promises of better service, good results, or reduced fees. Respondent was fully aware that the PAO was already representing Zamora's husband, yet she assumed the drafting of the replacement pleading, effectively displacing the original counsel without any justifiable basis.

Respondent's failure to attend the scheduled hearing and, more egregiously, her failure to file the appeal that caused the reglementary period to lapse constituted **negligence in handling a legal matter entrusted to her**, in violation of **Canon 17** and **Rule 18.03** of the CPR. These provisions enjoin lawyers to be mindful of the trust and confidence reposed in them and to refrain from neglecting matters entrusted to their care. Her subsequent denial of any professional relation with Zamora only deepened the breach, as it constituted an attempt to evade accountability for her own negligence.

Applying the precedent set in *Hernandez v. Padilla* — where a lawyer who similarly denied any lawyer-client relationship and was negligent in handling a client's case was suspended for six months — the SC imposed the same penalty here.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer-client relationship is established the moment a person seeks and receives legal advice and assistance from an attorney on a matter pertinent to the profession, even without a formal retainer, written contract, prior professional engagement, or payment of fees.** The subsequent denial of such a relationship, or the absence of a formal engagement following the consultation, is of no consequence.

The **practice of law** embraces any activity, in or out of court, requiring the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training, and experience — including (a) the preparation of pleadings and other papers incident to actions; (b) the management of actions and proceedings on behalf of clients; and (c) advising clients and acting for them in matters involving the legal effects of facts and conditions determined by a trained legal mind.

A lawyer **shall not solicit legal business** for the purpose of gain (Rule 2.03), shall not **encroach upon the professional employment of another lawyer** (Rule 8.02), shall not **neglect a legal matter entrusted** to them (Rule 18.03), and must be **mindful of the trust and confidence reposed** in them by clients (Canon 17).

## NOTES

This case is frequently cited for the principle that the existence of a lawyer-client relationship is a question of fact — courts look at the totality of the parties' conduct (consultations, advice given, pleadings drafted) rather than the existence of a formal retainer or paid fee.

It is also a useful pairing with *Hernandez v. Padilla* — both are textbook examples of how a lawyer's attempt to deny the existence of professional employment does not insulate them from administrative liability when their conduct clearly demonstrates otherwise.

Watch out for the bar exam trick: the respondent's claim that "no retainer was paid" or "no contract was signed" is *never* a defense to the existence of a lawyer-client relationship.
