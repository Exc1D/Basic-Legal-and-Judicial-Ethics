
**A.C. No. 13911 & 13912, October 03, 2023**

**Ponente:** Lopez, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Notarial Practice and Disciplinary Authority of the SC

> **Gist:** The SC suspended a lawyer for notarizing a document without the affiant's personal appearance and established a new guideline that a lawyer's suspension begins via constructive receipt if their whereabouts are unknown despite diligent efforts by the IBP.

## FACTS
Spouses Joy and Rimas Calixto filed consolidated administrative complaints against Atty. Cora Jane P. Baleros for violating the **2004 Rules on Notarial Practice**. The controversy began when Joy, desperate for funds to treat her daughter's illness, secured a loan that eventually led to the fraudulent transfer of their family property. Rimas discovered that a Special Power of Attorney and a Deed of Absolute Sale, both notarized by Atty. Baleros, were used to authorize Joy to sell the property. Rimas insisted he never executed the SPA, as he was in Mountain Province tending to his mother when it was supposedly notarized in Baguio, and his signature was forged. When the IBP Committee on Bar Discipline tried to serve orders requiring Atty. Baleros to answer the complaints, they discovered she had left the country in 2015 without leaving a forwarding address. The IBP sent notices to her last known addresses, but they were returned for being insufficient or non-existent. Despite her absence and failure to file an answer, the IBP recommended indefinite suspension and perpetual disqualification from being a notary public, which the IBP Board of Governors adopted.

## ISSUES
The issues are whether Atty. Baleros was denied due process due to the failure of actual notice, whether she violated the 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice by notarizing the SPA without the affiant's personal appearance, and the novel question of when a lawyer's period of suspension should commence if their whereabouts are entirely unknown.

## RULING
The SC held that there was no denial of due process. Citing the doctrine in Stemmerik vs. Atty. Mas, the SC explained that lawyers are duty-bound to update their contact details with the IBP. Since Atty. Baleros rendered service of notice impossible by leaving the country without updating her records, the notice requirement cannot apply to her, and she is deemed to have waived it. The IBP exhausted all avenues to notify her, satisfying the essence of due process which is simply an opportunity to be heard. 

On the merits, the SC found Atty. Baleros guilty of violating the 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice and the **Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (CPRA)**. A notary public must require the physical presence of the affiant to verify the genuineness of their signature. Since Rimas was physically in Mountain Province during the supposed execution in Baguio, Atty. Baleros failed in her duty. This breach of the Notarial Rules also constitutes a violation of Canon III, Section 2 of the CPRA on Fidelity. Considering her previous administrative liability in Dr. Malvar vs. Atty. Baleros as an aggravating circumstance under Canon VI, Section 38 of the CPRA, the SC imposed a two-year suspension from the practice of law, immediate revocation of her notarial commission, and permanent disqualification from being commissioned as a notary public. 

Addressing the novel issue, the SC modified the Brillantes guidelines, which state that suspension is effective upon receipt by the respondent lawyer. When a lawyer cannot be located despite diligent efforts, the SC construed the phrase "upon receipt thereof" to also mean **constructive receipt**. The suspension begins upon service to the lawyer's address in the official IBP records, provided the decision is sent at least twice to that address.

## DOCTRINE
**When a respondent lawyer who has been meted the penalty of suspension cannot be located and whose whereabouts are unknown despite diligent efforts, the SC shall construe the phrase "upon receipt thereof by the respondent lawyer" to mean constructive receipt.** The decision imposing suspension is deemed received upon service to the lawyer's address as manifested with the IBP, provided that diligent and exhaustive efforts were made to locate the lawyer, and the decision was sent at least twice to the official address on record.

## NOTES
This case clarifies that the CPRA, which took effect in May 2023, applies to pending and future cases, meaning erring notaries are now also charged with violating Canon III on Fidelity in addition to the Notarial Rules. A common misconception addressed here is that a lawyer can evade suspension by simply disappearing or moving abroad; the SC's disciplinary authority remains, and constructive receipt ensures the penalty runs regardless of the lawyer's actual location. Finally, this case serves as a strict reminder that the personal appearance requirement in notarial practice is absolute and cannot be sacrificed for the convenience of absent affiants.
