
**Full Case Citation:** B.M. No. 1036, June 10, 2003

**Ponente:** Carpio, J.

**Subject:** Legal Ethics — Bar Admission; Unauthorized Practice of Law

> **Gist:** A bar candidate who passed the bar examinations but engaged in the practice of law before taking his oath and signing the Roll of Attorneys is denied admission to the Philippine Bar for lack of moral fitness, since unauthorized practice of law demonstrates the very moral unfitness that disqualifies one from the legal profession.

## FACTS

Edwin L. Rana, respondent, passed the 2000 Bar Examinations. On 21 May 2001, one day before the scheduled mass oath-taking of successful bar examinees, complainant Donna Marie S. Aguirre filed a Petition for Denial of Admission to the Bar against him, charging him with unauthorized practice of law, grave misconduct, violation of law, and grave misrepresentation. The SC allowed Rana to take the lawyer's oath on 22 May 2001 at the Philippine International Convention Center but prohibited him from signing the Roll of Attorneys pending resolution of the charges.

Complainant alleged that Rana, while not yet a lawyer, appeared as counsel for vice mayoralty candidate George Bunan before the Municipal Board of Election Canvassers (MBEC) of Mandaon, Masbate. Specifically, Rana filed a pleading dated 19 May 2001 entitled "Formal Objection to the Inclusion in the Canvassing of Votes in Some Precincts for the Office of Vice-Mayor," in which he signed himself as "counsel for George Bunan." Complainant further alleged that Rana was a secretary of the Sangguniang Bayan of Mandaon, Masbate, a government position that supposedly prohibited him from acting as counsel for private clients. Complainant also accused Rana of grave misrepresentation, claiming he filed the pleading without Bunan's actual engagement of his services as a political maneuver to delay the proclamation of the winning vice mayoralty candidate.

In his Comment, Rana admitted that Bunan sought his "specific assistance" to represent him before the MBEC, but claimed he assisted Bunan "not as a lawyer but as a person who knows the law." He submitted a copy of his resignation as secretary of the Sangguniang Bayan dated 11 May 2001, which was allegedly accepted on the same date. Rana characterized the complaint as politically motivated, since complainant was the daughter of the losing mayoralty candidate in Mandaon, Masbate. The case was referred to the Office of the Bar Confidant (OBC), which found that Rana indeed appeared as counsel before the MBEC prior to taking his oath and recommended denial of admission to the Bar.

## ISSUES

The principal issue is whether respondent Rana, who passed the 2000 Bar Examinations and took the lawyer's oath but had not yet signed the Roll of Attorneys, may be denied admission to the Philippine Bar on the ground that he engaged in the practice of law before being licensed to do so. A secondary issue is whether the charges of violation of law (acting as counsel while a government employee) and grave misrepresentation are supported by evidence.

## RULING

The SC agreed with the OBC's findings and denied Rana's admission to the Philippine Bar on the ground of unauthorized practice of law. The records showed that Rana appeared as counsel and signed pleadings before the MBEC in connection with the May 2001 elections, all prior to his taking the lawyer's oath on 22 May 2001. In his pleading dated 19 May 2001, Rana explicitly described himself as "Counsel for, and in behalf of Vice Mayoralty Candidate, GEORGE T. BUNAN." Likewise, on 14 May 2001, mayoralty candidate Emily Estipona-Hao "retained" Rana as counsel, and on the same date Rana entered his "appearance as counsel" for Estipona-Hao and the REFORMA LM-PPC before the MBEC. Rana even signed as counsel for Estipona-Hao in a petition for proclamation dated 19 May 2001. All of these acts clearly constituted the practice of law without a license.

Drawing from *Philippine Lawyers Association v. Agrava* (105 Phil. 173, 1959), the SC reiterated that the practice of law is not confined to litigation in court; it embraces the preparation of pleadings, the management of actions and proceedings on behalf of clients before judicial and quasi-judicial bodies, conveyancing, and all advice to clients involving the application of legal knowledge. Citing *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]* (G.R. No. 100113, 3 September 1991, 201 SCRA 210), the SC further defined "practice of law" as any activity, in or out of court, requiring the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training, and experience — encompassing any service that requires the use of legal knowledge or skill. By appearing in proceedings and filing pleadings before the MBEC without being a member of the Bar, Rana performed acts that are typically performed only by members of the legal profession.

The SC emphasized that the right to practice law is not a natural or constitutional right but a privilege limited to persons of good moral character with special qualifications duly ascertained and certified. A bar candidate does not acquire the right to practice law simply by passing the bar examinations; possession of **moral integrity** is of greater importance than possession of legal learning. As the SC stressed at the outset of the decision, "Before one is admitted to the Philippine Bar, he must possess the requisite moral integrity for membership in the legal profession… A bar candidate who is morally unfit cannot practice law even if he passes the bar examinations." Rana's act of holding himself out as "counsel" while knowing he had no authority to practice law demonstrated the precise moral unfitness that disqualifies one from admission.

The SC also clarified the two essential requisites for becoming a full-fledged lawyer under existing rules: (1) the lawyer's oath administered by the SC, and (2) the signing in the Roll of Attorneys. Rana had taken the oath but had not signed the Roll. The SC held that even if both requisites had been completed, the privilege of admission to the Bar could still be withheld from one who practiced law without a license, citing *Ui v. Bonifacio* (Administrative Case No. 3319, 8 June 2000, 333 SCRA 38). The SC further noted that under Section 3(e) of Rule 71 of the Rules of Court, a person who engages in the unauthorized practice of law is liable for indirect contempt of court, referencing *Beltran, Jr. v. Abad* (B.M. No. 139, 28 March 1983, 121 SCRA 217) where a bar candidate who practiced before admission was held in contempt.

On the charge of violation of law, the SC found no supporting evidence. Rana tendered his resignation as secretary of the Sangguniang Bayan effective 11 May 2001, and the resignation was accepted on the same date by Vice-Mayor Napoleon Relox. Thus, Rana was no longer a government employee at the time of the acts complained of. On the charge of grave misrepresentation, the records showed that Bunan did authorize Rana to represent him before the MBEC and similar bodies, negating the claim of misrepresentation. However, even if those two charges failed, the unauthorized practice of law alone was sufficient to deny Rana's admission.

## DOCTRINE

**The practice of law is a privilege, not a right, and may be withheld even from one who has passed the bar examinations if the candidate engaged in the unauthorized practice of law before admission, since such conduct demonstrates moral unfitness for membership in the legal profession.** The right to practice law presupposes the possession of **good moral character**, integrity, legal knowledge, educational attainment, and public trust, since a lawyer is an officer of the court. The two essential requisites to become a full-fledged lawyer are: **(1) the taking of the lawyer's oath administered by the SC; and (2) the signing of the Roll of Attorneys.** "Practice of law" means any activity, in or out of court, that requires the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training, and experience, including the preparation of pleadings, representation before judicial and quasi-judicial bodies, conveyancing, and all advice to clients involving the application of legal knowledge and skill. A person who engages in the practice of law without being a member of the Philippine Bar is liable for indirect contempt of court under Section 3(e) of Rule 71 of the Rules of Court.

## NOTES

This case is frequently cited in bar admission and legal ethics examinations as authority for the proposition that passing the bar alone does not entitle one to practice law — the oath and signing of the Roll are equally indispensable.

The decision is a useful companion to *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]* for the definition of "practice of law" and clarifies that representation before quasi-judicial bodies like the MBEC falls squarely within the scope of legal practice, not merely courtroom litigation.

A common misconception this case corrects is that a successful bar examinee may immediately act as counsel upon release of results; in reality, any such premature engagement constitutes unauthorized practice of law and may permanently bar admission, regardless of political motivation behind the complaint.
