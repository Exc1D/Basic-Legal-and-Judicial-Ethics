**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Practicing Law While Suspended; Willful Disobedience of a Lawful Order

> **Gist:** A lawyer who continues filing pleadings and appearing as counsel during an already-imposed suspension is committing a fresh, independently punishable offense — willful disobedience of the Court's own order — warranting an additional suspension on top of the original one.

## FACTS

Atty. Haide V. Gumba had earlier been suspended from the practice of law arising from a separate transaction with complainant Tomas P. Tan, Jr. (a loan secured by a deed of sale over property that Atty. Gumba's own special power of attorney did not actually authorize her to sell). After being duly notified of her suspension, Atty. Gumba nonetheless continued to practice law — filing pleadings and personally appearing as counsel in courts during the period she was supposed to be suspended.

## ISSUES

Whether continuing to practice law during an active, duly-notified suspension constitutes disobedience of a lawful order of the Court warranting an additional, stiffer penalty.

## RULING

The SC imposed an additional six-month suspension on top of Atty. Gumba's original six-month suspension, with a warning that a repetition would be dealt with more severely. The Court treated her continued practice during the suspension period not as a mere continuation of the original misconduct, but as a **separate act of willful disobedience** to a lawful order of the Court — the very body whose authority a suspended lawyer is bound to respect above all. Practicing law without authority during a suspension undermines the disciplinary system itself: a penalty that a lawyer can simply ignore is no penalty at all.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who practices law during an active suspension commits a separate, additional offense — willful disobedience of the Court's order — distinct from and punishable on top of the misconduct that gave rise to the original suspension.**

## NOTES

Pair with [[Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas]] and [[Canonoy vs. Atty. Guibone]] for the fuller picture of how strictly the Court treats the boundaries of a suspension: the period is fixed by the Court's own resolution (not the lawyer's convenience), lifting requires sworn compliance, and any practice during the suspended period is punished as a fresh violation rather than folded into the original case.
