
**G.R. No. 100113, September 3, 1991 (Second Division)**

**Ponente:** Justice Jorge R. Paras

**Subject:** Constitutional Law / Legal and Judicial Ethics — Interpretation of "practice of law" as a constitutional qualification for public office

> **Gist:** The SC held that Atty. Christian Monsod's combined work as a corporate executive, international finance officer, government consultant, Constitutional Commissioner, and civic advocate — in which he consistently applied legal knowledge to negotiation, drafting, advisory, and policy functions — satisfied the constitutional requirement under Section 1(1), Article IX-C that a COMELEC Chairman must have been "engaged in the practice of law for at least ten years," and absent a clear showing of grave abuse of discretion, the Commission on Appointments' confirmation of his nomination must be upheld.

## FACTS

The case arose from a constitutional confrontation framed as a single qualification question. Petitioner Renato Cayetano, suing as a citizen and taxpayer, filed a petition for certiorari and prohibition to annul the confirmation by the Commission on Appointments of respondent Christian Monsod as Chairman of the COMELEC. President Corazon C. Aquino had nominated Monsod in a letter received by the Secretariat of the Commission on Appointments on April 25, 1991. Following public hearings, the Commission on Appointments confirmed the nomination on June 5, 1991, and Monsod took his oath of office and assumed the chairmanship on June 18, 1991. Cayetano anchored his challenge solely on the ground that Monsod had not been "engaged in the practice of law for at least ten years" as required by Section 1(1), Article IX-C of the 1987 Constitution.

Monsod's credentials were substantial but unconventional for a candidate of the Bar. He passed the Philippine Bar examinations of 1960 with a grade of 86.55%, had been a dues-paying member of the Integrated Bar since its inception in 1972-73, and had paid professional license fees as a lawyer for more than ten years. After graduating from the UP College of Law and a brief stint in his father's law office, however, he pursued an M.A. in Economics at the University of Pennsylvania and went on to a career in international finance and corporate management. From 1963 to 1970 he served with the World Bank Group as an operations officer negotiating loans and coordinating legal, economic, and project work in Costa Rica and Panama; upon returning to the Philippines in 1970 he joined the Meralco Group, later served as chief executive officer of an investment bank and of a business conglomerate, and from 1986 onward acted as legal and economic consultant or chief executive officer for various companies.

His parallel work in the public sphere included serving as Secretary-General (1986) and National Chairman (1987) of NAMFREL — appearing for it in accreditation hearings before the COMELEC — and as Co-Chairman of the Bishops-Businessmen's Conference for Human Development, where he lobbied for the agrarian reform law and the urban land reform bill. He sat as a member of the Davide Commission (the 1990 quasi-judicial fact-finding body) and of the 1986-1987 Constitutional Commission, where he chaired the Committee on Accountability of Public Officers. None of these latter positions required bar membership for appointment. Petitioner did not attack Monsod's integrity or executive competence — only whether his atypical career qualified as "practice of law" in the constitutional sense.

## ISSUES

Whether the SC may properly review the determination by the Commission on Appointments that a nominee possesses the qualifications required by the Constitution, and whether the Commission committed grave abuse of discretion in confirming Monsod's nomination. At the heart of the case, what activities and what manner of engagement constitute "practice of law" under Section 1(1), Article IX-C of the 1987 Constitution, and whether Atty. Christian Monsod's career as an international finance officer, corporate executive, consultant, and member of various constitutional and quasi-judicial bodies satisfies the requirement that a COMELEC Chairman must have been "engaged in the practice of law for at least ten years."

## RULING

By a sharply divided vote, the SC dismissed the petition. Writing for the majority, Justice Paras rejected the narrow, litigation-centric definition of "practice of law" advanced by the dissenters and instead adopted what he described as the modern conception of law practice, consistent with the framers' intent. Drawing on American and Philippine authorities — Black's Law Dictionary, Philippine Lawyers Association v. Agrava, and In re Opinion of the Justices — he emphasized that the practice of law is not exhausted by courtroom appearances; it embraces conveyancing, legal counselling, the preparation and execution of legal instruments, the giving of legal advice on a wide variety of subjects, and the negotiation of contracts, even when performed entirely outside any court and with no immediate relation to litigation.

Paras gave particular weight to the records of the 1986 Constitutional Commission, where Commissioner Foz obtained the body's explicit clarification that a lawyer's work in an agency such as the COA counts as practice of law as long as it requires the application of legal knowledge or talent. This frank admission of liberal construction, reinforced by the trend in modern law practice toward corporate counselling, preventive lawyering, and cross-disciplinary decision-making, led the majority to conclude that Monsod's career as "a lawyer-economist, a lawyer-manager, a lawyer-entrepreneur of industry, a lawyer-negotiator of contracts, and a lawyer-legislator of both the rich and the poor" comfortably satisfied the ten-year requirement. The majority relied as well on the analogy of the Commission on Audit, the Chairman and two Commissioners of which must — under Section 1(1), Article IX-D — likewise be members of the Bar engaged in the practice of law for at least ten years, with the same Foz interpretation controlling.

On the question of justiciability, the majority invoked the well-settled rule that **appointment is an essentially discretionary power** that courts will not disturb absent a clear showing of **grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction** under Article VIII, Section 1 of the Constitution. Citing Luego v. Civil Service Commission and Central Bank v. Civil Service Commission, Paras noted that the appointee's qualifications, once established, place him beyond challenge on the ground that another might have been more suited for the post — a wisdom-of-choice question reserved to the political branches. The Court emphasized the symmetrical absurdity of judicial review in this domain: just as it could not reverse a rejection by the Commission, neither could it overturn a confirmation absent grave abuse — a position Justice Gutierrez's dissent later catalogued in detail.

Four justices dissented. Justice Padilla, joined by Justice Bidin, argued that "practice" connotes active, habitual, repeated, or customary action, citing the Commission's own Memorandum enumerating four determinants: (1) **habituality** — customarily or habitually holding oneself out to the public as a lawyer; (2) **compensation** — rendering legal services available to the public for a fee or livelihood; (3) **application of legal principle, practice, or procedure** requiring legal knowledge, training, and experience; and (4) an **attorney-client relationship**. Applying these, Padilla concluded that even granting Monsod's activities the broadest possible scope, they were isolated transactions rather than the "succession of acts of the same kind" demanded by [[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva|People v. Villanueva]] (14 SCRA 109). Justice Cruz warned that the majority's global definition would sweep in real estate brokers, insurance adjusters, and even drivers of public utility vehicles — anyone whose work incidentally touches a regulation — thus rendering the qualification toothless. Justice Gutierrez meticulously documented Monsod's employment history and concluded that Monsod had **lawyers working for him** rather than rendering legal services himself; any legal tasks he performed were one-off or seasonal, and bar membership was not even a requirement for several of the bodies on which he had served.

## DOCTRINE

**The "practice of law" is not confined to litigation or the conduct of cases in court. It embraces the rendition of services requiring the knowledge and application of legal principles and technique to serve the interest of another with his consent, including legal counselling, conveyancing, the preparation and execution of legal instruments, the giving of legal advice, and the negotiation of contracts and agreements — performed in or out of court, in government or in private practice.** Under the modern conception recognized by the 1986 Constitutional Commission, the work of lawyers as negotiators, planners, diagnosticians, business counsellors, and public servants falls within the term, and the constitutional requirement of "engaged in the practice of law" is satisfied by substantial, repeated application of legal skill to serve legitimate client or institutional interests across a ten-year span.

Procedurally, the SC will not overturn a confirmation by the Commission on Appointments of a constitutionally qualified appointee except upon a clear showing of **grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction** — there being no authority in the courts to substitute their own choice for that of the Commission, whether the Commission's action is to confirm or to reject.

## NOTES

A classic split-decision case (5-4 in operational terms, with one justice on leave who left no instruction and two who did not participate), this remains the leading Philippine authority on what "practice of law" means under the constitutional qualification clauses for COMELEC and COA officials — and is heavily cited in bar exam questions on Legal Ethics, Political Law, and Election Law.

Note the doctrinal tension that makes the case such fertile classroom material: the majority's functional, liberal reading tied to the framers' intent versus the dissenters' insistence on the stricter **habituality-plus-compensation-plus-attorney-client-relationship** test articulated in [[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva|People v. Villanueva]] and echoed in the Commission on Appointments' own Memorandum.

For Legal and Judicial Ethics purposes specifically, the case is essential reading on three points: the **scope of the practice of law** beyond litigation; the difference between mere **bar membership** and the **active engagement** contemplated by qualification clauses; and the Court's recognition that the four determinants — habituality, compensation, application of legal principle, and attorney-client relationship — remain useful guides for unauthorized practice questions even where the majority's broader definition ultimately prevailed on the constitutional qualification at issue.
