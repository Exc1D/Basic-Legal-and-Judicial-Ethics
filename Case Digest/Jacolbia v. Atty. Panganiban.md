
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 12627, February 18, 2020

**Ponente:** Perlas-Bernabe, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Neglect of a Legal Matter; Failure to Account for and Return Client Funds and Documents; CPRA Canon I, Sec. 1 (Duty to Make Independent, Accessible, Efficient, and Effective Legal Service)

> **Gist:** A lawyer engaged in 2004 to transfer and register a client's land title let twelve years pass without lifting a finger, then refused — without any justification — to return the client's ₱244,865.00 in fees and her original land title upon demand. The Court held this a flagrant breach of diligence and fiduciary duty, suspending him three years and ordering full restitution.

## FACTS

Sometime in 2004, Leilani Jacolbia engaged Atty. Jimmy R. Panganiban to facilitate the transfer and registration of title over a 9,076-square-meter agricultural land in Barangay Quisao, Pililla, Rizal. She paid him ₱244,865.00 as legal fees inclusive of all necessary expenses and turned over the relevant documents, including the original copy of Original Certificate of Title (OCT) No. M-3772.

Several years passed without respondent taking any action on the transfer. On May 16, 2013, complainant sent a demand letter requiring him to return all pertinent documents — including the OCT — and to refund the amount she had paid. Respondent did not comply. Complainant then filed an administrative complaint before the IBP Commission on Bar Discipline (CBD) on August 26, 2016 — twelve years after the engagement began.

Respondent ignored the proceedings at every stage: he did not file an answer, did not attend the mandatory conference despite due notice, and did not file a position paper. His only participation was a motion to reset the mandatory conference and hold his position paper in abeyance, which was denied.

## ISSUES

Whether respondent should be held administratively liable for (1) neglecting a legal matter entrusted to him for twelve years, and (2) refusing to return the client's funds and documents upon demand.

## RULING

Yes. The Investigating Commissioner found respondent liable and recommended a one-year suspension; the IBP Board of Governors increased this to three years plus a ₱15,000.00 fine, given respondent's bad faith, lack of remorse, and repeated disregard of IBP orders. The Supreme Court affirmed in full.

The Court grounded the neglect finding in Canons 2, 17, and 18 (Rule 18.03) of the 1988 Code of Professional Responsibility (CPR) — twelve years of total inaction on an engagement is inexcusable negligence, full stop, regardless of the reason. Separately, respondent's refusal to return the ₱244,865.00 and the original title upon demand violated Canon 16 (Rules 16.01 and 16.03): a lawyer who holds client funds or property in trust must account for and deliver them on demand, and an unexplained refusal to do so **gives rise to a presumption that the lawyer has appropriated the funds for personal use** — "a gross violation of general morality, as well as of professional ethics." Respondent's disregard of the IBP-CBD's own orders was treated as a further, aggravating violation of Canons 11 and 12 (Rule 12.04).

Respondent was suspended from the practice of law for three years, fined ₱15,000.00, and ordered to return the ₱244,865.00 with 6% interest per annum from receipt of the Decision until full payment.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer's prolonged, unexplained failure to act on an entrusted legal matter, compounded by an unjustified refusal to return the client's funds and documents upon demand, is inexcusable negligence and a breach of the fiduciary duty owed to the client — warranting suspension and full restitution with interest.**

## NOTES

This case predates the CPRA (it was decided under the 1988 CPR, before the CPRA's 30 May 2023 effectivity) and is framed throughout in terms of Canons 2, 11, 12, 16, 17, and 18 of the old Code. The syllabus nonetheless anchors it to **CPRA Canon I, Sec. 1** — "A lawyer shall make legal services accessible in an efficient and effective manner… maintain independence, act with integrity, and at all times ensure the efficient and effective delivery of justice." The doctrinal bridge is old CPR **Canon 2** ("A lawyer shall make his legal services available in an efficient and convenient manner compatible with the independence, integrity and effectiveness of the profession"), which the Court expressly invoked here and which the CPRA substantially carries forward into Sec. 1. Read this case as the paradigm failure of that duty: not slow or imperfect service, but twelve years of *none at all*.

The fund-and-document-return holding (Canon 16 lineage) is a second, independent thread in this case — closer to the CPRA's fidelity and fund-handling provisions than to Canon I — but the syllabus places the whole case under Canon I, so this note treats it as a single illustration of what happens when "accessible, efficient, and effective legal service" completely breaks down.
