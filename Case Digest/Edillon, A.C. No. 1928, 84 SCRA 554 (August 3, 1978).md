## In the Matter of the IBP Membership Dues Delinquency of Atty. Marcial A. Edillon (Reinstatement)

**A.C. No. 1928, December 19, 1980**

**Ponente:** Fernando, C.J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Bar Integration; Reinstatement to the Bar

> **Gist:** A lawyer previously disbarred for stubbornly refusing to pay his IBP membership dues may be reinstated once he pays his arrears, accepts the SC's plenary authority to regulate the legal profession, and demonstrates genuine contrition — because the power to discipline is preservative, not vindictive.

## FACTS

Atty. Marcial A. Edillon was disbarred on August 3, 1978 in a unanimous decision penned by the late Chief Justice Castro, after years of defiant refusal to pay his membership dues to the Integrated Bar of the Philippines. The IBP Board of Governors had unanimously adopted Resolution No. 75-65 in Administrative Case No. MDD-1 recommending his removal from the Roll of Attorneys under Section 10, Rule 139-A of the Rules of Court, which provides that default in payment of annual dues for one year shall be a ground for removal of the delinquent member's name from the Roll. Edillon's defense was unyielding: he argued that the mandatory payment of IBP dues violated his constitutional rights to liberty and property, asserting that he was being compelled, as a precondition to remaining a lawyer in good standing, to financially support an organization to which he was personally antagonistic. The SC rejected this contention, citing its earlier Resolution in [*In re: Integration of the Bar of the Philippines* (49 SCRA 22, 1973)](https://lawphil.net/judjuris/juri1973/jan1973/ibp_1973.html), which had exhaustively settled that bar integration raises no constitutional question and is a valid exercise of the Court's power to raise professional standards.

After disbarment, Edillon filed a motion for reconsideration dated August 19, 1978, which was denied on November 13, 1978 because he persisted in denying the full competence of the SC over the matter. From June 5, 1979 onward, however, Edillon sent a series of communications — letters dated June 5, 1979, August 7, 1979, November 13, 1979, and April 12, 1980 — displaying a markedly different tone. The defiance was gone, replaced by pleas rooted in mitigating circumstances: his poor health, his advanced age, and his concern for former clients who still relied on him for counsel. He had, in his career, been a valiant though at times unreasonable defender of the causes entrusted to him. On October 23, 1980, the SC granted his petition for reinstatement through a minute resolution, reserving the issuance of an extended opinion.

## ISSUES

Whether the SC should grant Edillon's petition for reinstatement to the Philippine Bar despite his prior disbarment for the deliberate and persistent refusal to pay IBP membership dues, considering that more than two years had elapsed and that his subsequent actuations appeared to demonstrate a change of heart and acceptance of the Court's plenary authority over the legal profession.

## RULING

The SC resolved to restore Edillon to membership in the bar. Writing for the Court, Chief Justice Fernando affirmed that **the full and plenary discretion in the exercise of its competence to reinstate a disbarred member of the bar admits of no doubt.** All relevant factors — public interest, the integrity of the profession, and the welfare of the recreant who has purged himself of guilt — must be given due weight in determining whether reinstatement is warranted. The Court found that Edillon had satisfied the conditions for reinstatement: he had fully paid his delinquent IBP membership fees, submitted a verified application for reinstatement to the IBP Board of Governors, and executed an undertaking to abide by all IBP bylaws and resolutions. Critically, there was now a clear acceptance on his part of the competence of the SC in exercising its plenary power to regulate the legal profession and to integrate the bar.

The Court also took into account the more than two years that had elapsed during which Edillon was barred from practicing his profession, the change in his attitude as evidenced by his later communications, and the mitigating personal circumstances he invoked. The ruling drew upon an established principle articulated by Justice Malcolm in *Villavicencio v. Lukban* (39 Phil. 778, 1919) regarding the inherent power to punish for contempt, which the SC extended to disciplinary authority over members of the bar: **the power to discipline, especially if amounting to disbarment, should be exercised on the preservative and not on the vindictive principle.** Subsequent jurisprudence had consistently applied this doctrine (People v. Estenzo, 64 SCRA 211; Fontelera v. Amores, 70 SCRA 37; Royeca v. Animas, 71 SCRA 1; Blancaflor v. Laya, 82 SCRA 148; Calo v. Tapucar, 88 SCRA 78).

## DOCTRINE

**The power to discipline a member of the bar, especially one that results in disbarment, must be exercised on the preservative rather than the vindictive principle.** Related to this are several key propositions established or reaffirmed in this case:

First, **membership in the bar is a privilege burdened with conditions.** Following Cardozo, the right to practice law is not an absolute right but a privilege that carries with it corresponding obligations. Failure to abide by any of these conditions may result in loss of the privilege if the gravity of the breach warrants such a drastic consequence.

Second, **there is no irretrievable finality to either admission to or loss of bar membership.** Just as no one has an absolute right to be admitted to the bar, no disbarment is necessarily permanent. After sufficient time has elapsed and the transgressor has demonstrated genuine contrition through his actuations, the restoration of the privilege may once again be considered.

Third, **the SC possesses full and plenary discretion to reinstate a disbarred member of the bar**, considering public interest, the integrity of the legal profession, and the welfare of the lawyer who has purged himself of guilt.

## NOTES

This case is best read alongside *In re: Integration of the Bar of the Philippines* (49 SCRA 22, 1973), which settled the constitutionality of compulsory IBP membership and dues — Edillon's disbarment rested squarely on that foundation. The decision is a useful template for understanding how the SC calibrates the preservative principle in reinstatement cases: attitude change, full compliance with prior obligations (here, payment of all delinquent dues), and the lapse of meaningful time are the practical markers the Court looks for. The extended citation chain to *Villavicencio v. Lukban* is worth remembering for bar exam purposes — whenever a question involves the *quantum* of disciplinary sanction or the philosophy behind reinstatement, this is the controlling doctrinal anchor.
