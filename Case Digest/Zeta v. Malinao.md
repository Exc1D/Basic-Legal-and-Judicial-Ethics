**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Unauthorized Practice of Law by a Court Employee; Grave Misconduct

> **Gist:** A court interpreter who represents parties in municipal courts and collects fees for it — competing with licensed counsel while falsifying his own time records to hide his absences — is guilty of illegal practice of law and grave misconduct, warranting dismissal from judicial service with a permanent bar on reemployment.

## FACTS

Felicisimo Malinao, a court interpreter, was administratively charged with illegal practice of law, grave misconduct, falsification of records, and violation of civil service regulations. The evidence — public records and the declarations of judges before whom he had appeared — showed that Malinao had been representing litigants in municipal courts, holding himself out as competent to handle their cases and collecting fees for doing so, in direct competition with licensed attorneys who were properly authorized to appear. To conceal his absences from his post while conducting this unauthorized practice, he falsified his own daily time records to show he had been present in court on days he was, in fact, elsewhere representing private clients.

## ISSUES

Whether a non-lawyer court employee's representation of litigants in municipal courts, for a fee, constitutes illegal practice of law and grave misconduct warranting dismissal from judicial service.

## RULING

The SC found the investigator's factual conclusions amply supported by the evidence and held Malinao liable for illegal practice of law and grave misconduct. As a non-lawyer, Malinao had no authority whatsoever to represent parties as counsel; doing so for compensation, repeatedly, and in competition with licensed attorneys, was squarely unauthorized practice of law. The falsification of his own time records to conceal the practice compounded the offense, since it was not an isolated lapse but a sustained, deliberate scheme carried out from within the judicial system he was supposed to serve. The Court ordered his dismissal from his position as court interpreter, with a bar against future reemployment anywhere in the judicial branch.

## DOCTRINE

**A non-lawyer court employee who represents litigants in court proceedings for a fee, in competition with licensed counsel, commits illegal practice of law and grave misconduct** — an offense made graver, not merely cumulative, when the employee also falsifies official records to conceal the unauthorized practice from their own employer.

## NOTES

One of the oldest cases in the vault's unauthorized-practice line, and useful precisely because it is the plainest fact pattern: no lawyer status claimed, no letterhead, no partnership — just a non-lawyer directly appearing for pay. Compare with [[Arienda v. Monilla]] (document preparation rather than appearance) and [[Cambaliza v. Cristal-Tenorio]] (holding out via letterhead and ID rather than direct appearance) for the range of ways the same underlying wrong can be committed.
