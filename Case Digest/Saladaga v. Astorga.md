
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 4697 (and A.C. No. 4728), November 25, 2014

**Ponente:** Leonardo-De Castro, J. (per secondary sources; confirm against the Supreme Court E-Library before citing in an exam answer)

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Dishonest Conduct Outside the Practice of Law; Penalty Calibration

> **Gist:** A lawyer's dishonest and deceitful dealing in a purely personal property transaction — not something done "as counsel" for anyone — still draws serious professional discipline, because a lawyer's fitness to practice is measured by conduct in general, not only by conduct performed while formally acting as counsel.

## FACTS

Florencio Saladaga and Atty. Arturo Astorga entered into a Deed of Sale with Right to Repurchase over a parcel of coconut land, under which Atty. Astorga, as vendor, represented that he held perfect title to the property, free of liens and encumbrances, and reserved a two-year period to repurchase it. Atty. Astorga did not exercise the right to repurchase within the stipulated period, and no renewal was made despite Saladaga's demand. It later emerged that Atty. Astorga had misrepresented the state of the title: the certificate of title he referenced had already been cancelled and transferred to his wife's name well before the sale, a fact he did not disclose to Saladaga.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer's dishonest misrepresentation in a private property transaction, unconnected to any attorney-client engagement, warrants professional discipline.

## RULING

The SC suspended Atty. Astorga from the practice of law for two years, adopting the IBP Board of Governors' recommendation. The Court held that misrepresenting the true status of the property's title — knowing it had already been cancelled and transferred — was unlawful, dishonest, and deceitful conduct in violation of the Code of Professional Responsibility, and a disregard of the Land Registration Act's provisions on registered title. That the transaction was a purely private dealing, with no attorney-client relationship involved, did not exempt Atty. Astorga from discipline: a lawyer's obligation to deal honestly is not confined to matters handled in a professional capacity.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer may be disciplined for dishonest or deceitful conduct even in a purely personal transaction unconnected to any attorney-client engagement.** Professional fitness to practice law is measured by a lawyer's conduct generally, not only by conduct performed while formally acting as counsel for a client.

## NOTES

A useful penalty-calibration anchor: two years of suspension for serious personal dishonesty involving property and title misrepresentation, without any courtroom appearance or client relationship at all. It also rebuts a common bar-exam trap — "I did this as a private individual, not as a lawyer, so it isn't lawyer misconduct" — which is squarely wrong.
