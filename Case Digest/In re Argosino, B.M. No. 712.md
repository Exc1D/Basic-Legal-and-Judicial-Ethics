## *Re: Petition of Al Argosino to Take the Lawyers Oath*

**Full Case Citation:** B.M. No. 712, March 19, 1997

**Ponente:** Padilla, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Admission to the Bar; Moral Character Requirement

> **Gist:** The SC allowed a bar passer who had previously been convicted of reckless imprudence resulting in homicide — arising from a fraternity hazing death — to take the lawyer's oath, holding that he had purged himself of the deficiency in moral character by his subsequent conduct, community service, and evidence of reformation, subject to a strong admonition on the weight of the lawyer's oath.

## FACTS

Petitioner Al Caparros Argosino passed the 1993 Bar Examinations. However, the SC deferred his oath-taking because of a prior conviction for **Reckless Imprudence Resulting in Homicide**. The conviction stemmed from the death of Raul Camaligan, a neophyte who died during fraternity initiation rites in September 1991. Petitioner and seven other accused initially pleaded not guilty to homicide charges, but later withdrew their pleas and, upon re-arraignment, all pleaded guilty to the lesser offense of reckless imprudence resulting in homicide. The trial court sentenced each of the accused to imprisonment ranging from two years, four months, and one day to four years. After the judgment, petitioner applied for and was granted probation on June 18, 1993, and was subsequently discharged from probation by order of the trial court dated April 11, 1994. On April 14, 1994, petitioner filed with the SC a petition to be allowed to take the lawyer's oath, anchored on his discharge from probation. In a resolution dated July 13, 1995, then Senior Associate Justice Florentino P. Feliciano required petitioner to submit evidence showing he had overcome the deficiency in his **moral character**. In compliance, petitioner submitted at least fifteen certifications and letters — including endorsements from two senators, five trial court judges, and six members of religious orders — and presented evidence that a scholarship foundation had been established in honor of the hazing victim through the joint efforts of Camaligan's family and the eight accused.

## ISSUES

The threshold issue was whether petitioner Al Argosino, having been convicted of reckless imprudence resulting in homicide in connection with a fatal fraternity hazing, had purged himself of the evident deficiency in **moral character** such that he could now be permitted to take the lawyer's oath, sign the Roll of Attorneys, and be admitted to the practice of law. A related consideration was the proper weight to be given to the sentiments of the victim's father, himself a lawyer, regarding petitioner's moral fitness for admission.

## RULING

The SC resolved to **allow** petitioner Al Caparros Argosino to take the lawyer's oath, sign the Roll of Attorneys, and thereafter practice the legal profession. The SC was persuaded that petitioner had exerted all efforts to atone for the death of Raul Camaligan and that he was not inherently of bad moral fiber; the various certifications demonstrated that he was a devout Catholic with genuine concern for civic duties and public service, including helping the more unfortunate members of his community. The SC also took judicial notice of the **general tendency of youth to be rash, temerarious, and uncalculating**, and was prepared to give petitioner the benefit of the doubt.

Nevertheless, the decision was reached only after a "very careful evaluation" of the competing considerations. The SC acknowledged the gravitas of the offense: the "senseless beatings" inflicted upon Raul Camaligan constituted an **evident absence of moral fitness** for admission to the bar, because such conduct was totally irresponsible, irrelevant, and uncalled for. The Court emphasized that the practice of law is a **privilege** extended only to those who possess the strict intellectual and moral qualifications required of lawyers, who are instruments in the effective and efficient administration of justice. The SC further underscored its sworn duty not only to weed out lawyers already disgracing the profession, but equally to **prevent "misfits" from taking the lawyer's oath** and further tarnishing the public image of the bar.

The SC also addressed the position of Atty. Gilbert Camaligan, the victim's father. While recognizing the deep pain of a parent who had lost a son — and praising the father's act of Christian forgiveness as no less than exceptional — the SC observed that Atty. Camaligan himself was "not in a position to state if petitioner is now morally fit to be a lawyer" and had accordingly left the matter to the sound discretion of the Court. That judicial discretion, the SC clarified, was the operative standard; the resolution of the issue required "weighing and reweighing" of the reasons for and against allowing admission.

Finally, in allowing petitioner to take the oath, the SC issued a pointed **admonition**: the lawyer's oath is *not* a mere ceremony or formality, and every lawyer must, at all times, weigh his or her actions according to the sworn promises made upon taking the oath. Compliance with the lawyer's oath and the **Code of Professional Responsibility**, the SC noted, would render the administration of justice faster, fairer, and easier for all concerned.

## DOCTRINE

**The practice of law is a privilege, not a right; admission to the bar requires not only intellectual competence but also good moral character, and the SC has both the power and the duty to defer or deny the oath-taking of any bar candidate found to be a "misfit" who would tarnish the public image of the legal profession.**

**Moral character deficiency arising from misconduct — including participation in reckless fraternity hazing that caused death — may be purged through subsequent conduct demonstrating reformation, accountability, civic-mindedness, and sincere atonement, such that the SC may, in the exercise of its sound discretion, allow the candidate to take the lawyer's oath.**

The lawyer's oath is not a mere formality; it constitutes a continuing set of sworn obligations under which every lawyer must, at all times, conduct himself or herself in accordance with the **Code of Professional Responsibility** and the promises made upon admission to the bar.

## NOTES

This case is a classic illustration of the SC's **equitable discretion** in bar admission matters: even where moral character has been impaired by conviction for a serious offense, full admission may follow upon convincing evidence of reformation, supported by credible endorsements and acts of atonement.

The ruling also reflects the SC's recognition of **judicial notice of youthful indiscretion** — "the general tendency of youth to be rash, temerarious and uncalculating" — a useful framing for similar cases involving bar applicants with prior convictions for acts committed during their minority or early adulthood.

The Court's deliberate engagement with the **victim's father, himself a lawyer**, underscores the deeply personal dimension of bar admission controversies and the standing of the aggrieved party to be heard, although the ultimate determination of moral fitness remains vested in the SC's sound discretion, not in any private person.
