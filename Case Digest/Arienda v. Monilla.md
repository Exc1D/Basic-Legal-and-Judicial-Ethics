
**A.M. No. P-11-2980 (Formerly OCA I.P.I. No. 08-3016-P) | June 10, 2013**

**Ponente:** Leonardo-De Castro, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Administrative Liability of Court Employees

> **Gist:** A court stenographer who prepared and finalized an extrajudicial settlement of estate for a private client despite not being a lawyer was found guilty of **simple misconduct**, and the SC imposed a fine equivalent to four months' salary to be deducted from her retirement benefits.

## FACTS

This is an administrative complaint for **conduct unbecoming a court employee** and **abuse of authority** filed by Leticia A. Arienda against Evelyn A. Monilla, Court Stenographer III of the RTC, Branch 4 of Legazpi City. In her letter-complaint dated October 8, 2008, complainant alleged that respondent, together with her husband Atty. Zaldy Monilla, offered their services in settling the estate of her deceased mother. The spouses Monilla represented that they would prepare an extrajudicial settlement while respondent's brother, a geodetic engineer, would conduct the survey of the property. Complainant issued six Temporary Receipts showing total payments of ₱49,800.00 to the spouses Monilla. When complainant later demanded the approved survey plan and the extrajudicial settlement, the spouses Monilla refused to release the documents until she paid the remaining ₱20,000.00 she owed. Complainant eventually discovered that respondent was merely a court stenographer and not a lawyer, and that her husband Atty. Monilla was employed at the DAR.

In her comment, respondent denied soliciting the engagement and instead claimed that complainant and her sister were the ones who approached her to mediate with her brother, Engr. Arquero, a geodetic engineer, for the partition of four lots. Respondent admitted, however, that despite knowing she was not a lawyer, she **prepared and finalized the extrajudicial settlement** of complainant's deceased mother's estate. She also admitted receiving ₱49,800.00 from complainant, of which ₱25,000.00 was supposed to cover the preparation of various documents (deeds of sale, extrajudicial settlement, contracts to sell, authorities to sell, and a demand letter) and ₱24,800.00 was for Engr. Arquero's surveying services. Respondent had already retired effective April 23, 2007 at the time the complaint was filed. The Office of the Court Administrator recommended that respondent be found guilty of simple misconduct with a fine equivalent to four months' salary to be deducted from her retirement benefits, which the SC ultimately adopted.

## ISSUES

At issue is whether respondent, a court stenographer who is not a lawyer, may be held administratively liable for preparing and finalizing an extrajudicial settlement of estate for a private individual and receiving compensation for such service. A related question is whether respondent's act constitutes **misconduct** that warrants the imposition of an administrative penalty.

## RULING

The SC found respondent **guilty of simple misconduct**. The Court emphasized that the preparation of an extrajudicial settlement of estate constitutes **practice of law**, citing *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]*, which defines practice of law as "any activity, in or out of court, which requires the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training and experience." Since respondent is not a lawyer, she had no authority to prepare and finalize the extrajudicial settlement, and being a court employee, she ought to have known that performing such a service was improper. Worse, respondent received money for these unauthorized services.

Even assuming the work was done pursuant to a private arrangement between respondent and complainant, the SC held that a court employee's conduct "must always be beyond reproach and circumscribed with the heavy burden of responsibility as to let her be free from any suspicion that may taint the judiciary." Court personnel are expected to exhibit the highest sense of honesty and integrity not only in the performance of their official duties but also in their personal and private dealings. The SC also dismissed respondent's attempt to introduce Civil Case No. 5287 (a case between complainant and a third party) as relevant, ruling that it had no bearing on the administrative matter at hand.

**Misconduct** was defined as "wrongful, unlawful conduct, motivated by a premeditated, obstinate or intentional purpose," and any transgression or deviation from the established norm, whether work-related or not, amounts to misconduct. The applicable penalty under **Section 52(B)(2) of the Revised Uniform Rules on Administrative Cases in the Civil Service** is suspension for one month and one day to six months. Taking into account that this was respondent's first offense and that she had served the judiciary for almost 16 years, the SC imposed the penalty of **a fine equivalent to four months' salary**, to be deducted from her retirement benefits, since respondent had already retired.

## DOCTRINE

A court employee who is not a lawyer commits **simple misconduct** when she prepares and finalizes an extrajudicial settlement of estate for a private client and receives compensation therefor. **Court personnel must maintain the highest standard of honesty and integrity not only in the performance of their official duties but also in their personal and private dealings, so as to preserve the good name and standing of the judiciary.** The preparation of an extrajudicial settlement falls squarely within the definition of practice of law under *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]* — any activity requiring the application of law, legal procedure, knowledge, training, and experience — and only those authorized to practice law may perform such acts.

## NOTES

This case is a useful reminder that **judicial ethics extends beyond official functions** — a court employee's private dealings are equally subject to the exacting standards imposed on the judiciary.

The ruling underscores that the **practice of law is not limited to courtroom appearances**; document preparation like extrajudicial settlements, which require the application of legal knowledge, is included.

Since respondent had already retired, the SC converted the suspension into a **fine deductible from retirement benefits** — an important practical consideration when respondent is no longer in government service.
