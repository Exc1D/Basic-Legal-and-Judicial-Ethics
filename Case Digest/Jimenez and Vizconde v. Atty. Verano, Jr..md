
**Full Case Citation:** Adm. Case No. 8108 (consolidated with Adm. Case No. 10299), July 15, 2014

**Ponente:** Sereno, C.J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Influence Peddling Beyond the Regular Courts; CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2 (Duty to Lead a Merit-Based Legal Practice)

> **Gist:** Counsel for the accused in a high-profile drug case personally lobbied the Secretary of Justice and prepared a release order on official DOJ letterhead without authority, hoping to expedite his clients' release. The Court held that the ban on influence peddling is not confined to the regular courts but extends to "all other venues in the justice sector," suspending him six months.

## FACTS

Richard S. Brodett and Joseph R. Tecson — reported in the media as the "Alabang Boys" — were accused by the PDEA of illegal sale and use of dangerous drugs. A Joint Inquest Resolution dated December 2, 2008 dropped the charges for lack of probable cause. Amid public controversy over the case (including allegations of a prosecutorial "cover-up"), the House Committee on Illegal Drugs held congressional hearings, during which it was revealed that Atty. Felisberto L. Verano, Jr. — counsel for the accused — had prepared a release order for his clients using the letterhead of the Department of Justice and the stationery of then-Secretary Raul Gonzales, without authority to do so.

Dante La Jimenez and Lauro G. Vizconde, founders of Volunteers Against Crime and Corruption (VACC), filed a disbarment complaint (Adm. Case No. 8108), arguing respondent had no authority to use DOJ letterhead. Atty. Oliver O. Lozano filed a similar complaint (Adm. Case No. 10299) on the ground that respondent's conduct disrespected legal processes; he later withdrew it, but the Court proceeded on the merits regardless, consolidating both cases.

At the IBP hearing, respondent admitted he personally went to see Secretary Gonzales at his office — with the parents of one of the accused — and discussed the case with him for roughly two hours, after the Undersecretary declined to act quickly enough. Respondent admitted preparing the draft release order specifically for the Secretary's signature, calling it an attempt to "make it easy" and "convenient" for the Secretary to sign should he agree with the appeal. Respondent argued that because the Secretary never actually signed the draft, it remained "a mere scrap of paper with no effect at all," and that he acted out of overzealous faith in his clients' innocence.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer's act of personally approaching an executive-branch official (rather than a court) and preparing an unauthorized draft order to expedite a pending matter constitutes a violation of the duty to rely on the merits of a cause, warranting discipline.

## RULING

Yes. The IBP Investigating Commissioner found respondent's own admissions established that he "fed" the draft order to the DOJ Secretary — highly irregular conduct tending to influence a public official — and found him guilty of violating **Canon 13** of the CPR, recommending a mere warning. The Supreme Court agreed with the finding of liability but found a warning "a mere slap on the wrist," imposing instead a **six-month suspension**.

The Court's key doctrinal move: Canon 13 (reliance on the merits of a cause, no impropriety that influences or appears to influence *the court*) is not textually limited to judicial proceedings. **"[O]ther provisions in the Code of Professional Responsibility likewise prohibit acts of influence-peddling not limited to the regular courts, but even in all other venues in the justice sector, where respect for the rule of law is at all times demanded from a member of the bar."** Respondent's approach to the DOJ Secretary — a prosecutorial, not judicial, authority — was squarely within this expanded reach. The Court also invoked Rule 1.02 (no activities that lessen confidence in the legal system) and Rule 15.06 (a lawyer shall not state or imply ability to influence any public official, tribunal, or legislative body), and found him guilty of violating **Rules 1.02 and 15.07**, in relation to Canon 13.

The Court expressly reaffirmed: **"The primary duty of lawyers is not to their clients but to the administration of justice… Any means, not honorable, fair and honest which is resorted to by the lawyer, even in the pursuit of his devotion to his client's cause, is condemnable and unethical."**

## DOCTRINE

**The duty to rely solely on the merits of a cause, and to avoid any act that gives the appearance of influence, is not confined to proceedings before a court — it extends to any venue in the justice sector, including executive agencies such as the Department of Justice, wherever respect for the rule of law is demanded of a lawyer.**

## NOTES

This case sits at the intersection of two CPRA Canon I duties. The syllabus places it under **Sec. 2** (merit-based practice) alongside [[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]] and [[Vantage Lighting Philippines v. Atty. Diño, Jr.]], and this note follows that placement as the primary home. But its facts — a lawyer personally lobbying a government office to intervene in a matter pending before another arm of that same agency (the inquest prosecutor) — are also a close doctrinal cousin of CPRA Canon I, **Sec. 4** ("a lawyer shall not assist or cause a branch, agency, office or officer of the government to interfere in any matter before any court, tribunal, or other government agency"). Treat this case as a bridge between the two duties: it is charged and decided as a Canon 13 (merit-based) violation, but its facts illustrate exactly the kind of agency-interference the CPRA's Sec. 4 was later drafted to name explicitly. See [[Canon I - Independence]] for the Sec. 4 discussion.

Of the three merit-based-practice cases in this vault, this is the mildest penalty (six months) — the respondent lobbied an executive official and drafted an unauthorized (never-executed) order, but did not offer money or threaten court personnel, unlike [[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]] (attempted bribery, two years) or [[Vantage Lighting Philippines v. Atty. Diño, Jr.]] (bribery claims plus retaliatory harassment suits, disbarment).
