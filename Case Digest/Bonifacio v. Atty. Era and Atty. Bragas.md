
**A.C. No. 11754, October 3, 2017**

**Ponente:** Associate Justice Noel Gimenez Tijam

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Code of Professional Responsibility; Unauthorized Practice of Law

> **Gist:** A lawyer who continues to perform acts characteristic of legal practice during his period of suspension commits willful disobedience of a lawful order of the SC, and a fellow lawyer who assists him in such unauthorized practice violates Canon 9 of the Code of Professional Responsibility.

## FACTS

This administrative case stemmed from a verified Affidavit-Complaint filed by Joaquin G. Bonifacio against Attys. Edgardo O. Era and Diane Karen B. Bragas for violation of the Code of Professional Responsibility (CPR). The antecedents trace back to an illegal dismissal case filed against Bonifacio and his company, Solid Engine Rebuilders Corporation, by a group of former employees (the Abucejo Group) before the NLRC, where Atty. Era and his law firm served as counsel for the complainants. The Labor Arbiter ruled against Bonifacio, ordering him to pay separation pay, full backwages, and pro-rated 13th month pay — a decision that survived all appeals up to the SC. Several writs of execution were thereafter issued to enforce the judgment, with the total award ballooning to ₱4,012,166.43 including interest and attorney's fees.

In a separate administrative case, *Samson v. Era* (A.C. No. 6664), the SC found ==Atty. Era guilty of representing conflicting interests== under Rule 15.03, Canon 15 and Canon 17 of the CPR, and **suspended him from the practice of law for two years** in a July 16, 2013 Decision.

==While serving this suspension, Atty. Era continued to act on behalf of the Abucejo Group in implementing the alias writ of execution.== On November 28, 2013, he attended the public auction over Bonifacio's corporate properties, tendered the winning bid for his clients, secured the certificate of sale, and presented it to the corporation's officers and employees. He then attempted to pull out the auctioned items but instead negotiated with Bonifacio's children in his law office, floating a counter-offer that escalated from ₱6 Million to ₱9 Million. When settlement talks failed, Attys. Era and Bragas returned on December 3, 2013 with their clients and several men, forcibly opening the establishment to remove the properties — an incident captured on video and which led to a criminal complaint for grave coercion against both attorneys.

Bonifacio then filed the instant administrative complaint on August 8, 2014. In their defense, Attys. Era and Bragas argued that Bonifacio lacked personal knowledge of the events since he was not personally present, and that ==Atty. Era was merely acting as attorney-in-fact under a Special Power of Attorney (SPA), not as a lawyer practicing law.== Atty. Bragas claimed she was simply representing the firm's clients as an associate.

The IBP Investigating Commissioner recommended dismissal for insufficiency of evidence, noting that Bonifacio's subsequent pleadings — including a Motion to Close and Terminate Case acknowledging full satisfaction of the judgment, and a Motion to Withdraw the criminal complaint — contradicted his allegations of unlawful enforcement. However, the IBP Board of Governors reversed and found Atty. Era guilty of unauthorized practice of law, suspending him for three years, and suspended Atty. Bragas for one month for assisting him. Both parties failed to file a motion for reconsideration, leading the SC to take final action.

## ISSUES

The SC framed two principal questions for resolution
- Whether Atty. Era engaged in the practice of law during the period of his suspension such that another disciplinary action was warranted. 
- Whether Atty. Bragas was guilty of directly or indirectly assisting Atty. Era in his unauthorized practice of law, justifying disciplinary action against her as well.

## RULING

The SC sustained the findings of the IBP Board of Governors on both counts.

On the first issue, the SC held that Atty. Era's acts unmistakably constituted the **practice of law** despite his invocation of the SPA as a shield. Drawing from *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]*, *Philippine Lawyers Association v. Agrava*, and *Noe-Lacsamana v. Bustamante*, the SC reiterated that practice of law encompasses not only court appearances but any activity, in or out of court, requiring the application of legal principles, legal procedure, specialized knowledge, training, and experience. It includes advising clients, preparing legal instruments, negotiating on their behalf in matters connected with the law, and performing acts characteristic of the legal profession. The SC emphasized that one customarily or habitually holds oneself out as a lawyer through acts indicative of that purpose — such as identifying oneself as an attorney, appearing in court for a client, or associating with a law office.

Applied to Atty. Era's conduct, the SC found that his participation went well beyond what any non-lawyer could perform. He appeared at the public auction on behalf of his winning clients, tendered their bid, obtained and presented the certificate of sale, asserted his clients' ownership over the properties, initiated the pull-out, and negotiated the settlement amount in his law office. Crucially, the SC pointed out that the SPA itself was executed precisely because Atty. Era was his clients' legal counsel — his clients had even stipulated that no amicable settlement could be entered into without Atty. Era's prior written consent. To allow the SPA to shield Atty. Era from the consequences of his suspension would, in the SC's view, **cunningly undermine the suspension order** itself, which the Court could not countenance. ==The SPA only authorized him to appear at the May 4, 2006 auction and to receive payment in the form of checks, neither of which covered the November 28, 2013 auction or the forcible pull-out of corporate properties.==

Since Atty. Era committed these acts during the two-year suspension imposed in *Samson v. Era*, his conduct amounted to **willful disobedience of a lawful order of the SC**, a ground for suspension or disbarment under Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court. The SC stressed that lawyers bear a graver responsibility than any other to uphold the integrity of the courts and respect their processes — a duty Atty. Era's "intentional maneuver to circumvent the suspension order" plainly betrayed. Citing *Molina v. Magat* and *Ibana-Andrade v. Paita-Moya*, where the SC had similarly disciplined lawyers who practiced despite subsisting suspension orders, the SC adopted the Board's recommendation of a **three-year suspension** for Atty. Era, taking into account that this was already his second infraction.

On the second issue, the SC found ==Atty. Bragas liable for **assisting in the unauthorized practice of law** in violation of **Canon 9** of the CPR, which mandates that a lawyer shall not, directly or indirectly, assist in the unauthorized practice of law.== Public interest and policy require that law practice be limited to individuals duly qualified in education and character, and every lawyer has a duty to prevent — or at the very least, not participate in — unauthorized practice. Atty. Bragas knew of Atty. Era's suspension yet accompanied him and their clients in forcibly opening Bonifacio's establishment. Being an associate in Atty. Era's law firm could not be used as a convenient cover; the facts showed that Atty. Bragas did not step in to replace Atty. Era as counsel but merely aided him as he continued performing all acts related to the labor case. The SC imposed a **one-month suspension** on her.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who continues to perform acts characteristic of legal practice during the period of his suspension from the Bar commits willful disobedience of a lawful order of the SC under Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court.** The practice of law is not confined to litigation or court appearances; it covers any activity, in or out of court, that requires the application of legal principles, procedure, knowledge, training, and experience — including advising clients, preparing legal instruments, negotiating settlements, and acting as an advocate in representative capacity. A lawyer holds himself out as such through acts like appearing for clients, identifying himself as an attorney, or associating with a law firm for the general practice of law.

A Special Power of Attorney granted by clients cannot serve as a device to circumvent a suspension order, especially when the very authority emanates from the lawyer's role as legal counsel and when the acts performed involve the application of legal training and judgment.

**A lawyer who knowingly assists a suspended lawyer in performing acts of law practice violates Canon 9 of the Code of Professional Responsibility**, which prohibits direct or indirect assistance in the unauthorized practice of law. Mere association in a law firm does not excuse a lawyer from this duty.

## NOTES

For bar review purposes, remember that *Samson v. Era* is the predicate suspension here — when a lawyer commits a second infraction while already serving a suspension, the penalty escalates significantly (three years on top of the existing two-year suspension).

The SPA defense is a recurring strategy among suspended lawyers — this case squarely rejects it, holding that the document's existence actually proves the lawyer was engaged in legal work since it was precisely the lawyer-client relationship that gave rise to the authority.

Atty. Bragas's one-month suspension may seem light, but Canon 9 violations are taken seriously because they undermine the integrity of the profession's gatekeeping function — even minimal assistance to a suspended lawyer can trigger liability.
