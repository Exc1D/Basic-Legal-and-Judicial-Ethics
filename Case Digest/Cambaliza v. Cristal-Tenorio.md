
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 6290, July 14, 2004

**Ponente:** Davide, Jr., C.J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Unauthorized Practice of Law; Assisting Non-Lawyers to Hold Out as Lawyers

> **Gist:** A lawyer who allows a non-lawyer spouse to be listed as a partner on the law office's letterhead and to carry an identification card styling him "Atty." — thereby holding him out to the public as authorized to practice — is guilty of assisting in the unauthorized practice of law, even without proof that the non-lawyer personally appeared in court.

## FACTS

Ana Marie Cambaliza, a former employee of the Cristal-Tenorio Law Office, filed a disbarment complaint against Atty. Ana Luz B. Cristal-Tenorio, charging her with deceit, gross immorality, and malpractice. Among the charges was that the respondent's husband, Felicisimo R. Tenorio, Jr. — who was not a lawyer — was listed as a partner on the law office's letterhead and carried an identification card identifying him as "Atty. Felicisimo Tenorio, Jr.," an ID that the respondent herself signed as chairperson of the office.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer violates the duty not to assist unauthorized practice of law by allowing a non-lawyer to be held out publicly — through letterhead and identification — as a member of the Bar and a partner in the firm, even without evidence that the non-lawyer personally appeared in court or signed pleadings.

## RULING

The SC found Atty. Cristal-Tenorio guilty of violating Canon 9 and Rule 9.01 of the then-governing Code of Professional Responsibility — the duty not to assist unauthorized practice, now anchored in [[Unauthorized Practice of Law|CPRA, Canon II, Sec. 35]] — and suspended her from the practice of law for six months. The Court explained that "holding out" a non-lawyer as authorized to practice does not require proof of an actual court appearance: publicly representing the person as a lawyer, whether through firm letterhead, an identification card, or any other public-facing device, is itself the harm the rule targets, because it deceives clients and the public into believing they are dealing with someone accountable to the Court's disciplinary authority when they are not.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who permits a non-lawyer to be held out to the public as a member of the Bar — through firm letterhead, business cards, identification, or similar representations — is guilty of assisting in the unauthorized practice of law**, whether or not the non-lawyer is shown to have personally appeared in court or signed a pleading. The harm the rule addresses is the public's inability to hold a non-lawyer accountable the way it can hold a lawyer accountable, and that harm exists the moment the false representation is made.

## NOTES

This case broadens the unauthorized-practice picture beyond "who filed the pleading" (the [[Gonzales, Jr. and Concepcion v. Attys. Rivera and Bangoy|Gonzales v. Rivera and Bangoy]] fact pattern) to "who is held out as a lawyer at all" — a letterhead or a business card can be enough. It is also a family-context illustration, alongside [[Bonifacio v. Atty. Era and Atty. Bragas]], of how a lawyer's closest professional and personal relationships are exactly where this duty is most often tested.
