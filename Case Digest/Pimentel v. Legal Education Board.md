
**Full Case Citation:** G.R. Nos. 230642 & 242954, September 10, 2019 (En Banc); Resolution on the motions for reconsideration, November 9, 2021

**Ponente:** En Banc (2019 Decision); Reyes, Jr., J. (2021 Resolution)

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Supervision and Control of the Legal Profession; Legal Education Board; Separation of Powers

> **Gist:** The SC drew the line between the State's power over legal education (exercised through the Legal Education Board under RA 7662) and the Court's own exclusive constitutional power over admission to the practice of law — striking down LEB issuances, including the PhiLSAT, that crossed from regulating law schools into deciding who may become a lawyer.

## FACTS

Eighteen petitioners — law professors, law students, and a law school dean among them — challenged the constitutionality of RA 7662 (the Legal Education Reform Act of 1993) and several issuances of the Legal Education Board (LEB), the executive agency the law created to supervise legal education. The most contested issuance was LEBMO No. 7-2016, which made the Philippine Law School Admission Test (PhiLSAT) a mandatory prerequisite for admission to any law school's basic law course, with a passing score operating as a strict cutoff. Petitioners argued that LEB's supervisory reach over legal education necessarily intruded on the Supreme Court's own exclusive constitutional authority over admission to the practice of law, the Integrated Bar, and legal assistance to the underprivileged, under Article VIII, Section 5(5) of the 1987 Constitution, and that PhiLSAT specifically violated institutional academic freedom.

## ISSUES

Whether RA 7662 and the LEB's issuances are unconstitutional for encroaching upon the Supreme Court's exclusive rule-making power over admission to the practice of law, and whether the PhiLSAT requirement in particular violates institutional academic freedom.

## RULING

The SC upheld LEB's general jurisdiction over legal education — including its power to prescribe minimum standards for law school admission and curricula under RA 7662, Sec. 7(c) and (e) — as a valid exercise of the State's traditional authority over education, distinct from the Court's power over the practice of law itself. However, the Court struck down as unconstitutional: (1) the mandatory continuing legal education requirement imposed on practicing lawyers who serve as law faculty (RA 7662, Sec. 2, 2nd par. and Sec. 7(h)), because continuing legal education of the Bar falls squarely within the Court's own turf; (2) the mandatory law-practice internship as a pre-graduation requirement (Sec. 7(g)); and (3) LEB's stated objective of producing "socially responsible" lawyers as a basis for regulation (Sec. 3(a)(2)) — all as encroachments on the Court's exclusive power to shape who becomes a lawyer and how. The Court also struck down the PhiLSAT as an unconstitutional imposition on institutional academic freedom, reasoning that making it an absolute cutoff for admission to any law school effectively let LEB — not the individual law school, and not the Supreme Court — decide who may even begin studying law. The Court later resolved motions for reconsideration in a 2021 Resolution, which revisited aspects of the 2019 ruling while maintaining that LEB may not invade the Court's own exclusive domain over the practice of law.

## DOCTRINE

**The State's power over legal education (exercised through the LEB under RA 7662) and the Supreme Court's constitutional power over admission to the practice of law (Art. VIII, Sec. 5(5)) are related but distinct.** The LEB may prescribe minimum standards for law schools and curricula, but any LEB issuance that functions as a gatekeeping requirement for who may become a lawyer — rather than merely regulating who may study law and how — crosses into the Court's exclusive constitutional domain and is void.

## NOTES

This case is why [[Admission to the Bar]] treats "completion of LEB-prescribed law courses" as a filter the Court chooses to use, not a concession that LEB shares the Court's admission power. Bar takers commonly conflate two different questions — "can LEB regulate law schools?" (yes) and "can LEB decide who becomes a lawyer?" (no) — and this case is the cleanest illustration of where that line falls. The PhiLSAT holding is also a useful academic-freedom illustration outside the pure legal-ethics context, and a reminder that LEB issuances are only as good as their fit within RA 7662's own delegated powers.
