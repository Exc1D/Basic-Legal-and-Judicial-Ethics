
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 11444 (Formerly CBD Case No. 17-5464), February 18, 2026

**Ponente:** Inting, J.

**Subject:** Legal Ethics — Notarial Practice; Suspension from the Practice of Law; Compliance with Disciplinary Penalties

> **Gist:** The SC denied the respondent's attempt to have his suspension period credited from an earlier date of his own choosing, ruling that the penalty of suspension from the practice of law takes effect only upon the lawyer's receipt of the SC's resolution imposing the penalty, and its lifting requires a sworn statement of compliance — not a unilateral declaration of voluntary cessation.

## FACTS

Andy Dela Victoria Canonoy and his five siblings, as heirs of their late mother Angelita Dela Victoria Canonoy, filed an administrative complaint against Atty. Jose B. Guibone for notarizing a falsified Extrajudicial Settlement and Partition of Estate dated September 22, 2015. The heirs claimed they never appeared before the respondent and that their signatures on the document were forged — an allegation supported by evidence showing they were scattered across different locations in the Philippines and abroad at the time of the alleged notarization.

Investigation further revealed a disturbing pattern of irregular notarial practice. Respondent maintained multiple notarial offices, permitted his staff to sign documents on his behalf, used multiple notarial seals, and notarized documents upon the mere presentation of a community tax certificate — practices squarely condemned under the 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice. Respondent himself admitted to these acts.

In its February 25, 2025 Resolution, the SC En Banc found Atty. Guibone guilty of violating the 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice and the Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (CPRA). For violations committed in bad faith, the SC imposed a one-year suspension from the practice of law, revocation of his notarial commission, disqualification from being commissioned as notary public for two years, and a fine of PHP 100,001.00. For making untruthful statements in an affidavit submitted as evidence in court, he received an additional one-year suspension. He received the resolution only on May 13, 2025.

Rather than accept the reckoning date set by law, Atty. Guibone filed a Manifestation on June 25, 2025, claiming he had voluntarily stopped practicing law on January 30, 2024, upon receiving a Notice of Resolution from the Integrated Bar of the Philippines (IBP). He asked to be allowed to resume practice on January 30, 2026. Complainant opposed this, contending that suspension should be reckoned from receipt of the SC's resolution, not an IBP resolution, which is merely recommendatory. Complainant also alleged that respondent had in fact continued practicing law during 2024 and 2025, including serving as Legal Unit Head and Regional Legal Officer of the Department of Education–CARAGA.

## ISSUES

At issue is whether the respondent's alleged voluntary cessation from the practice of law on January 30, 2024 — based on his receipt of an IBP resolution — may be credited as service of the penalty of suspension imposed by the SC in its February 25, 2025 Resolution.

## RULING

The SC denied the respondent's Manifestation. The Court anchored its ruling on two foundational principles of lawyer discipline under the Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability.

First, **suspension from the practice of law takes effect only upon the lawyer's receipt of the SC's decision or resolution imposing the penalty**. Citing *Re: Order dated 01 October 2015 in Criminal Case No. 15-318727-34 against Atty. Brillantes*, the SC reiterated that the reckoning date is anchored on receipt of the Court's own resolution, not on any earlier event chosen by the lawyer. Atty. Guibone's unilateral claim that he stopped practicing law on January 30, 2024 has no legal effect because no penalty had yet been imposed on him at that point, and his self-serving act of cessation cannot bind the Court.

Second, **IBP resolutions are merely recommendatory and do not attain finality absent action by the SC**. The Court explained, citing *Katipunan, Jr. v. Atty. Carrera*, that until the Court acts on an IBP recommendation and the lawyer is duly notified, no penalty of suspension may be deemed to have commenced. Respondent cannot, therefore, anchor his desired reckoning date on the IBP's notice.

Third, **the lifting of suspension is not automatic**. Drawing from *Brillantes*, the SC held that the lifting of suspension is effected only upon the lawyer's submission of a sworn statement of compliance to the proper offices, attesting that the full suspension period has been served and that the lawyer desisted from practice during its entire duration. The commencement and termination of suspension must be placed beyond the lawyer's control — a principle reinforced in *[[Calixto vs. Atty. Baleros|Calixto v. Atty. Balleros]]*, which stressed that lawyers may not benefit from their own acts or omissions to escape disciplinary consequences. The penalty's commencement may not be made dependent on the erring lawyer's convenience.

Applying these rules, the SC held that Atty. Guibone's suspension began only on May 13, 2025, the date he received the February 25, 2025 Resolution. Since he has not yet fully served the penalty — and has not filed a sworn statement of compliance — his Manifestation seeking to resume practice on January 30, 2026 lacks basis.

The Court also referenced *Valdez v. Atty. Hipe*, clarifying that while administrative suspension is lifted instantly upon filing of a sworn statement of compliance without need for further confirmation from the Court, any false statement made under oath in such compliance constitutes ground for a more severe penalty, or even disbarment. The Office of the Bar Confidant was tasked to carefully note and record these filings.

## DOCTRINE

**The penalty of suspension from the practice of law takes effect only upon the lawyer's receipt of the SC's decision or resolution imposing the penalty, and its lifting is effected only upon the lawyer's submission of a sworn statement of compliance attesting that the full period of suspension has been served and that the lawyer has desisted from the practice of law during its entire duration.** A lawyer's unilateral or voluntary cessation from practice does not constitute compliance with the penalty, and IBP resolutions — being merely recommendatory — cannot fix the commencement of suspension absent action by the Court. The commencement and termination of disciplinary penalties must be placed beyond the control of the erring lawyer and may not be made to depend on his or her convenience.

## NOTES

This case is a practical reminder that the **reckoning date of suspension is a matter of law, not a matter of lawyer choice** — a useful counter to attempts at "self-help" compliance that bar reviewers should watch out for. The juxtaposition with *Valdez v. Hipe* shows the Court's compromise position: while the lawyer controls the *end* of suspension (by filing the sworn statement), he cannot manipulate the *start*. Finally, the case ties together notarial misconduct, falsification in court submissions, and procedural compliance — illustrating how multiple CPRA violations can stack suspensions into a single disciplinary matrix.
