
**G.R. No. 89591-96, January 24, 2000**

**Ponente:** Davide, Jr., C.J. (on behalf of the First Division)

**Subject:** Criminal Law; Legal and Judicial Ethics — Right of Detention Prisoners to Practice Law

> **Gist:** A detention prisoner cannot practice law, and a trial court's order allowing a criminal accused to be detained outside a provincial jail — in the residence of the clerk of court — must yield when the underlying reason (threats to his life) no longer exists, especially when the accused has been abusing his privileged arrangement by acting as if he were a free man.

## FACTS

This case arose from Criminal Cases Nos. 3350-3355 pending before the Regional Trial Court (RTC), Branch 12, San Jose, Antique, where private respondent **Avelino T. Javellana** stood as one of the accused. In August 1989, the trial court issued an order dated August 8, 1989 placing Javellana under the custody of **Atty. Deogracias del Rosario**, then Clerk of Court of the same RTC, on the ground that sufficient reason existed why Javellana should not be detained at the Antique Provincial Jail due to perceived threats to his life. The order specifically directed that Javellana be held as a detention prisoner in del Rosario's residence and "not be allowed liberty to roam around."

The People of the Philippines elevated the matter to the Supreme Court, which rendered a decision on August 13, 1990 affirming the trial court's custody arrangement. Javellana moved for reconsideration, which the SC denied in a resolution dated September 8, 1999. What complicated matters was Javellana's non-compliance with the custody order — instead of being detained in del Rosario's residence, he went about his normal activities and continued practicing law. When Javellana sought to appear as counsel in Criminal Case No. 4262, the SC denied his motion in a July 30, 1990 resolution, ruling that a detention prisoner cannot appear as counsel. Despite this, Javellana continued accepting cases.

Adding to the procedural complexity, on April 7, 1997, Senior State Prosecutor Henrick F. Guingoyon filed a motion for clarification asking (1) whether the prohibition on Javellana's appearance as counsel was limited to Criminal Case No. 4262; (2) whether Atty. del Rosario — who had since been appointed judge — remained Javellana's custodian; and (3) whether Javellana should be considered a fugitive from justice. The SC merely "noted" this motion in June 1997. After reconsideration was denied in 1999, the trial court resumed hearings, and a motion was filed seeking revocation of the custody arrangement and Javellana's imprisonment at the provincial jail. Javellana then filed an urgent motion for clarification regarding whether the June 18, 1997 resolution had terminated the State Prosecutor's earlier motion.

## ISSUES

The principal questions for resolution were whether the trial court's August 8, 1989 order allowing Javellana to be detained outside the provincial jail should be maintained in light of changed circumstances, who should properly serve as Javellana's custodian in view of Atty. del Rosario's promotion to the judiciary, and whether Javellana — as a detention prisoner — could lawfully continue practicing law and appearing as counsel in cases.

## RULING

The SC set aside the August 8, 1989 order and ordered all accused in Criminal Cases Nos. 3350-3355, including Javellana and Arturo F. Pacificador, **detained at the Provincial Jail of Antique, San Jose, Antique, effective immediately**, and prohibited from going out except upon prior written permission of the trial court for a lawful purpose. Copies were directed to the Provincial Director of the PNP Antique Provincial Police Office and the Provincial Jail Warden to ensure compliance.

In so ruling, the SC emphasized that the perceived threats to Javellana's life no longer existed, rendering the basis for the special custody arrangement obsolete. Custody of an accused under the law carries with it the obligation to detain him in the proper facility — **actual restraint of liberty through imprisonment in jail** is the rule, and deviation is permissible only through bail or recognizance under **Rule 114, Section 1 of the 1985 Rules on Criminal Procedure**. Under **Rule 113, Sections 2 and 3**, an arrested person is deemed placed under the custody of the law. Because del Rosario had been promoted to judge, he ceased to be the personal custodian; the succeeding Clerk of Court would have assumed the same undertaking, but the SC found the entire arrangement no longer tenable.

On the practice of law issue, the SC held unequivocally that **detention prisoners are not allowed to practice their profession**. The privilege of holding oneself out as a lawyer necessarily requires the freedom to appear before courts, confer with clients, and travel — liberties incompatible with detention status. The Court clarified that the earlier July 30, 1990 prohibition against Javellana appearing as counsel applied not only to Criminal Case No. 4262 but to **all other cases as well**, with the sole exception of appearances where Javellana himself is the accused defending his own liberty. Reading the trial court's August 8, 1989 order in its proper context — that Javellana "is not to be allowed liberty to roam around but is to be held as a detention prisoner" — the SC concluded that the prohibition on practicing law was already implicit in the very terms of his detention.

Finally, recognizing that the criminal cases had dragged on for more than ten years, the SC directed the presiding judge of RTC Branch 12 to proceed with the trial with all deliberate dispatch and to avoid further delay.

## DOCTRINE

**As a necessary consequence of arrest and detention, all prisoners — whether under preventive detention or serving final sentence — cannot practice their profession, engage in any business or occupation, or hold any office, whether elective or appointive, while in detention.** A detention prisoner enjoys none of the freedoms ordinarily required to discharge the duties of a lawyer or other professional, and any authority to practice is automatically suspended the moment the lawyer is placed under custodial restraint. When a person indicted for an offense is arrested, he is **deemed placed under the custody of the law** and must be detained in jail during the pendency of the case against him, unless released on bail or recognizance. The status of detention prisoner is thus incompatible with the unfettered practice of law.

## NOTES

This case is frequently cited for the **categorical rule that detention prisoners are barred from practicing their profession** — a doctrine that flows from the broader principle that detention involves actual restraint of liberty incompatible with professional engagements. It also illustrates how **extrajudicial custody arrangements** (such as house detention) require a continuing factual basis; once the threat justifying the deviation disappears, the default rule — detention in a regular penal facility — reasserts itself. Notably, the SC's ruling that the ban on practice covers all cases and not just Criminal Case No. 4262 eliminates a common loophole argument raised by detained lawyers seeking to circumvent the prohibition.
