
**Full Case Citation: A.C. No. 10261, July 16, 2019**

**Ponente:** Per Curiam

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Disbarment; Code of Professional Responsibility

> **Gist:** The SC disbarred Atty. Manuel V. Mendoza for flip-flopping on sworn statements before courts, using intemperate language against a party, ignoring mandatory pleading requirements, and demonstrating a pattern of misconduct that showed he was unfit to remain in the legal profession.

## FACTS

Rufina Luy Lim, the surviving spouse of Pastor Y. Lim who died on June 11, 1994, filed a disbarment complaint against Atty. Manuel V. Mendoza for multiple violations of the Code of Professional Responsibility (CPR) and Section 20, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court. Rufina claimed that during his lifetime, Pastor organized several dummy corporations — including Skyline International, Inc. and Nell Mart, Inc. — using his mistresses and employees as incorporators and stockholders in order to defeat her claims over conjugal properties. After Pastor's death, Rufina filed a Joint Petition for the settlement of his estate before the RTC of Quezon City. Miguel Lim, brother of Pastor, filed a Petition for Intervention in 1995 expressly declaring under oath that the corporations were dummies created by Pastor and that the purported incorporators and stockholders were mere figureheads.

Atty. Mendoza notarized that Petition for Intervention as well as the supporting affidavits of Teresa T. Lim, Lani G. Wenceslao, Susan Sarcia-Sabado, and Miguel, all of whom admitted that they never paid a single centavo for the shares attributed to them and had no actual participation in the corporations' operations. The trouble began when Atty. Mendoza later took on a completely contradictory position: he became counsel for Skyline and argued that the company was a legitimate registered owner of the properties, even claiming it was entitled to protect its interests against Rufina — the very person whose cause the earlier notarized documents had supported. He also acted as Vice-President of Nell Mart and demanded that tenants vacate properties covered by certain TCTs, all while allegedly knowing the corporation was a dummy. On top of this, Rufina accused him of using intemperate language in his pleadings, accusing her of collecting "BILLIONS OF PESOS" in rent that were "DISSIPATED ON HER GAMBLING VICES."

Atty. Mendoza defended himself by claiming the 1972 Agreement between Rufina and Pastor effectively partitioned their conjugal properties, and that the RTC had already excluded the bank deposits of Nell Mart from the estate. He argued the Petition for Intervention was merely a "pre-arranged" document between Rufina and Miguel, making its statements hearsay since both Miguel and his mother Yao Hiong had died before testifying. He also characterized the disbarment case as retaliation for Rufina's losses in related cases.

## ISSUES

The principal question is whether Atty. Mendoza violated the CPR and is deserving of disciplinary sanction — specifically whether his contradictory sworn positions before different courts constitute falsehood and lack of candor in violation of Canon 10 and Rule 10.01, whether his use of derogatory language against Rufina breached the standards of conduct expected of lawyers, whether his ignorance or disregard of basic legal principles warrants sanction, and whether the totality of his misconduct — compounded by a prior disciplinary offense — justifies the ultimate penalty of disbarment.

## RULING

The SC adopted the findings of the IBP Board of Governors but went further, imposing **disbarment** instead of the recommended two-year suspension because Atty. Mendoza had previously been sanctioned in *Sosa v. Atty. Mendoza* (756 Phil. 490 [2015]) for violation of Rule 1.01 of the CPR arising from his willful failure to pay a ₱500,000.00 loan, where he had been suspended for one year with a stern warning that a repetition would bring a heavier penalty.

On the core issue of candor, the SC found that Atty. Mendoza's **flip-flopping positions** betrayed a glaring lack of forthrightness. He had personally signed and notarized the Petition for Intervention and supporting affidavits declaring the corporations as dummies, then later claimed the corporations were legitimate and even acquired shares in them as alleged payment for attorney's fees. Since both positions could not be true simultaneously, the SC concluded he had been "less than truthful in at least one occasion," and this the Court could not countenance. Under Canon 10 of the CPR and Rule 10.01, a lawyer is enjoined from doing any falsehood, consenting to any falsehood in court, or misleading the court through any artifice. **Candor before the courts is non-negotiable**; any deception, whether direct or by indirection, constitutes misconduct meriting disciplinary action.

The SC also rejected Atty. Mendoza's attempt to disclaim the Petition for Intervention on the ground that it was a "pre-arranged" document. A counsel's signature on a pleading is not an empty formality — it is a **positive declaration that the lawyer has read the pleading, that there is ground to support it, and that it is not interposed for delay**. By signing the document, Atty. Mendoza affirmed its contents and could not later assail them without admitting he had misled the court. His position that the corporations were dummies, then his reversal, demonstrated either ignorance of the law or wanton disregard for it — both equally deserving of sanction, especially since Canon 5 requires lawyers to keep abreast of legal developments.

The SC further held that Atty. Mendoza's assertion that the May 11, 1972 Agreement waiving future support and property claims was "binding against third persons" revealed a fundamental misunderstanding of the law, since such an agreement cannot prejudice persons who are not parties to it. His imputation that Rufina had "DISSIPATED [billions of pesos] ON HER GAMBLING VICES" was likewise condemned as **intemperate, abusive, and unbecoming** of a member of the Bar. The CPR directs lawyers to use language that is emphatic but respectful, convincing but not derogatory — language befitting one gentleman addressing another. Finally, his failure to indicate his Professional Tax Receipt Number, IBP Receipt, Roll of Attorneys Number, and MCLE compliance in his Position Paper violated Bar Matter Nos. 1132 and 1922 — requirements that ensure only qualified individuals engage in legal practice and that are not to be treated as empty formalities.

## DOCTRINE

**Lawyers, as officers of the court, are expected to act with complete candor in all their dealings; they must not resort to deception through artifice or guile, and any act that obstructs the administration of justice constitutes misconduct warranting disciplinary action.** A lawyer's signature on a pleading is a positive certification of its truth and proper basis — not an empty formality — and a lawyer who signs a pleading cannot later disavow its contents without admitting that he misled the court. **Membership in the Bar is a privilege burdened with conditions, granted only to those who possess the strict intellectual and moral qualifications demanded of officers of the court**, and lawyers must maintain not only legal proficiency but also a high standard of morality, honesty, integrity, and fair dealing at all times. **Repeated offenses and a pattern of misconduct are themselves indicative of a lawyer's unfitness to remain in the profession**, justifying the imposition of progressively severe penalties up to disbarment.

## NOTES

This case is a textbook illustration of how **inconsistent sworn statements** before different tribunals can constitute falsehood under Rule 10.01 of the CPR — a useful reminder that lawyers cannot simply "switch sides" on factual assertions without consequence. It also reinforces that even pending administrative complaints and Bar Matter requirements (like including roll numbers and MCLE compliance in pleadings) are taken seriously as ethical violations. Pair this with *Sosa v. Mendoza* (the prior case against the same lawyer) to see how the SC escalates penalties for recidivist misconduct — a recurring theme in Philippine disciplinary jurisprudence where disbarment becomes appropriate when lesser sanctions have failed to correct a lawyer's behavior.
