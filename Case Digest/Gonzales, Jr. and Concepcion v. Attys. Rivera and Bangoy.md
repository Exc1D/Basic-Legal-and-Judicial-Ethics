
**A.C. No. 10627 (from A.C. No. 6622), April 3, 2024**

**Ponente:** Kho, Jr., J.

**Subject:** Legal Ethics — Unauthorized Practice of Law; Gross Misconduct and Insubordination

> **Gist:** A lawyer who knowingly co-signs pleadings with a disbarred attorney is equally guilty of unauthorized practice of law and may be suspended, while a lawyer's repeated defiance of SC directives — even when not liable for the substantive charge — constitutes gross misconduct warranting a substantial fine.

## FACTS

This administrative case arose from a Letter-Request filed by complainants Reno R. Gonzales, Jr. and Robin Bryan F. Concepcion of Tan Venturaza and Valdez Law Office, which the SC treated as a complaint against Attys. Socrates Rivera and Cres Dan Bangoy for violation of Canon 9 of the Code of Professional Responsibility (CPR), now the **Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (CPRA)**. The backdrop involves Atty. Bede S. Tabalingcos, who was disbarred by the SC in July 2012 in *Villatuya v. Atty. Tabalingcos* for engaging in bigamy. Despite the finality of his disbarment by October 2012, Tabalingcos continued practicing law — specifically by filing motions in an administrative case before the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP), docketed as OSI-AC No. 2012-027.

The complaint centered on two specific acts. First, **Atty. Cres Dan Bangoy** co-signed as counsel a Motion for Extension of Time to File Memorandum dated October 18, 2012, in the BSP case under the Tabalingcos and Associates Law Office, together with the disbarred Tabalingcos. Bangoy claimed he had already left the firm in August 2012 upon learning of the disbarment decision, but his signature on the October 2012 motion contradicted this. Second, **Atty. Socrates Rivera** signed a Notice of Change of Address dated December 2, 2013 as co-counsel of Tabalingcos and Associates Law Office in the same BSP case. Complainants argued that by signing pleadings under the firm's name, respondents held Tabalingcos out to the public as still authorized to practice law.

Bangoy filed his Comment denying knowledge of or participation in Tabalingcos's unauthorized practice, while Rivera completely ignored the proceedings. Despite multiple resolutions requiring him to file his Comment — and a show-cause order — Rivera never complied. The SC eventually imposed a PHP 1,000.00 fine on Rivera in November 2020 for his defiance, which he likewise never paid. Notably, Rivera had a string of prior administrative cases against him: a one-year suspension in *Petelo v. Rivera*, a disbarment in *Reyes v. Rivera* (A.C. No. 9114) for misrepresentation, and a PHP 100,000.00 fine in *Professional Services, Inc. v. Rivera* (A.C. No. 11241) for defrauding a client.

## ISSUES

The sole issue for resolution is whether grounds exist to hold respondents administrably liable. This breaks down into two sub-questions: whether Bangoy and Rivera assisted in Tabalingcos's unauthorized practice of law by signing pleadings under the disbarred lawyer's firm name, and whether Rivera's repeated failure to comply with the SC's directives independently warrants administrative sanction.

## RULING

The SC found both respondents guilty, though on different grounds. Applying the **CPRA**, which took effect on May 30, 2023 and applies to pending cases under Section 1 of its General Provisions, the SC ruled that **Bangoy is administratively liable for assisting in unauthorized practice of law**. The SC emphasized that the practice of law implies customarily or habitually holding oneself out to the public as a lawyer for compensation — covering not just courtroom appearances but also acts like preparing pleadings and associating oneself as a partner in a law office. Bangoy's defense that he had already separated from the firm was unpersuasive; his own Comment admitted he knew of the disbarment weeks before he supposedly left, yet he still co-signed the motion with Tabalingcos two months after learning of the disbarment. By doing so, he knowingly held out to the public that both he and Tabalingcos were members of the Philippine Bar in good standing authorized to represent the client. Following the precedent in *Cambaliza v. Cristal-Tenorio*, *Noe-Lacsamana v. Bustamante*, and *Judge Laguindanum v. Quintana* — where lawyers who allowed non-lawyers or disbarred persons to be associated with their practices were suspended for six months — the SC imposed the same six-month suspension on Bangoy.

**Rivera, however, was acquitted of the charge of assisting in unauthorized practice of law.** The SC distinguished his act of merely signing a Notice of Change of Address as co-counsel of record in the BSP case. Rivera had no authority to remove Tabalingcos and Associates Law Office as counsel of record, and including the firm name in a notice to state a matter of record did not constitute active misrepresentation. Moreover, a law firm has a separate juridical personality from its name partners; when a client engages a law firm, the firm itself is the counsel of record, not the individual members. Unlike in *Cambaliza* and *Plus Builders, Inc. v. Revilla* — where respondents listed non-lawyers as partners or held themselves out as partners of unlicensed entities — Rivera's act was a passive, ministerial act that did not amount to aiding and abetting.

Despite this acquittal on the substantive charge, the SC held Rivera guilty of **gross misconduct and insubordination** for his repeated defiance of the SC's directives. His failure to file a Comment despite multiple orders, his failure to comply with the show-cause resolution, and his failure to pay the PHP 1,000.00 fine — all of which remained unfulfilled to this day — constituted inexcusable, shameful, and unlawful conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice. Under Canon VI, Section 34(c) of the CPRA, willful and deliberate disobedience of SC orders is a less serious offense, but given Rivera's **propensity to violate the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]]** as shown by his three prior administrative cases, the SC imposed a PHP 50,000.00 fine and reiterated the unpaid PHP 1,000.00 fine. Pursuant to Section 42, Canon VI of the CPRA, since Rivera had already been previously disbarred, the penalty of disbarment itself could no longer be imposed; instead, the penalties are recorded in his personal file at the Office of the Bar Confidant to be considered should he ever apply for judicial clemency.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who knowingly and willfully assists in the unauthorized practice of law — whether by co-signing pleadings with a disbarred attorney, allowing non-lawyers to be held out as partners, or holding oneself out as a partner of an unlicensed entity — is guilty of violating the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]] and the CPRA/CPR and is subject to suspension or disbarment.** The practice of law is a high personal privilege limited to those of good moral character with special educational qualifications, and it is every lawyer's duty to prevent, or at the very least not assist in, the unauthorized practice of law by others. Assisting and abetting such unauthorized practice manifests a lack of respect for the law and dishonesty deserving of severe punishment.

Equally important, **a lawyer's willful and deliberate failure to comply with lawful orders and directives of the SC — including orders to file a Comment, show-cause resolutions, and orders to pay fines — constitutes gross misconduct and insubordination**, which alone can merit the penalty of disbarment or, where the lawyer has already been previously disbarred, a substantial fine recorded in his personal file for future consideration upon any application for reinstatement.

## NOTES

This case is significant because it draws a clear line between *active assistance* in unauthorized practice (like Bangoy's co-signing of pleadings with a disbarred lawyer, which warrants suspension) and *passive association* (like Rivera's mere signing of a notice as co-counsel of record, which does not). For bar review, remember the rule that a **law firm has a separate juridical personality from its name partners** — a disbarred name partner does not automatically render the entire firm unauthorized to practice. Also worth noting: Rivera's case is a textbook example of how accumulated administrative infractions can compound into severe financial penalties even when the substantive charge fails, because insubordination alone is enough to warrant the highest sanctions under Canon VI of the CPRA.
