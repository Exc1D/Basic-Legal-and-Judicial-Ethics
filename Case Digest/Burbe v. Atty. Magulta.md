
**Full Case Citation: AC No. 99-634, June 10, 2002**

**Ponente:** Panganiban, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Disciplinary Action Against Lawyers

> **Gist:** A lawyer who misappropriates the filing fee entrusted by his client is guilty of violating Rules 16.01 (holding client funds in trust) and 18.03 (diligence in handling cases) of the Code of Professional Responsibility, warranting suspension from the practice of law for one year, regardless of whether the lawyer was ultimately paid his fees or later refunded the amount.

## FACTS

Dominador P. Burbe, a businessman, engaged the services of Atty. Alberto C. Magulta of the Respicio, Magulta and Adan Law Offices in Davao City sometime in September 1998 to represent him in a money claim arising from breach of contract against Regwill Industries, Inc. Atty. Magulta drafted the demand letter, prepared other legal papers, and eventually drafted the complaint itself for filing. On January 4, 1999, Burbe deposited P25,000.00 with the law firm for the filing fee of the case, for which the office issued a receipt. The lawyer then repeatedly assured his client that the complaint had been filed, even bringing Burbe to the Hall of Justice in Davao City to make it appear that he was following up with the Clerk of Court. When Burbe personally verified with the Clerk of Court, he received a Certification dated May 27, 1999 stating that no case had been filed whatsoever. Confronted with this, Atty. Magulta finally admitted that he had spent the P25,000.00 for his own purposes. He issued two postdated checks totaling P20,000.00 as partial reimbursement. Burbe filed a disbarment complaint with the IBP Commission on Bar Discipline on June 14, 1999, alleging **misrepresentation, dishonesty, and oppressive conduct**.

In his defense, Atty. Magulta argued that no attorney-client relationship existed because Burbe never paid him any fee — that his services were merely a personal favor to a *kumpadre* of one of his law partners. He claimed that the P25,000.00 was meant as an acceptance fee, not a filing fee, and that the receipt was erroneously issued. He further argued that Burbe had actually instructed him to suspend the filing of the case in February 1999 pending negotiations with another company, and that his refusal to file the case without receiving his full legal fees (which he claimed amounted to P187,742.00 since the claim was almost P4 million) was justified.

The IBP Commission on Bar Discipline found the explanation unconvincing and recommended suspension for one year, a recommendation the SC adopted.

## ISSUES

The threshold issue was whether an attorney-client relationship existed between Atty. Magulta and Burbe, considering that no legal fees had been paid. Assuming such a relationship existed, the next issue was whether the lawyer's failure to file the complaint and his appropriation of the P25,000.00 entrusted to him constituted professional misconduct warranting disciplinary action.

## RULING

The SC found Atty. Magulta guilty of violating **Rules 16.01 and 18.03 of the Code of Professional Responsibility** and suspended him from the practice of law for one year.

On the existence of an attorney-client relationship, the SC firmly rejected the argument that the relationship must depend on the payment of fees. Citing *Hilado v. David* (84 Phil. 569, 1949), the SC held that to constitute professional employment, it is not essential that the client employed the attorney on any previous occasion, nor is it necessary that any retainer be paid, promised, or charged. What matters is whether the person consulted the lawyer with a view to obtaining professional advice or assistance, and whether the attorney voluntarily acquiesced to such consultation. Once Atty. Magulta agreed to prepare the complaint and actually drafted it, the professional employment was established — regardless of the kumpadre connection, the personal favor characterization, or the absence of fees paid.

On the **misappropriation of funds**, the SC applied two interrelated CPR provisions. **Rule 16.01** requires lawyers to hold in trust all moneys and properties of clients that may come into their possession, while **Rule 18.03** prohibits neglect of legal matters entrusted to them. The SC noted that the receipt issued by the law office itself belied Atty. Magulta's claim that the money was meant for acceptance fees — it was "quite incredible" for office personnel to be prevailed upon to issue an erroneous receipt, and the lawyer's failure to immediately correct the alleged error further undermined his credibility. The conversion of client funds into the lawyer's personal use constitutes a betrayal of public confidence in the legal profession, the SC held, citing *Sipin-Nabor v. Baterina* and *Gonato v. Adaza*. The subsequent reimbursement did not exculpate the lawyer from his breach of duty.

Crucially, the SC rejected the lawyer's invocation of his **attorney's lien** — the right to retain client funds until lawful fees are paid. While such a lien exists, it does not relieve lawyers of their duty to account promptly for moneys received, and it certainly does not authorize the conversion of funds clearly designated for a specific purpose, such as a filing fee. The SC likewise reminded the bar that lawyering is a profession, not a business: **duty to public service**, not the gaining of livelihood, must be the primary consideration.

However, the SC declined to impose the extreme penalty of **disbarment**, emphasizing that this sanction must be reserved for clear cases of misconduct that seriously affect the standing and character of the bar, citing *Montano v. Integrated Bar of the Philippines*.

## DOCTRINE

The SC laid down two interrelated doctrines that every law student should internalize:

**First, an attorney-client relationship is established by the act of seeking and voluntarily rendering legal advice, regardless of whether fees are paid or have been previously arranged.** This relationship springs from the consultation itself, and the lawyer is bound by the same duties of fidelity and diligence owed in any professional engagement. As the SC poetically put it, once lawyers agree to take up the cause of a client, **they owe fidelity to such cause and must always be mindful of the trust and confidence reposed in them.**

**Second, the practice of law is a profession, not a business.** Duty to public service and to the administration of justice should be the primary consideration of lawyers; emolument is merely a byproduct. This means that lawyers must subordinate personal gain to client welfare — a principle that directly undergirds the duty to hold client funds in trust under Rule 16.01 and to handle cases with diligence under Rule 18.03.

Together, these doctrines establish that **misappropriation of client funds, even if later reimbursed, constitutes professional misconduct** — the breach of trust has already occurred and the damage to the public's confidence in the bar cannot be undone by mere repayment.

## NOTES

Note that despite the lawyer's claim that no fees were paid and that the kumpadre relationship diminished his obligation, the SC treated the relationship as fully professional — this is a common misconception that often leads unsuspecting lawyers into disciplinary trouble. Students preparing for the bar should remember that the formation of an attorney-client relationship does not turn on payment but on the consultation and voluntary acceptance of the professional engagement.

Also worth remembering: partial reimbursement or even full restitution does not automatically wipe out the disciplinary liability for misappropriation. The SC treats the breach of trust as the actionable offense, not merely the financial loss to the client — which is why this case remains a frequently cited authority in actions involving conversion of client funds.
