
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 6593, February 4, 2010

**Ponente:** Puno, C.J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Good Moral Character as a Continuing Requirement; Gross Immorality; Disbarment

> **Gist:** Good moral character is both a condition precedent to admission and a continuing requirement for remaining a lawyer; a pattern of gross immoral conduct — here, a bigamous-in-substance relationship sustained over decades — warrants disbarment regardless of how much time has passed between the misconduct and the complaint.

## FACTS

Maelotisea Garrido, married to Atty. Angel Garrido since 1962, filed a disbarment complaint against her husband and against Atty. Romana Valencia after discovering that Atty. Garrido had been maintaining a long-term relationship with Atty. Valencia — with whom he had children — while still legally married to Maelotisea. The relationship and the resulting second family had continued for years, and both respondents were themselves members of the Bar.

## ISSUES

Whether the respondents' conduct — a lawyer maintaining a bigamous-in-substance relationship with another lawyer, sustained over a long period — constitutes gross immorality warranting disbarment, and whether the passage of time between the immoral acts and the filing of the complaint affects the respondents' present qualification to remain in the profession.

## RULING

The SC disbarred both respondents. The Court explained that good moral character is not a box checked once at admission; it is a continuing requirement a lawyer must possess for as long as membership in the Bar lasts. The time elapsed between the immoral conduct and the filing of a complaint does not cure or forgive it, because the relevant question is not when the wrong occurred but whether the lawyer currently possesses the moral fitness the profession demands. The Court distinguished ordinary "immoral" conduct from "grossly immoral" conduct — the latter being willful, flagrant, or shameless conduct showing moral indifference to the opinion of respectable members of the community, and so corrupt or unprincipled as to be reprehensible to a high degree — and found the respondents' sustained, public, bigamous-in-substance relationship squarely within the graver category.

## DOCTRINE

**Good moral character is both a condition precedent to admission and a continuing requirement for retaining membership in the legal profession.** The lapse of time between the commission of immoral acts and the filing of a disciplinary complaint does not excuse the conduct, because continuing fitness — not historical timing — is the measure. Disbarment is reserved for conduct that is grossly immoral: willful, flagrant, shameless, and reflecting moral indifference to the opinion of the community.

## NOTES

This case supplies the doctrinal backbone for the "continuing requirement" half of the good-moral-character rule already stated in [[Admission to the Bar]]. Pair it with [[In re Argosino, B.M. No. 712]] for the other half of the same doctrine: Argosino shows the Court's willingness to forgive and rehabilitate before admission, while Garrido shows the Court's willingness to strip the privilege after admission once gross immorality is proven, however long ago the conduct began.
