
**A.M. No. MTJ-02-1459 | October 14, 2003**

**Ponente:** Panganiban, J.

**Subject:** Judicial and Legal Ethics; Administrative Law

> **Gist:** A judge who fails to resolve a motion for preliminary injunction in a forcible entry case within the 30-day period mandated by Section 15, Rule 70 of the Rules of Court is guilty of gross inefficiency, while a court employee who appears as counsel for a co-plaintiff — but not for herself — commits an act warranting administrative sanction.

## FACTS

Imelda Y. Maderada, the clerk of court of the 12th Municipal Circuit Trial Court (MCTC) of Cabatuan and Maasin, Iloilo, filed an administrative complaint against Judge Ernesto H. Mediodea for gross ignorance of the law amounting to grave misconduct. The complaint arose from his handling of Civil Case No. 252, a forcible entry action governed by the Rule on Summary Procedure, which Maderada herself had initiated on September 7, 2001, against several defendants. Because Maderada was a court employee in the same sala, the presiding judge inhibited herself, and Executive Judge Tito Gustilo designated Judge Mediodea to hear the case.

Maderada filed a prayer for preliminary injunction, a TRO, and damages. Judge Mediodea ordered the defendants to show cause, scheduled hearings on September 21 and 28, 2001, and entertained various motions, including a Motion to Disqualify Maderada from appearing as counsel for her co-plaintiff, which he denied. Maderada herself filed three Motions for Rendition of Judgment, all of which were likewise denied. Despite the mandatory 30-day period under the Rules for resolving the motion for preliminary injunction, neither that motion nor the main case for forcible entry had been resolved even after four months from filing. Judge Mediodea eventually inhibited himself, and the case was reassigned to another judge.

In his defense, Judge Mediodea argued that the delay was justified because he was duty-bound to pass upon every motion, that Maderada had failed to present the evidence necessary for resolving the injunction plea, and that she had not exhausted available remedies before filing administrative charges. Maderada, for her part, was charged by the Office of the Court Administrator (OCA) with moonlighting for appearing in court as counsel for her co-plaintiff without prior authority from the SC. The OCA recommended a ₱1,000 fine against each party.

## ISSUES

The threshold question is whether Judge Mediodea is administratively liable for failing to decide the motion for preliminary injunction and the main case within the periods fixed by the Rules of Court, and whether his professed duty to rule on every motion excused the delay. A second issue, arising from the OCA's investigation, is whether Maderada — a court employee — may be held liable for engaging in the private practice of law when she appeared personally and on behalf of her co-plaintiff in the ejectment case without securing authority from the SC.

## RULING

The SC found Judge Mediodea guilty of **gross inefficiency** and modified the OCA's recommended fine to ₱10,000. Section 15 of Rule 70 expressly commands that a motion for preliminary mandatory injunction in a forcible entry case "shall" be decided within thirty days from filing — language that the SC characterized as unmistakably mandatory, leaving judges no discretion to delay. While Judge Mediodea correctly observed that a court must rule on every motion presented, this duty cannot be invoked to override the reglementary periods set by the Rules. Actions for forcible entry and unlawful detainer are summary in nature precisely because they involve the disruption of social order requiring prompt restoration; allowing the injunction plea to delay the main case indefinitely would strip the action of its summary character. Under **Rule 3.05 of the Code of Judicial Conduct**, judges are obligated to dispose of court business promptly and decide cases within the required periods, and inability to do so is not excusable absent a prior request for extension from the SC. The SC reminded judges that they must remain in full control of their sala and not yield to the convenience of lawyers or parties.

As for Maderada, the SC clarified that her personal appearance in her own case did not constitute unauthorized practice of law or moonlighting. **Section 34 of Rule 138 of the Rules of Court** expressly recognizes a party's right to conduct litigation personally or *in propria persona*, a right that flows from law itself and cannot be forfeited merely because the litigant happens to be a court employee. A person does not "practice law" by representing himself any more than he practices medicine by administering first aid to himself. Practice of law, as defined in jurisprudence, requires the habitual and customary holding out of oneself as a lawyer, typically for compensation — elements absent when a party simply litigates her own cause. The SC therefore rejected the OCA's view that self-representation by a judiciary employee amounts to moonlighting or malfeasance.

However, the SC drew a firm line at Maderada's representation of her co-plaintiff. Acting as counsel for another person is qualitatively different from defending one's own rights; the rationale for permitting self-representation — that one is protecting a personal interest — does not apply when an individual appears on behalf of a distinct party, even a relative. Court employees are expected to devote their full time to government service, and representing another party without prior SC authority impairs that duty. Because this was her first such infraction and there was no showing that she received a fee, the SC deemed a **reprimand** sufficient, though with a stern warning against repetition.

## DOCTRINE

**A judge's failure to decide a motion for preliminary injunction in a forcible entry case within the 30-day period mandated by Section 15, Rule 70 of the Rules of Court constitutes gross inefficiency warranting administrative sanction, regardless of the judge's duty to rule on every motion presented.**

Related doctrines established or reinforced by this case:

1. **Right to self-representation under Section 34, Rule 138.** A party — including a court employee — has a statutory right to conduct litigation personally (*in propria persona*). Such self-representation does not constitute the practice of law and therefore cannot give rise to administrative liability.

2. **Representation of another party by a court employee.** A court employee who appears as counsel for a co-plaintiff or any other distinct party, without prior authority from the SC, commits an act that undermines public service and may be sanctioned. The ruling distinguishes sharply between litigating one's own case and appearing on behalf of someone else.

3. **Mandatory character of the 30-day period.** Section 15, Rule 70 uses the word "shall," rendering the period for resolving preliminary injunctions in ejectment cases mandatory, not directory. Judges may not stretch this period on the ground that other motions are pending.

4. **Duty to control the proceedings.** Judges must remain in full control of their sala and may not allow the convenience of lawyers or parties to override the reglementary periods for case disposition.

5. **Moonlighting defined.** Engaging in a private vocation or profession without prior SC approval amounts to moonlighting; however, self-representation *pro se* by a judiciary employee does not fall within this prohibition.

## NOTES

This case is a good complement to **[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]** (1991) for understanding the boundaries of "practice of law" in the Philippine setting — here, the SC clarified that *pro se* litigation is outside its scope, but representation of others is not.

The ruling also serves as a useful reminder that the **Rule on Summary Procedure** applies to all ejectment cases regardless of the amount of damages sought, so the 30-day injunction period applies uniformly and cannot be sidestepped by reference to the complexity of ancillary motions.

For bar review purposes, remember the pairing: **right to self-representation is protected**, but **representation of another without authority is sanctionable** — a distinction the SC was careful to emphasize through the differing dispositions against Judge Mediodea (fine) and Maderada (reprimand only).
