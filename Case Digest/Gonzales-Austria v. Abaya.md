**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Discipline of a Government-Employed Lawyer; Forgery as Grounds for Bar Suspension

> **Gist:** A lawyer who holds a government office is not automatically shielded from bar discipline for misconduct committed in that office — if the misconduct is serious enough to reflect on the lawyer's moral fitness, such as forging a judge's signature, it can support suspension from the practice of law itself, separate from whatever administrative penalty the government position carries.

## FACTS

This consolidated administrative matter arose from charges that Judge Emmanuel M. Abaya of RTC Branch 51, Palawan, and Annabelle Cardenas, a court stenographic reporter, engaged in a "ghost employee" scheme — Cardenas was carried on the payroll and certified present from August 1983 to May 1984 despite never reporting for work, while actually employed elsewhere as a tourist guide, with Judge Abaya verifying her falsified time records as true and correct. Atty. Ligaya Gonzales-Austria, the original complainant and a clerk of court, was in turn found to have forged a judge's signature on a judicial order in the course of the same set of proceedings.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer who holds a government position — here, a clerk of court — may be disciplined as a member of the Bar for misconduct connected to that government office, and specifically whether forging a judge's signature on a judicial order warrants suspension from the practice of law.

## RULING

The SC found Judge Abaya, Cardenas, and Atty. Gonzales-Austria all guilty as charged (except one exaction charge against Judge Abaya). On the specific question of bar discipline, the Court articulated the operative rule: **a lawyer who holds a government office generally may not be disciplined as a member of the Bar for misconduct in the discharge of purely official government duties — unless that misconduct is of such a character as to affect the lawyer's qualification as a lawyer, or shows moral delinquency.** Forging a judge's signature on a judicial order, whatever authority or agency the lawyer claims to have relied on, is exactly that kind of misconduct: it is a serious breach of legal ethics and public trust, not a mere administrative lapse confined to the government position. Atty. Gonzales-Austria was suspended from the practice of law for one year, separate and apart from any administrative sanction tied to her government post.

## DOCTRINE

**A government-employed lawyer's official misconduct is not automatically a bar-discipline matter — but misconduct serious enough to reflect on moral fitness to practice law, such as forgery, supports suspension from the Bar independent of any administrative penalty for the government office itself.**

## NOTES

This case supplies the missing link between "is this private practice" (the usual [[Public Officials and Private Practice]] inquiry) and a distinct, easily overlooked question: "does this government lawyer's *official* misconduct also justify Bar discipline?" The answer turns on whether the misconduct reflects on moral fitness — a forged judicial signature clears that bar easily; an ordinary administrative lapse in office generally would not.
