**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Judges Absolutely Prohibited from Private Practice; Illegal Practice of Law

> **Gist:** A judge who continues to appear as counsel of record and file pleadings in a case after assuming judicial office — even a case he handled before appointment, and even for his own family — is engaged in illegal practice of law and violates the Code of Judicial Conduct.

## FACTS

Ramon A. Arejola, while still an attorney with the Public Attorney's Office, filed an application for land registration on behalf of himself and his co-heirs, including his sister, Nelia Arejola-Ziga. He was appointed to the judiciary as a Municipal Trial Court judge in 1997. Notwithstanding his new office, Judge Arejola continued to actively participate in the land registration case as counsel: he filed a Motion for Reconsideration nearly a year after assuming the bench, filed a Manifestation, and personally appeared as counsel at a hearing — all while sitting as a judge. His own sister and co-heir filed the administrative complaint against him.

## ISSUES

Whether a sitting judge's continued appearance as counsel of record in a case he had handled before his judicial appointment constitutes illegal practice of law, notwithstanding that the case involved his own family's inheritance and had been filed before he took office.

## RULING

The SC found Judge Arejola administratively liable. Under Rule 138, Sec. 35, judges and other officials and employees of the courts are absolutely prohibited from engaging in private practice of law — a prohibition rooted in the incompatibility between sitting in judgment and advocating a private cause, and one that carries no exception for cases begun before appointment or for family matters. His continued signing of pleadings, filing of a manifestation, and personal court appearance after assuming the bench were all acts of practicing law, not merely ministerial continuations of a pre-existing engagement. The Court held him liable for illegal practice of law and for violating the Code of Judicial Conduct, the Rules of Court, the Civil Service Rules, and RA 6713, and imposed a fine.

## DOCTRINE

**The Rule 138, Sec. 35 prohibition on judges and court officials engaging in private practice is absolute and admits no exception for cases that predate the judge's appointment or that involve the judge's own family.** A judge must formally withdraw from any pending private engagement upon assuming judicial office; continuing to sign pleadings or appear at hearings afterward is illegal practice of law.

## NOTES

This case gives [[Public Officials and Private Practice]]'s Rule 138, Sec. 35 entry a concrete face — the temptation to keep handling "just this one case," especially a family matter begun before appointment, is exactly the fact pattern the rule is built to catch. Compare with [[Office of the Court Administrator vs. Atty. Misael M. Ladaga]], which applies the same absolute-prohibition logic to a court lawyer rather than a judge.
