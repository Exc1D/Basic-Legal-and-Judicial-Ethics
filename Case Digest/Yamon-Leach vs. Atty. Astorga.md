
**A.C. No. 5987, August 28, 2019**

**Ponente:** Per Curiam (Bersamin, C.J. took no part)

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Disbarment; Code of Professional Responsibility; Lawyer's Oath

> **Gist:** A lawyer who misappropriates his client's money through falsified deeds and who persistently ignores lawful orders of the SC over many years is guilty of deceit, gross misconduct, violation of the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]] and the Code of Professional Responsibility, and willful disobedience — warranting the ultimate penalty of **disbarment**, especially when he is a habitual offender with prior administrative sanctions.

## FACTS

Complainant Vidaylin Yamon-Leach filed a disbarment case against her distant relative and family lawyer, **Atty. Arturo B. Astorga**, who was also an incumbent Provincial Board Member of Leyte. In September 2001, Atty. Astorga persuaded complainant to buy a P1.4 Million "beach-front" property allegedly owned by a certain Ms. Villaflora Un in Baybay, Leyte, assuring her that it could be paid in installments. Complainant entrusted him with P110,000 before leaving for Las Vegas, and then remitted an additional P1,300,215 through PNB, for an intended total payment to the supposed seller.

When complainant returned to the Philippines in December 2001 due to her father's death, Atty. Astorga handed her a "Deed of Absolute Sale of Portions of Registered Land," which was **undated, named different sellers ("Ariston Chaperon and Ursula Gumba")**, lacked a description of boundaries, and omitted tax certificate numbers. Despite these irregularities, Atty. Astorga assured her that he would "make the necessary corrections later" and, through a tickler and statement of account, demanded further sums for his attorney's fees, processing, and taxes. Complainant complied, remitting P204,000 and P205,436 in January 2002 — bringing his total acknowledged receipt to **P1,819,651.00**.

Complainant's brother later verified with Ms. Villaflora Un that she had received **not a single centavo** from Atty. Astorga, and the property was neither hers nor beach-front. Worse, both "sellers" named in the Deed — Ariston Chaperon and Ursula Gumba — had died years earlier, in **1994 and 1995**, respectively, making the signatures on the Deed forgeries. When confronted, Atty. Astorga admitted having used the money and offered apologies and promises to pay, including an offer to execute a Deed of Real Estate Mortgage over his Quezon City properties, none of which he ever fulfilled.

Beyond the substantive misconduct, **Atty. Astorga conducted himself with equal defiance toward the SC itself**. From April 2, 2003 through 2019, the SC issued at least five separate resolutions directing him to file his Comment — eventually fining him P1,000 and then P2,000 for non-compliance, and granting him extensions — but respondent never once filed the required pleading, nor offered any plausible justification for his silence. A 2019 resolution was returned unserved with a notation that he was "sick due to old age," mirroring the same excuse he used in a prior administrative case.

## ISSUES

Whether Atty. Astorga's persistent failure to comply with multiple lawful orders of the SC constitutes **gross misconduct and willful disobedience** warranting disciplinary action under Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court and the Code of Professional Responsibility.

Whether respondent's acts of **deceit, fraudulent misrepresentation, and misappropriation** of his client's money — accomplished through a falsified Deed of Absolute Sale bearing the forged signatures of deceased persons, and his continued failure to make restitution despite repeated promises — constitute violations of the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]], Article 19 of the Civil Code, and Rule 1.01, Canon 1 of the Code of Professional Responsibility justifying the ultimate penalty of **disbarment**.

## RULING

The SC held that respondent's brazen disregard of its directives established **willful disobedience and gross misconduct**, both of which are grounds for disbarment or suspension under Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court. Gross misconduct has been defined as any inexcusable, shameful, flagrant, or unlawful conduct in the administration of justice that is prejudicial to the rights of the parties, generally motivated by a premeditated, obstinate, or intentional purpose. The SC stressed that respondent, as an officer of the court and an indispensable partner in the administration of justice, bears a graver responsibility than ordinary litigants to obey court orders — and that a resolution of the SC is "not a mere request but an order which should be complied with promptly and completely." Through his non-compliance, respondent violated **Canon 12** of the Code (duty to assist in the speedy and efficient administration of justice), as well as **Rules 12.03 and 12.04** (prohibiting lawyers from letting extensions lapse without explanation, and from unduly delaying cases or misusing court processes).

On the substantive charges, the SC found the documentary evidence overwhelming: respondent himself sought out complainant, solicited over P1.8 Million, misrepresented that the funds would be used to purchase a beach-front property, pocketing the money instead; he forged a Deed of Sale to conceal his wrongdoing, even notarizing the signatures of persons who had been dead for **eight years**; and he then compounded his deceit by demanding additional amounts for fictitious taxes, fees, and professional services. These acts constituted a clear breach of **Article 19 of the Civil Code** (duty to act with justice, give everyone his due, and observe honesty and good faith), the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]] (which required him to "obey the laws," "do no falsehood," and conduct himself according to the best of his knowledge and discretion), and **Rule 1.01, Canon 1** of the Code, which forbids a lawyer from engaging in "unlawful, dishonest, immoral or deceitful conduct." The SC explained that deceitful conduct involves moral turpitude — anything contrary to justice, modesty, or good morals — and need not require a criminal conviction to warrant disciplinary action.

In determining the penalty, the SC weighed several aggravating factors: (1) the gravity of the deceit involving falsified notarized documents; (2) respondent's pattern of **prior administrative infractions** — having been fined for offensive language in *Nuñez v. Astorga* (492 Phil. 450, 2005) and suspended for two years in *Saladaga v. Astorga* (748 Phil. 1, 2014) for similar fraudulent misrepresentation regarding a fictitious property; (3) the fact that the Saladaga suspension was never served; and (4) his continued defiance of the SC's orders in the present case, demonstrating a "penchant for violating not only his oath and the Code, but orders from the Court as well."

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who misappropriates his client's money and who willfully disregards lawful orders of the SC commits deceit, gross misconduct, violation of the [[Revised Lawyer's Oath|Lawyer's Oath]] and the Code of Professional Responsibility, and willful disobedience — and may be meted the penalty of disbarment, particularly when his misconduct is compounded by a pattern of prior administrative violations.** Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court enumerates the following grounds for disbarment or suspension of an attorney: **(1) deceit; (2) malpractice; (3) gross misconduct in office; (4) grossly immoral conduct; (5) conviction of a crime involving moral turpitude; (6) violation of the lawyer's oath; (7) willful disobedience of any lawful order of a superior court; and (8) willfully appearing as an attorney for a party without authority to do so.**

Good moral character is not merely a condition precedent for admission to the Bar — **it is a continuing requirement** for maintaining one's membership in the legal profession. The practice of law is a **privilege**, not a right, and when an attorney is shown to be unworthy of the trust reposed in him, it becomes not only the right but the duty of the SC to withdraw that privilege.

Furthermore, under Canon 1, Rule 1.01 of the Code, **a lawyer shall not engage in unlawful, dishonest, immoral or deceitful conduct** — and disbarment is warranted where the lawyer engages in **calculated acts of deceit, abuse of client's trust, and misappropriation of funds**, as these reveal a basic moral flaw rendering him unfit to remain in the practice of law.

## NOTES

This case is a textbook illustration of **cumulative disciplinary liability**: a single administrative offense may merit suspension, but the same offense committed **after prior sanctions and warnings** justifies escalation to disbarment — respondent here had already been fined once and suspended once for substantially similar fraudulent conduct, making the third strike terminal.

Note also that **willful disobedience of the SC's lawful orders, standing alone**, is sufficient ground for disbarment under Section 27, Rule 138 — separate and apart from the substantive misconduct involving the client.

The decision dispatches **two warnings worth remembering for bar review**: first, even a claim of illness or old age cannot be used as a perpetual shield against receipt of court processes when the same excuse has been previously invoked and not substantiated; second, complaints involving **deceitful conduct by lawyers (Rule 1.01)** do not require proof of criminal intent beyond the elements of fraudulent misrepresentation, artifice, or device employed upon an unsuspecting party to that party's injury.
