
**G.R. No. L-19450, May 27, 1965**

**Ponente:** Paredes, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Private Practice by Government Attorneys

> **Gist:** The SC ruled that a City Attorney who makes an isolated appearance as private prosecutor in a single criminal case — with permission from the Secretary of Justice, on official leave, and without compensation — does not engage in prohibited private practice under Section 35, Rule 138 of the Revised Rules of Court.

## FACTS

On September 4, 1959, the Chief of Police of Alaminos, Laguna charged Simplicio Villanueva with Malicious Mischief before the Justice of the Peace (JP) Court of the same municipality. Villanueva was initially represented by counsel *de officio*, later replaced by counsel *de parte*. The complainant, who had a personal stake in the civil liability arising from the offense, secured the services of City Attorney Ariston Fule of San Pablo City to appear as private prosecutor. Fule obtained permission from the Secretary of Justice, with the conditions that every appearance would be considered official leave and that he would receive no compensation for his services.

Defense counsel challenged Fule's authority to appear on two grounds. First, citing *Aquino v. Blanco* (L-1532, November 28, 1947), counsel argued that a government attorney who had qualified as Assistant Provincial Fiscal or City Fiscal ceased, by operation of law, to engage in private law practice. Later, counsel invoked Section 32, Rule 127 (now Section 35, Rule 138) of the Revised Rules of Court, which prohibits certain government officials from engaging in private practice as members of the bar. The JP Court overruled both objections and allowed Fule's appearance. On appeal, the Court of First Instance (CFI) of Laguna, presided by Judge Hilarion U. Jarencio, affirmed the JP's order, and the matter was elevated to the SC.

## ISSUES

Whether City Attorney Fule's appearance as private prosecutor in a criminal case before the JP Court of Alaminos, Laguna constituted **private practice** prohibited under Section 32, Rule 127 (now Section 35, Rule 138) of the Revised Rules of Court.

Whether the City Attorney could validly appear as an **agent or friend** of the offended party under Section 31, Rule 127 of the Rules of Court, given his official position as Assistant City Attorney of San Pablo City.

## RULING

The SC affirmed the CFI's decision and held that Fule's isolated appearance did not constitute private practice within the meaning of the Rules. The SC explained that **"practice"** requires more than a single act — it consists in frequent or customary actions, a succession of acts of the same kind. Drawing from American jurisprudence (*State v. Cotner*, 87 Kan. 864 and *State v. Bryan*, 98 N.C. 644), the SC observed that practice of law, to fall within statutory prohibition, has been interpreted as customarily or habitually holding one's self out to the public and demanding compensation for such services. A lone appearance on one occasion is not conclusive proof of engagement in private law practice.

The SC adopted the Solicitor General's formulation that private practice essentially implies presenting oneself in the active and continued practice of the legal profession, with professional services available to the public for compensation, whether as a source of livelihood or in consideration of those services. Because Fule's appearance was isolated, uncompensated, made on official leave, and authorized by the Secretary of Justice, it could not be characterized as prohibited practice.

Beyond the prohibition question, the CFI correctly recognized that the offended party had a right to representation in the civil action impliedly instituted alongside the criminal action. Under Section 31, Rule 127 of the Rules of Court, a party may conduct litigation in person, with the aid of an agent or friend, or with the aid of an attorney. Fule appeared precisely as an agent or friend of the complainant. Since the prosecution of crimes originating from Alaminos fell under the Office of the Provincial Fiscal — not the City Attorney of San Pablo — there was no overlap or conflict between Fule's official functions and his role as private prosecutor in this particular case.

## DOCTRINE

**An isolated appearance by a government attorney as private prosecutor — made without compensation, on official leave, and with permission from the Secretary of Justice — does not constitute "private practice" within the prohibition of Section 35, Rule 138 of the Revised Rules of Court.** Practice of law, for purposes of the prohibition, requires the **frequent or habitual exercise** of the profession: holding one's self out to the public and offering legal services for compensation as a source of livelihood. A single appearance as agent or friend of an offended party, under Section 31, Rule 127, does not satisfy this standard.

## NOTES

This case is a useful counterweight to *Aquino v. Blanco* — while *Aquino* holds that a fiscal automatically ceases private practice upon qualification, *Villanueva* clarifies that isolated appearances (especially when compensated-free and officially sanctioned) remain permissible.

Worth remembering for the bar: the prohibition under Section 35, Rule 138 targets **habitual, compensated engagement**, not one-off acts of legal assistance — the distinction between being "engaged in private practice" and merely appearing "as an agent or friend" under Section 31, Rule 127 is the linchpin of this ruling.
