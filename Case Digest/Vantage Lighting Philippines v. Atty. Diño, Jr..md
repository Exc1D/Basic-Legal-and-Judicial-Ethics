
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 7389 (consolidated with A.C. No. 10596), July 2, 2019

**Ponente:** Jardeleza, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Bribery Solicitation; Merit-Based Practice; Harassment of a Former Client; CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2 (Duty to Lead a Merit-Based Legal Practice)

> **Gist:** Counsel told his corporate client it had to pay ₱150,000.00 "to the judge" to secure a temporary restraining order, pocketed ₱20,000.00 of it, and then — when the client balked at paying more — filed five retaliatory criminal and civil cases against the client and its officers within two months. The Court found this conduct disbarment-worthy: a three-year suspension was "too light a penalty" for a lawyer who claimed the judiciary itself was for sale.

## FACTS

On August 15, 2006, Vantage Lighting Philippines, Inc. engaged Atty. Jose A. Diño, Jr. under a Retainer's Agreement to sue PHPC Co. and Hitachi Plant Engineering Co. Ltd. The civil complaint was filed before the RTC of Parañaque City on September 5, 2006. On September 11, 2006, respondent called Vantage's Vice President for Finance, Ma. Cecilia G. Roque, informing her that Vantage had to pay **₱150,000.00 to the judge** to whom the case would be raffled, for issuance of a temporary restraining order (TRO). He texted that if Vantage could not produce the cash by 2:00 p.m., he would advance ₱20,000.00 himself. The case was raffled to Judge Rolando How. Vantage, believing this a routine legal expense, paid respondent ₱20,000.00.

Respondent then told Roque he had "already paid 130k but that's my own lookout," instructed her to have a messenger ready to collect the TRO, and promised it would issue "tomorrow." When the TRO did not materialize as promised, he texted that "the deal with the judge is no longer valid" and that the ₱20,000.00 would be returned — then, days later, that a "fixer" said the judge would release the order later, which he refused to accept, again promising a refund. Vantage re-deposited the additional ₱65,000.00 respondent had separately demanded and sent a messenger for the promised ₱20,000.00 refund. Respondent refused to return it, said he would simply apply it to his fees, and — when Roque said she needed to clear the matter with management — became angry, threatening that Vantage would "be sorry" if it did not pay his fees and reimburse the ₱130,000.00 he claimed to have given "fixers" as payment to Judge How for the TRO.

Respondent withdrew as counsel on September 21, 2006 and, within the next two months, filed **five separate cases** against Vantage and its officers: an *estafa* complaint, a collection suit for unpaid fees, a grave oral defamation complaint, a libel complaint, and a falsification complaint (the last also naming Vantage's new counsel, Attys. Paris and Sherwin Real, whom respondent accused — without proof — of fabricating a letter in his name). Respondent's own complaint against the Reals was separately docketed as A.C. No. 10596 and consolidated with this case.

## ISSUES

Whether respondent's representation that he could secure a TRO by bribing the presiding judge, and his subsequent filing of multiple retaliatory suits against his former client, constitute gross misconduct warranting disbarment.

## RULING

Yes. The IBP Investigating Commissioner found that respondent tainted the judiciary's image by claiming the ₱150,000.00 would facilitate the TRO's issuance, and recommended a three-month suspension; the IBP Board of Governors increased this to one year. The Supreme Court found even this insufficient and imposed **disbarment**.

The Court rejected respondent's claim that the ₱150,000.00 was merely for legitimate "mobilization expenses," noting his own *estafa* complaint-affidavit admitted the amount was for securing the TRO, and that the Retainer's Agreement never authorized hiring "collaborating lawyers." By representing that he could secure a TRO through a ₱150,000.00 payment to the judge, respondent violated **Canon 13** of the CPR. The Court's ratio is the sharpest statement in this vault's Canon I case set: **"As an officer of the Court, [a lawyer] has a paramount duty to protect the court's integrity and assist it in the administration of justice according to law. He should not espouse a belief that the judicial system can be bought, much less contribute to the perpetuation of such belief… This inexcusable, shameful and unlawful act… by itself, constitutes gross misconduct."**

The five retaliatory suits compounded the violation: instead of pursuing his fee claim through the lien mechanism available under Rule 16.03, respondent chose harassment, violating the Lawyer's Oath (against suing any "groundless, false or unlawful suit") and Rule 20.04 (avoid client-fee controversies via lawsuit except to prevent injustice or fraud). Filing a disbarment case against the Reals — his former client's new counsel — for no proven reason also violated **Canon 8** (courtesy toward professional colleagues). The Court expressly found a three-year suspension "too light a penalty" given the gravity of claiming a member of the judiciary was for sale, and ordered disbarment. The consolidated complaint against Attys. Real was dismissed for lack of evidence.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who represents to a client that a favorable court ruling can be purchased — even where no bribe is conclusively proven to have reached the judge — commits gross misconduct warranting disbarment, because the harm lies in besmirching the judiciary's integrity and normalizing the belief that courts are for sale.**

## NOTES

**Spelling note:** the respondent's surname is **Diño** (with the tilde), per the official Supreme Court decision, chanrobles, and the SC E-Library. Some bar-review materials render it "Dino" (dropping the tilde), and the syllabus for this course renders it "Difto" — almost certainly a transcription slip. This file is titled and cited using the correct spelling, with "Atty. Jose A. Dino" and "Atty. Jose Difto" carried as aliases so links written either way still resolve here.

This is the most severe of the three merit-based-practice penalties in this vault — disbarment, versus six months in [[Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.]] and an (already-mooted) two years in [[Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho]]. The distinguishing aggravator the Court leaned on is not the bribery claim alone but the **retaliatory pattern**: five vexatious suits filed against a former client within two months of a fee dispute. Good exam material for penalty-calibration questions — three respondents, three degrees of merit-based-practice breach, three different sanctions.
