
**G.R. No. 148753, July 30, 2004**

**Ponente:** Justice Artemio V. Panganiban

**Subject:** Obligations and Contracts; Banking Law; Truth in Lending Act; Credit Transactions

> **Gist:** Banks cannot unilaterally raise interest rates and penalties in loan contracts without the borrower's consent — such escalation clauses violate the **mutuality of contracts** under Article 1308 of the Civil Code, and courts have the authority to equitably reduce iniquitous or unconscionable rates even though the Usury Law has been repealed; here, what PNB claimed as a P2-million deficiency turned out to be an overcollection, requiring the bank to refund over P3.6 million to the borrowers.

## FACTS

In 1989, Petitioner New Sampaguita Builders Construction, Inc. (NSBCI), through Board Resolutions and a corporate Secretary's Certificate, obtained an P8-million commercial loan from Respondent Philippine National Bank (PNB) to fund various construction projects, including MWSS watermain works, NEA-Liberty farm development, and Clark Air Base barracks. The loan was secured by a real estate mortgage over ten parcels of residential land in Pangasinan registered in the names of Petitioner-spouses Eduardo and Arcelita Dee, who signed the mortgage as accommodation mortgagors and sureties through a Joint and Solidary Agreement (JSA).

NSBCI executed three promissory notes totaling P8 million, with stipulated interest rates of 19.5 percent and 21.5 percent per annum, plus a 6 percent penalty per annum on unpaid amounts. The notes contained a uniform clause permitting PNB to unilaterally increase interest rates "within the limits allowed by law at any time depending on whatever policy it may adopt in the future," without prior notice to or consent from NSBCI. The corresponding Credit Agreements and Disclosure Statements were issued under the Truth in Lending Act (RA 3765), but none specified the method of computation or any basis for adjusting rates upward.

When NSBCI defaulted, it sent post-dated checks and requested loan restructuring. After the checks bounced and negotiations collapsed, PNB filed a Petition for Sale under Act 3135, as amended, leading to an extrajudicial foreclosure on February 26, 1992, with PNB emerging as the highest bidder at P10,334,000. NSBCI failed to redeem, so PNB consolidated title and demanded a deficiency of P2,172,476.43 plus interest. NSBCI and the Spouses Dee refused, prompting PNB to file a collection suit. The RTC dismissed PNB's complaint, finding the loan account bloated and noting NSBCI's supposed entitlement to PNB's debt relief package (DRP). The CA reversed and ordered petitioners to pay the deficiency computed using the original 6 percent penalty rate. Hence this Petition.

## ISSUES

The Petition raises two overarching questions. First, whether NSBCI's loan accounts were validly bloated by PNB through unilateral interest and penalty escalations imposed without the borrowers' consent. Second, whether the extrajudicial foreclosure proceedings and the resulting deficiency claim against petitioners are valid and enforceable.

Subsumed within these are subsidiary questions on the availability of PNB's debt relief package, the correctness of the subsidiary ledger entries, the adequacy of the auction price, the solidary liability of petitioner-spouses under the JSA, and whether the foreclosure complied with jurisdictional requirements.

## RULING

The Petition is partly meritorious. The SC affirmed the CA's ruling that the extrajudicial foreclosure was valid but modified the deficiency computation, holding that no deficiency existed — instead, PNB had overcollected and was ordered to refund P3,686,101.52 to petitioners.

**On the bloated loan accounts.** The SC held that the unilateral escalation clause in the promissory notes, which allowed PNB to raise interest rates at its sole discretion without notice or assent from NSBCI, violated the **principle of mutuality of contracts** under Article 1308 of the Civil Code. Such a one-sided imposition is void because the fulfillment of the contract cannot be left to the will of only one party. The notes were contracts of adhesion, drafted entirely by PNB on a "take it or leave it" basis, and any ambiguity in them must be construed strictly against the lender under Article 1377. Although CB Circular No. 905 effectively repealed the Usury Law ceiling, the SC stressed that nothing in the Circular grants lenders carte blanche authority to raise interest rates to levels that would "enslave their borrowers or lead to a hemorrhaging of their assets." Interest rates ranging from 26 to 35 percent imposed by PNB were equitably reduced for being iniquitous, unconscionable, and exorbitant, even if not technically usurious.

The SC further found that PNB violated the automatic conversion provision of the promissory notes, which required that portions remaining unpaid after 730 days be converted into medium-term loans at the applicable rate. Because PNB failed to make this conversion and did not specify the medium-term rate, the SC applied the legal rate of 12 percent per annum under CB Circular 416 from the date of automatic conversion onward. As for penalties, the SC struck down PNB's unilateral increase from 6 percent to 36 percent per annum as iniquitous and unconscionable, equitably reducing the penalty to zero under Article 2227 of the Civil Code. Other charges, including insurance and service fees not disclosed in the statements, were similarly disallowed.

**On the Truth in Lending Act.** The SC gave "teeth" to the forty-one-year-old RA 3765, holding that undisclosed or unrevealed interest increases, penalties, and charges in the Disclosure Statements — even if stipulated in the promissory notes — cannot be given effect. Novation of the disclosure figures by the later-executed notes cannot be presumed because the animus novandi must be express and unequivocal.

**On the debt relief package.** The SC agreed with the CA that NSBCI failed to establish entitlement to PNB's DRP, which is a mere offer subject to specific conditions, not an outright grant. NSBCI did not prove that it was seriously affected by the economic slowdown near the US military bases or that it possessed sufficient margin of equity in hard collaterals.

**On the extrajudicial foreclosure.** The SC held that the foreclosure was valid because PNB had authority to foreclose under the real estate mortgage, the notices were duly published and posted, the sale was conducted within the province where the properties lay, and no fraud was shown. The auction price of P10,334,000 against PNB's claim of P12,506,476 was not shocking or unconscionable; in fact, a lower bid price makes redemption easier for the mortgagor. The inadequacy of the bid price alone is not ground to set aside a public auction.

**On the Joint and Solidary Agreement.** The JSA was a contract of **suretyship**, not guaranty, under Article 2047 of the Civil Code. The Spouses Dee consented to be jointly and severally liable as sureties, but their liability was confined to the P7.7 million revolving credit line covered by the JSA dated August 31, 1989. Since the JSA was executed after the first promissory note but before the second and third, only the unpaid portion of the first note plus the second and third notes fell within its scope.

**On the deficiency claim.** Applying simple interest, the legal principles on the application of payments (Articles 1252, 1253, and 1254 of the Civil Code), and using only the stipulated rates of 19.5 percent and 21.5 percent before conversion and 12 percent thereafter, with a 1 percent attorney's fee and no penalty or insurance charges, the SC computed the total outstanding obligation as of February 26, 1992 to be P6,647,898.48. Subtracting the bid price of P10,334,000 left a surplus of P3,686,101.52, which PNB must return under the principles of **solutio indebiti** (Article 1960) and the prohibition against unjust enrichment.

## DOCTRINE

**Courts have the authority to strike down or modify provisions in promissory notes that grant lenders unrestrained power to unilaterally increase interest rates, penalties, and other charges without the borrower's prior notice and consent, because such unilateral authority violates the mutuality of contracts under Article 1308 of the Civil Code.** Although the Usury Law has been effectively repealed by CB Circular No. 905, courts may still reduce iniquitous or unconscionable rates for the use of money, and excessive interests, penalties, and other charges not reflected in the disclosure statements under the Truth in Lending Act (RA 3765) cannot be given effect even if stipulated in the promissory notes.

The key principles established are: (1) escalation clauses that allow one party to unilaterally modify interest rates are void for violating mutuality; (2) contracts of adhesion are construed strictly against the party who drafted them; (3) iniquitous or unconscionable liquidated damages may be equitably reduced to zero under Article 2227; (4) the Truth in Lending Act requires full disclosure of all charges prior to the consummation of the loan; and (5) novation by implied will cannot be presumed — the animus novandi must be express and unequivocal.

## NOTES

This case is frequently cited for the proposition that **CB Circular No. 905 did not authorize lenders to raise interest rates unilaterally** — a common misconception in bar exam questions. The decision also clarifies that contracts of adhesion, while not invalid per se, are strictly construed against the drafting party, and any ambiguity is resolved against the lender under Article 1377.

The SC's invocation of **solutio indebiti** is notable because it transformed PNB's deficiency claim into an overcollection, demonstrating that the remedy of refund is available when a creditor collects more than what is legally due. For bar review purposes, remember the hierarchy of interest rate application: stipulated rate → legal rate upon automatic conversion → equitable reduction if iniquitous.

This case also illustrates the interplay between the **Truth in Lending Act** and contractual autonomy, establishing that disclosure statements serve as a financial watchdog for borrowers — a doctrinal shift that has since been reinforced in subsequent jurisprudence on banking transparency.
