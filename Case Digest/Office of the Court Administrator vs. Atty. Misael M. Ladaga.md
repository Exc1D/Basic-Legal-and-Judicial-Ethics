
A.M. No. P-99-1287, January 26, 2001

**Ponente:** Kapunan, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Code of Conduct for Public Officials; Private Practice of Law by Court Employees

> **Gist:** While isolated court appearances by a court employee do not constitute "private practice" of law, he still needs **prior written permission from the head of the Department** (the Supreme Court, not his presiding judge) before engaging in any outside legal activity, even if done for free and with his judge's informal blessing.

## FACTS

Atty. Misael M. Ladaga was the Branch Clerk of Court of the Regional Trial Court of Makati, Branch 133. In a letter dated August 31, 1998, he asked the Court Administrator for authority to appear as **pro bono counsel** for his cousin, Narcisa Naldoza Ladaga, who was facing a Falsification of Public Document charge in the Metropolitan Trial Court of Quezon City. While that request was still pending, he actually went ahead and entered his appearance for his cousin in court on several dates — May 4–15, 1998, June 18, 1998, July 13, 1998, and August 5, 1998. For each of these court appearances, he filed leave applications which were approved, and his Presiding Judge was aware of what he was doing.

When the private complainant in the criminal case wrote the Court Administrator to inquire about Ladaga's authority to appear, the OCA referred the matter to him for comment. Ladaga admitted he had appeared without prior authorization but explained that his cousin was being maliciously prosecuted by a politically connected family out of personal vendetta. He further pointed out that his cousin had been his mentor and benefactress since childhood and that, as the only lawyer in the family, he felt duty-bound to help her pro bono. The private complainant, Lisa Payoyo Andres, had filed the case after her husband left her to cohabit with Ladaga's cousin, and the falsification charge stemmed from the birth certificate of a child born out of that affair.

On December 8, 1998, the Supreme Court denied Ladaga's request for authorization and directed the OCA to file formal charges against him. This led to the filing of the administrative complaint for violation of **Section 7(b)(2) of Republic Act No. 6713** (Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees), which prohibits government employees from engaging in the private practice of their profession unless authorized by the Constitution or law.

## ISSUES

At issue is whether Atty. Ladaga's several court appearances as pro bono counsel for his cousin constitute the prohibited "private practice" of law under **Section 7(b)(2) of R.A. 6713** and **Section 35, Rule 138 of the Revised Rules of Court**.

A related question is whether Ladaga violated **Section 12, Rule XVIII of the Revised Civil Service Rules** by failing to secure prior written permission from the head of his Department before engaging in outside legal activity, and whether the knowledge and acquiescence of his Presiding Judge could substitute for such written permission from the Supreme Court.

## RULING

The Supreme Court agreed with the investigating judge's recommendation to reprimand Ladaga, but clarified two distinct points. First, the SC held that Ladaga's several court appearances did **not** rise to the level of "private practice" of law as contemplated by the prohibition. Citing *[[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva|People vs. Villanueva]]* (121 Phil. 894), the SC explained that "private practice" of a profession implies something more than an isolated appearance — it contemplates a **succession of acts of the same nature done habitually or customarily**, with the lawyer holding himself out to the public as available for professional services for compensation. Although Ladaga appeared on multiple dates, the SC characterized these collectively as "isolated instances" falling short of the habitual and compensated engagement that the prohibition targets.

Second, however, the SC ruled that Ladaga violated **Section 12, Rule XVIII of the Revised Civil Service Rules**, which flatly requires any government officer or employee to obtain **written permission from the head of the Department** before engaging directly in any private vocation or profession. The SC emphasized that the proper authority for judiciary employees is the **Supreme Court itself**, not the Presiding Judge of the employee's station. The Presiding Judge's informal awareness of Ladaga's activities could not cure the absence of formal written permission from the Court. While Ladaga had been diligent enough to file leave applications for every court date, those leaves merely authorized his absence from office — they did not authorize the outside legal engagement itself.

The SC thus found that a reprimand was warranted on account of the procedural violation, though it gave weight to the mitigating facts that the appearance was pro bono, that this was Ladaga's first offense in seven years of government service, and that it arose from a desire to help a close relative in genuine financial difficulty.

## DOCTRINE

**Isolated court appearances by a court employee, even if recurring on several dates, do not constitute the "private practice" of law prohibited under Section 7(b)(2) of R.A. 6713 and Section 35, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court**, because "private practice" contemplates the **habitual or customary holding out of one's self to the public as a lawyer for compensation** as a source of livelihood, not a one-time or limited pro bono engagement.

However, even where the engagement does not amount to private practice, **government employees must still secure prior written permission from the head of the Department before engaging in outside professional activity under Section 12, Rule XVIII of the Revised Civil Service Rules.** For employees of the judiciary, the **head of the Department is the Supreme Court itself**, and mere acquiescence by the employee's Presiding Judge is insufficient to satisfy this requirement.

## NOTES

A common misconception is that the *[[People vs. Simplicio Villanueva|People vs. Villanueva]]* ruling means any court employee can walk into any hearing on a whim without clearance — remember that *Villanueva* only addressed the **definition** of "private practice," and the separate Civil Service Rules requirement of written permission from the Department head applies independently and is strictly enforced.

For bar purposes, always distinguish: (1) the prohibition on **private practice** (which looks at habituality and compensation), and (2) the requirement of **written permission for outside activity** (which is a separate, formalistic requirement — even pro bono, even with the presiding judge's nod, you still need the Supreme Court's written go-signal if you're a court employee).

This case is a useful illustration of how the SC treats minor first-offense violations by rank-and-file court personnel — reprimand with stern warning rather than suspension, particularly where mitigating circumstances like pro bono motive and length of service are present.
