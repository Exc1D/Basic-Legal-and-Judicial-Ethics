
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 5732, June 16, 2015

**Ponente:** Per Curiam (En Banc)

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Neglect of a Legal Matter; Dishonesty Toward a Client; Lawyer's Discretion Over Procedure and Strategy

> **Gist:** A lawyer let the deadline for a DARAB position paper lapse without filing anything, then let his client believe for nine months that the paper had been filed — while he himself had already received, and concealed, the adverse decision dismissing the case. The Court suspended him three years, well beyond the IBP's recommended four months, for the compounded neglect and dishonesty.

## FACTS

In early November 2000, Alfredo C. Olvida engaged Atty. Arnel C. Gonzales to file and handle a case to terminate the tenancy of Alfonso Lumanta over a 54,000-square-meter coconut farm in Tibungco, Davao City, owned by complainant's wife. Complainant paid a ₱15,000.00 acceptance fee and ₱700.00 advance appearance fee, and provided respondent with all supporting documents — a leasehold agreement, affidavits of witnesses, a certification of failed settlement, and inspection and conciliation records. The case was filed with the DARAB on January 22, 2001. After an unsuccessful amicable settlement conference on February 21, 2001, the Board directed the parties to submit position papers within 40 days.

Complainant repeatedly called respondent's office for updates as the deadline approached, including on April 25, 2001 — the last day for filing — without success. His secretary eventually told complainant the position paper had "already been filed," but could not produce a copy because respondent had prepared it himself on his own computer. Nine months later, on December 13, 2001, complainant finally received a copy of the DARAB decision — dismissing his case for lack of merit, expressly because respondent had never filed any position paper. Respondent had received his own copy of that adverse decision earlier and never told complainant.

Complainant terminated respondent and engaged new counsel to file a motion for reconsideration. Respondent took more than **seven years** to file his comment on the disbarment complaint, drawing a ₱2,000.00 fine from the Court for non-compliance with a show-cause order. When he finally answered, he blamed complainant — arguing a position paper was optional under DARAB rules, that complainant had failed to provide sufficient documents, and that complainant wanted to dictate how the case should be handled while refusing respondent's advice.

## ISSUES

Whether respondent's failure to file the position paper, concealment of the adverse decision from his client, and attempt to shift blame to the client constitute neglect and dishonesty warranting discipline.

## RULING

Yes. IBP Commissioner Cachapero found respondent negligent and recommended a four-month suspension, adopted by the IBP Board of Governors. The Supreme Court agreed on liability but found the four-month penalty inadequate given the dishonesty the IBP investigation had "glossed over," imposing a **three-year suspension** instead.

The Court grounded liability in **Canon 17** (fidelity to the client's cause) and **Canon 18** (competence and diligence), specifically Rule 18.02 (no neglect of a legal matter) and Rule 18.04 (keep the client informed and respond to requests within a reasonable time). Respondent's excuse — that complainant had failed to cooperate and wanted to "dictate the procedure" — was squarely rejected: **"a lawyer 'shall not allow his client to dictate the procedure in handling the case.'"** Even accepting a difference of opinion existed, that gave respondent no license to do nothing; he "should have acted as a lawyer in the case, not as a mere agent waiting for the complainant's instructions," and should have candidly told complainant to find new counsel if he could not or would not file the paper. The concealment of the adverse decision — letting his own secretary tell complainant the paper had been filed, then saying nothing for nine months while complainant awaited a ruling that had already gone against him — was treated as an independent aggravating dishonesty, not mere sloppiness.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer's own professional judgment on how to handle a case controls over a client's contrary preference — a disagreement over strategy is never an excuse for doing nothing, and a lawyer who lets a client believe work has been done that was not is guilty of a dishonesty distinct from, and aggravating of, the underlying neglect.**

## NOTES

**Naming note:** the complainant's surname is **Olvida**, not "Oliva" — the syllabus's spelling is almost certainly a transcription slip. This file carries "Oliva v. Gonzales" as an alias so the syllabus's own citation resolves here.

**A placement discrepancy, flagged honestly:** the syllabus cites this case under CPRA Canon I, **Sec. 4** ("Duty Not to Allow Interference in Any Matter Before a Court or Tribunal"). Having now read the decision in full, nothing in it involves government interference, agency pressure, or any third party inserting itself into a pending matter — the case is a straightforward neglect-and-dishonesty matter arising from a DARAB tenancy dispute. The one line in the decision that actually connects to Canon I doctrine is the Court's rejection of respondent's "the client wanted to dictate procedure" excuse, sourced to the old CPR's **Canon 19, Rule 19.03** — and that maps far more naturally onto **CPRA Canon I, Sec. 5** ("A lawyer shall not allow the client to dictate or determine the procedure in handling the case"), for which the syllabus lists no case at all. This vault therefore digests and cross-links the case primarily under [[Lawyer's Duty and Discretion in Procedure and Strategy]] rather than forcing an "interference" reading the facts do not support. See [[Canon I - Independence]] for the Sec. 4 discussion, which notes this same discrepancy from the other direction.
