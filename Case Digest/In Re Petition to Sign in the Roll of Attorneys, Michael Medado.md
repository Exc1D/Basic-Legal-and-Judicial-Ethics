# In Re: Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys (Michael A. Medado)

**B.M. No. 2540, September 24, 2013**

**Ponente:** Sereno, C.J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics

> **Gist:** The SC allowed a petitioner who practiced law for over 30 years without signing the Roll of Attorneys to finally sign, but penalized him with a one-year delay in admission and a fine for unauthorized practice of law.

## FACTS
Michael A. Medado graduated from the University of the Philippines with a Bachelor of Laws degree in 1979 and passed the bar examinations that same year. He took the Attorney’s Oath at the Philippine International Convention Center in May 1980 and was scheduled to sign the Roll of Attorneys a few days later. However, he misplaced the notice to sign and went on vacation in his province, mistakenly believing that what he had signed at the PICC entrance was already the Roll of Attorneys. Years later, while rummaging through old files, he found the notice and realized his omission. Since he was primarily engaged in corporate and taxation work rather than litigation, he operated under the mistaken belief that signing the roll was not urgent and eventually forgot about it. In 2005, he was unable to provide his roll number for Mandatory Continuing Legal Education (MCLE) compliance. He filed a petition to sign the roll in 2012. The Office of the Bar Confidant (OBC) conducted a clarificatory conference and recommended denying the petition for gross negligence and misconduct.

## ISSUES
The issue is whether the petitioner should be allowed to sign the Roll of Attorneys despite a delay spanning over three decades. At issue is also whether his continued practice of law without signing the roll constitutes unauthorized practice of law, and if so, what the appropriate penalty should be given that he is not technically a full-fledged member of the Bar.

## RULING
The SC granted the petition, allowing Medado to sign the Roll of Attorneys, subject to a penalty. The SC reasoned that denying the petition would be akin to imposing the ultimate penalty of disbarment, which is reserved for the most serious ethical transgressions. The SC found that disbarment was not warranted because Medado demonstrated good faith and good moral character by voluntarily coming forward to correct his own omission, even after 30 years. He had not been subjected to any disqualification proceedings and had maintained a competent legal career in various reputable organizations. 

However, the SC did not fully exculpate him from liability. While Medado initially acted under an honest mistake of fact when he thought he had already signed the roll, this excuse vanished the moment he found the misplaced notice and realized his omission. At that point, he knew he was not a full-fledged member of the Bar, yet he chose to continue practicing law. The SC applied the principle *ignorantia facti excusat; ignorantia legis neminem excusat*, noting that a mistake of fact may excuse a person, but a mistake of law cannot, as everyone is presumed to know the law. By continuing to practice law despite knowing he had not signed the roll, Medado willfully engaged in the **unauthorized practice of law**. This transgresses **Canon 9 of the Code of Professional Responsibility**, which prohibits lawyers from assisting in the unauthorized practice of law—a prohibition that subsumes the lawyer's own unauthorized practice. While this act could also constitute indirect contempt of court under Rule 71 of the Rules of Court, the SC refrained from making such a finding because no formal charges for contempt had been filed. 

## DOCTRINE
**Signing in the Roll of Attorneys is the final act that makes a bar passer a full-fledged member of the Philippine Bar.** Practicing law without having signed in the Roll of Attorneys constitutes unauthorized practice of law, violating Canon 9 of the Code of Professional Responsibility. For a practitioner who is not yet a full-fledged lawyer, the appropriate penalty for unauthorized practice of law is a penalty akin to suspension—by delaying the allowance to sign the roll—and the imposition of a fine.

## NOTES
Taking the Attorney’s Oath is not sufficient to make one a full-fledged lawyer; signing the Roll of Attorneys is the crucial, final step that completes admission to the Bar. A common misconception is that Canon 9 only covers assisting *others* in the unauthorized practice of law, but this case clarifies that it also covers a lawyer's own unauthorized practice. The SC draws a clear line between mistake of fact and mistake of law in this context: once the petitioner discovered the misplaced notice, his mistake of fact ended, and his continued practice became a willful violation of the rules.
