
**Full Case Citation:** B.M. No. 914, October 1, 1999

**Ponente:** Kapunan, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Citizenship; Election of Philippine Citizenship; Admission to the Bar

> **Gist:** A legitimate child of a Filipino mother and an alien father, born under the 1935 Constitution, must elect Philippine citizenship within a reasonable time after reaching the age of majority — construed as roughly three years — or the election is void; passing the Bar Examinations does not cure a defective or belated election.

## FACTS

Vicente D. Ching was born in 1964 to a Filipino mother and a Chinese father, while the 1935 Constitution was in force. Under that Constitution, a child born to a Filipino mother and an alien father did not acquire Filipino citizenship at birth but only an inchoate right to elect it upon reaching the age of majority. Ching lived his entire life in the Philippines, voted, held elective office, and worked as a certified public accountant before studying law, without ever formally electing Philippine citizenship. After passing the 1998 Bar Examinations, he was allowed to take the exams on the condition that he later submit proof of citizenship. He then formally elected Philippine citizenship — fourteen years after reaching the age of majority.

## ISSUES

Whether Ching's election of Philippine citizenship, made fourteen years after reaching the age of majority, was made within a "reasonable time" as required for a valid election under the 1935 Constitution, and whether his years of de facto life as a Filipino could substitute for a timely formal election.

## RULING

The SC denied Ching's application for admission to the Bar. The Court held that "reasonable time" for electing Philippine citizenship means a period not extending beyond three years from reaching the age of majority, allowing only for the most excusable delay. Fourteen years was well beyond any reasonable reading of that standard. The Court rejected the argument that Ching's continuous residence, civic participation, and professional life in the Philippines could substitute for the formal election the law requires — citizenship being a legal status perfected only through the specific procedure the law prescribes (a sworn statement of election filed with the nearest civil registry, accompanied by an oath of allegiance), not inferred from conduct alone. Because Ching was not, at the time of his application, a Filipino citizen, he did not meet Rule 138's threshold citizenship requirement and could not be admitted to the Bar regardless of having passed the Examinations.

## DOCTRINE

**A legitimate child born under the 1935 Constitution to a Filipino mother and an alien father must elect Philippine citizenship within a reasonable time — construed as approximately three years — after reaching the age of majority.** Passing the Bar Examinations does not waive or cure a defective or untimely election; citizenship must be established as a fact before a Bar Examination result can translate into admission.

## NOTES

A clean illustration that admission to the Bar is a strict, cumulative checklist: excelling at one requirement (passing the Examinations) does not offset failing another (citizenship). Contrast with [[In Re Petition to Re-acquire the Privilege to Practice Law, B.M. No. 2112|In Re: Muneses]], where citizenship was validly held, lost, and later properly reacquired through the correct legal procedure (RA 9225) rather than inferred from years of conduct.
