
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 12661, February 19, 2020

**Ponente:** Lazaro-Javier, J. (First Division)

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Lawyer's Oath; Diligence and Client Communication; Canon 18, Code of Professional Responsibility

> **Gist:** A lawyer who conceals an adverse Supreme Court resolution from his client, and unilaterally decides not to move for reconsideration without telling the client, violates the Lawyer's Oath and the duty to keep a client informed — proof that the oath is invoked even in ordinary neglect cases having nothing to do with admission or dishonesty toward the court.

## FACTS

Benjamin M. Katipunan, Jr., a seafarer, engaged Atty. Rebene C. Carrera to represent him in a disability-benefits claim, from the NLRC all the way to the Supreme Court. The Court denied Katipunan's petition for review by Resolution dated January 27, 2010. Atty. Carrera received a copy on February 25, 2010 but did not tell his client. When Katipunan visited the office in March 2010 to ask about the case, Atty. Carrera told him it was still pending. Katipunan visited again in May 2010, asked the same question, received the same false answer, and then borrowed the case folder himself — only then discovering the resolution denying his petition, already final because no motion for reconsideration had been filed within the reglementary period.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer's failure to inform his client of an adverse court resolution, and his unilateral decision not to file a motion for reconsideration without consulting the client, constitute a violation of the Lawyer's Oath and the duty of diligence under Canon 18 of the Code of Professional Responsibility.

## RULING

The SC found Atty. Carrera guilty and suspended him from the practice of law for one month. The Court held that the Lawyer's Oath "is not a mere formality recited for a few minutes in the glare of flashing cameras" — a lawyer must conduct himself according to the oath at all times, and can be disciplined for any conduct, professional or private, that renders him unfit to remain an officer of the court. Atty. Carrera's silence about the resolution, and his repeated false assurance that the case was still pending, violated his oath-bound duty of candor and diligence to his client. Separately, the Court held that a lawyer is never in a position to unilaterally decide, on the client's behalf, whether to forgo the client's last available remedy; Atty. Carrera should have told Katipunan that he had decided against filing a motion for reconsideration and let Katipunan choose whether to engage another lawyer to pursue it. This combination — concealment plus unilateral decision-making — violated Canon 18, Rules 18.03 (neglect of a legal matter) and 18.04 (duty to keep the client informed), and Canon 15 of the Canons of Professional Ethics (undivided devotion to the client's cause).

## DOCTRINE

**The Lawyer's Oath is a continuing standard of conduct, not a ceremonial recitation confined to the admission ceremony.** A lawyer who conceals an adverse ruling from a client, or who unilaterally forecloses a client's remaining legal remedy without consultation, violates both the Lawyer's Oath and Canon 18's duty of diligence and communication — regardless of whether the lawyer's underlying handling of the case was otherwise competent.

## NOTES

This case is the cleanest illustration that oath violations are not reserved for dramatic misconduct like falsification or theft of client funds — an ordinary, almost mundane failure to update a client can be enough. See [[Revised Lawyer's Oath]] for why this matters for exam purposes: the oath functions as an umbrella ground cited alongside whatever specific CPR/CPRA rule is also violated, across nearly every kind of disciplinary case.
