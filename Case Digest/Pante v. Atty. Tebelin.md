
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 13630 (Formerly CBD Case No. 17-5285), June 27, 2023 (En Banc)

**Ponente:** Per Curiam

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Revised Lawyer's Oath; Fiduciary Duty; Prohibition on Borrowing from a Client; CPRA Canons II-IV

> **Gist:** A lawyer who fabricates a fake petition to deceive a client into believing a case was filed, fails to render the paid-for service, and then borrows money from the same client while the client is hospitalized, violates the Revised Lawyer's Oath and the CPRA's Propriety, Fidelity, and Competence-and-Diligence canons — and a prior, ignored disciplinary sanction for similar conduct turns the case into disbarment.

## FACTS

Alifer C. Pante engaged Atty. Jose Allan M. Tebelin in 2012 to file a petition for declaration of nullity of his marriage, for a package fee of PHP 200,000.00, and made a series of payments toward it. Atty. Tebelin eventually handed Pante a document purporting to be a filed Petition, complete with a case number — which the Regional Trial Court later certified did not exist; the case number belonged to an unrelated case. When confronted, Atty. Tebelin begged Pante not to file a complaint, executed promissory notes, and referred Pante to another lawyer whom Pante never actually met or heard from. While Pante was confined at a hospital, Atty. Tebelin borrowed PHP 50,000.00 from him for his own children's tuition, later asking for further sums supposedly for a psychologist's fee and a publication fee that were never fully paid to the actual service providers. Atty. Tebelin eventually stopped responding altogether, and Pante had to engage another lawyer. This was not Atty. Tebelin's first offense: he had previously been suspended for two months and ordered to return money to a different client in an earlier case, a sanction he had also ignored.

## ISSUES

Whether Atty. Tebelin's fabrication of a fake petition, failure to render the paid-for legal service, and borrowing of money from his hospitalized client violated the Revised Lawyer's Oath and the CPRA, and what penalty is warranted given his prior disciplinary record.

## RULING

The SC disbarred Atty. Tebelin and ordered him to return all sums received, with 6% legal interest. The Court held that giving Pante a fabricated petition with a non-existent case number was dishonest conduct violating CPRA Canon II, Sec. 1 (propriety); failing to file the petition despite receiving full payment for it, and failing to update Pante on the case's status, violated Canon III (fidelity) and Canon IV (competence and diligence); and borrowing money from Pante while the latter was hospitalized — without the case-specific protections the CPRA requires for lawyer-client loans — violated Canon III, Sec. 52's prohibition on a lawyer borrowing from a client during the engagement. The Court found this conduct also a direct breach of the Revised Lawyer's Oath, quoting the oath's text in full in its decision. Because Atty. Tebelin had already been sanctioned once before for materially similar conduct — neglect, and failure to return client funds — and had ignored that sanction too, the Court treated the prior offense as an aggravating circumstance under CPRA Canon VI, Sec. 38(b), pushing the penalty from the IBP's originally recommended one-year suspension up to disbarment.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer's fabrication of case documents to deceive a client, failure to render paid legal services, and borrowing money from a client outside the CPRA's narrow exceptions, together constitute serious offenses under CPRA Canon VI — dishonesty/fraud/deceit and gross negligence — and violate the Revised Lawyer's Oath.** A prior disciplinary sanction for similar misconduct, especially one the lawyer ignored, is an aggravating circumstance that can elevate an otherwise suspension-level case to disbarment.

## NOTES

This case supplies the CPRA's own verbatim text of the Revised Lawyer's Oath — see [[Revised Lawyer's Oath]] for the full quotation and its significance. It is also a clean illustration of CPRA, Canon III, Sec. 52 (the lending/borrowing prohibition), a genuinely new-textured rule with limited prior case law: exploiting a client's trust to secure a personal loan is treated as an abuse of the fiduciary relationship distinct from, and additional to, the underlying neglect of the client's case.
