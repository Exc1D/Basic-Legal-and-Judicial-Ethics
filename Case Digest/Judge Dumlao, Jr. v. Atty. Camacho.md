
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 10498, September 4, 2018

**Ponente:** Gesmundo, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Influence Peddling; Attempted Bribery; Disrespect to Court Officers; CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2 (Duty to Lead a Merit-Based Legal Practice)

> **Gist:** Counsel for a litigant repeatedly namedropped sitting Supreme Court Justices, offered to share his attorney's fees with the trial judge in exchange for a favorable ruling, and barged into the judge's chambers to bully a sheriff into signing a self-prepared, wildly inflated garnishment order — conduct the Court branded influence peddling and attempted bribery, suspending him two years even though he had already been disbarred in a separate, earlier case.

## FACTS

Judge Ariel Florentino R. Dumlao, Jr. was the Presiding Judge of RTC Dagupan City, Branch 42, before whom *Pathways Trading International, Inc. v. Univet Agricultural Products, Inc., et al.* (CV Case No. 2004-0181-D) was pending. Atty. Manuel N. Camacho was counsel for Pathways.

While the case was pending, respondent fraternized with complainant, dropping the names of then-Chief Justice Maria Lourdes Sereno and Associate Justice Marvic Leonen as his supposed close friends and colleagues, and touting his connections through the U.P. College of Law. After the RTC granted Pathways' motion for summary judgment (₱16,000,000.00 plus 10% attorney's fees) and defendants filed a notice of appeal, respondent began calling complainant, asking him to deny the appeal and issue a writ of execution — and **offered to share a portion of his attorney's fees with complainant** in exchange. He claimed the case was being "closely monitored" by the named Justices and that then-President Aquino III would appoint him Presidential Legal Consultant. When complainant did not comply, respondent threatened that his "connections" would ensure complainant's disbarment.

The RTC denied the appeal on a procedural ground and issued a writ of execution. On May 22, 2014, respondent barged into complainant's chambers and demanded that the court sheriff, Russel Blair Nabua, sign a garnishment order respondent himself had prepared — seeking release of ₱18,690,000,643.00 (against an actual judgment of only ₱16,000,000.00) directly to respondent as counsel. When Sheriff Nabua properly refused (defendants had offered personal property first, per the Rules of Court's three-tiered execution process), respondent threatened him: *"Kapag hindi mo pipirmahan ito, papatanggal kita," "Alam ng nasa itaas ito," "Alam ng dalawang Justices ito."* He also sent complainant text messages accusing him and the sheriff of graft and promising Supreme Court and Ombudsman pleadings.

Respondent never filed a comment despite the Court's order, and did not participate in the IBP proceedings.

## ISSUES

Whether respondent's acts of cultivating familiarity with a judge, offering to share attorney's fees for a favorable ruling, claiming influence over named Justices, and threatening court officers constitute influence peddling, attempted bribery, and disrespect to the court warranting discipline — and what penalty is appropriate given respondent's prior disbarment in an unrelated case.

## RULING

Yes on liability. The IBP Investigating Commissioner recommended disbarment (not respondent's first infraction); the IBP Board of Governors reduced this to a six-month suspension. The Supreme Court modified the penalty upward to a **two-year suspension**, finding respondent guilty of violating Canons 10, 11, 13, and 19 (Rules 10.01, 11.03, 13.01, and 19.01) of the CPR and the Lawyer's Oath — for influence peddling and attempted bribery (Canon 13, Rule 13.01: reliance on the merits, no cultivating familiarity with judges; Canon 10, Rule 10.01: candor and no artifice toward the court), and for threatening court officers and disrespecting court processes (Canon 11, Rule 11.03: no scandalous or menacing language before the courts; Canon 19, Rule 19.01: fair and honest means only, no threats of unfounded charges for improper advantage).

Critically, the Court noted that respondent had **already been disbarred** in a separate, earlier case — *Sison, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho*, 777 Phil. 1 (2016) — for entering into a compromise agreement without his client's conformity and failing to account for ₱1,288,260.00 received from that client. Because Philippine jurisdiction recognizes **no double or multiple disbarment**, the Court could not disbar him again; instead, it imposed the two-year suspension **solely for the purpose of recording the infraction in his personal file with the Office of the Bar Confidant**, to be considered if he ever petitions to lift the earlier disbarment.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who cultivates familiarity with a judge, claims influence over the courts to obtain a favorable ruling, offers pecuniary inducements, or threatens court personnel into compliance commits influence peddling and attempted bribery — a violation independent of, and stacked on top of, any prior disciplinary record, even where disbarment can no longer be separately imposed.**

## NOTES

**Naming note:** the complainant's full name is Judge **Ariel Florentino R. Dumlao, Jr.** — "Florentino" is his middle name, not his surname. This vault titles the digest by the case's official caption, *Judge Dumlao, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho*, and carries the syllabus's own shorthand, "Judge Florentino v. Atty. Camacho," as an alias so links written either way resolve to this file.

**Do not confuse this case with *Sison, Jr. v. Atty. Camacho* (A.C. No. 10910)** — a separate, earlier disciplinary case against the same Atty. Manuel N. Camacho, arising from an unrelated matter (an unauthorized compromise and failure to account for client funds), which is the case that actually disbarred him. This case (A.C. No. 10498) is the one on the syllabus for CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2; the Court's own decision here expressly cross-references *Sison* only to explain why it could not disbar him a second time.

This is the clearest illustration in the vault of the CPRA Canon I, Sec. 2 duty — "a lawyer shall rely solely on the merits of a cause and not exert, or give the appearance of, any influence on, nor undermine the authority of, the court, tribunal or other government agency, or its proceedings." Pair with [[Jimenez and Vizconde v. Atty. Verano, Jr.]] and [[Vantage Lighting Philippines v. Atty. Diño, Jr.]] for a spectrum of penalties (6 months → 2 years/effectively-disbarred → disbarment) turning on how far the lawyer went: mere agency-lobbying, versus direct bribery offers to a judge, versus claiming the judiciary itself is for sale.
