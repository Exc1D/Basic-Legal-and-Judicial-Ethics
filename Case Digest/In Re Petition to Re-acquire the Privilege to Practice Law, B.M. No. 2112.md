
**Full Case Citation:** B.M. No. 2112, July 24, 2012

**Ponente:** Reyes, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Citizenship as a Continuing Requirement; Practice of Law; RA 9225

> **Gist:** A Filipino lawyer who becomes a naturalized citizen of another country loses the privilege to practice law in the Philippines, and reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 does not automatically restore that privilege — the lawyer must still petition the Supreme Court directly and retake the Lawyer's Oath.

## FACTS

Epifanio B. Muneses was admitted to the Philippine Bar in 1966. In 1981, he became a naturalized citizen of the United States, which under Philippine law caused him to lose his Filipino citizenship and, with it, his privilege to practice law — citizenship being a continuing qualification for Bar membership, not merely a one-time admission requirement. In 2006, Muneses reacquired his Philippine citizenship by taking the Oath of Allegiance before the Philippine Consulate General in Washington, D.C., pursuant to RA 9225 (the Citizenship Retention and Re-Acquisition Act of 2003). In 2009, he petitioned the Office of the Bar Confidant to be allowed to resume the practice of law.

## ISSUES

Whether reacquiring Philippine citizenship under RA 9225 automatically restores a lawyer's privilege to practice law, and if not, what conditions must be met before the privilege may resume.

## RULING

The SC granted the petition, but only upon conditions. The Court explained that Filipino citizenship is not only a requirement for admission to the Bar but a continuing requirement for remaining a member of it; losing citizenship suspends the privilege to practice, and reacquiring citizenship under RA 9225 does not, by itself, revive the practice privilege. Because the practice of law is a privilege the Court alone grants and regulates, a lawyer in Muneses's position must (1) petition the Court directly, and (2) retake the Lawyer's Oath, signifying that the lawyer once again binds himself to its obligations as a matter of present commitment, not merely past qualification. Muneses's petition was granted subject to retaking the oath and paying the appropriate fees, and the Office of the Bar Confidant was directed to draft standing guidelines for future petitions of the same kind.

## DOCTRINE

**Philippine citizenship is a continuing requirement for the practice of law, not merely a condition precedent to admission.** A Filipino lawyer who loses citizenship through naturalization abroad loses the privilege to practice; reacquiring citizenship under RA 9225 restores only the citizenship, not the practice privilege automatically — a Supreme Court petition and a fresh Lawyer's Oath are still required.

## NOTES

This case is the standing authority behind the guidelines the Office of the Bar Confidant later formalized for lawyers resuming practice after RA 9225 reacquisition. It pairs well with [[In Re Application for Admission to the Philippine Bar of Vicente D. Ching|In Re: Vicente Ching]]: Ching concerns a natural-born citizenship claim that was never perfected in time, while Muneses concerns a citizenship that was validly held, lost, and later properly reacquired. Together they show how strictly citizenship is policed at every stage of a legal career, not only at admission.
