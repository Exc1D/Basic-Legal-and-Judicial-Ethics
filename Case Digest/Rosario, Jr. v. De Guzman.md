
**G.R. No. 191247, July 10, 2013**

**Ponente:** Justice Jose Catral Mendoza

**Subject:** Legal Ethics — Attorney's Fees and Quantum Meruit

> **Gist:** A lawyer who faithfully represented his clients for 17 years through all court levels is entitled to attorney's fees based on *quantum meruit* even after the main judgment has become final, with the SC fixing the amount at 15% of the property's market value when the lawyer failed to substantiate the 25% oral agreement he claimed.

## FACTS

In August 1990, Spouses Pedro and Rosita de Guzman engaged the legal services of Atty. Francisco L. Rosario, Jr. to defend them in a complaint for annulment of contract and recovery of possession with damages filed by one Loreta A. Chong involving a 266-square meter parcel of land in Parañaque City covered by TCT No. 1292. Atty. Rosario saw the case through every level — from the RTC, up to the CA, and ultimately before the SC — where his clients emerged victorious at all stages. While the case was pending before the SC, the Spouses de Guzman died in a vehicular accident, prompting their substitution by their four children: Rosella, Lellani, Arleen, and Philip Ryan, all surnamed De Guzman.

After the favorable SC decision became final and executory on October 31, 2007, Atty. Rosario filed a Motion to Determine Attorney's Fees on September 8, 2009, claiming an oral agreement with the deceased spouses for 25% of the market value of the property. When the children refused to honor this claim, he asserted entitlement to that amount based on *quantum meruit*. The RTC denied the motion, reasoning that it lost jurisdiction over the case once the judgment had become final and executory, and that granting the motion would create a variance in the already final judgment. The RTC also pointed out that it had already awarded ₱10,000.00 as attorney's fees in its decision — an award belonging to the Spouses de Guzman (and now their heirs) as indemnity for damages, not as compensation to counsel.

## ISSUES

The principal issue before the SC was whether Atty. Rosario's claim for attorney's fees, as compensation for professional services rendered to his deceased clients, may be entertained and granted after the judgment in the main case has already become final and executory. A related procedural question was whether Atty. Rosario pursued the correct remedy — he filed a Rule 45 petition for review on certiorari, when a Rule 65 petition for certiorari would have been more appropriate since the RTC's denial allegedly constituted grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack of jurisdiction. Equally important was whether the lawyer could recover on the basis of an alleged oral agreement where no written contract existed, and whether the amount claimed was reasonable under the circumstances.

## RULING

The SC granted the petition, treating it as having been filed under Rule 65 despite the procedural misstep, invoking the broader interest of justice and the principle that strict application of procedural rules should not obstruct the speedy disposition of cases on the merits. The SC noted that while the petitioner technically violated the **doctrine of hierarchy of courts** by going directly to it instead of first filing with the CA, this principle admits of exceptions, particularly when demanded by the broader interest of justice.

On the merits, the SC drew a careful distinction between the **two concepts of attorney's fees**: the *ordinary* concept, which is the reasonable compensation paid by a client to his lawyer for professional services rendered, and the *extraordinary* concept, which is an indemnity for damages awarded by the court to a winning litigant against the losing party. The ₱10,000.00 awarded by the RTC in the original case belonged to the heirs as an extraordinary item of damages and had nothing to do with the compensation owed to Atty. Rosario. Granting the lawyer's motion would therefore not constitute a double award or create a variance in the final judgment.

The SC affirmed that under the ruling in *Traders Royal Bank Employees Union-Independent v. NLRC*, a claim for attorney's fees for professional services may be filed as an incident of the main action, either before or after judgment is rendered, and the determination of the amount may await the finality of the judgment. The prescriptive period under **Article 1145 of the Civil Code** for oral contracts — six years — governs such claims, and the cause of action begins to run only from the moment the client refuses to pay. Since the children refused payment after 2007 and Atty. Rosario filed his motion in September 2009, his claim was well within the prescriptive period.

The SC also held that under **Rule 20.01 of the Code of Professional Responsibility**, the determination of attorney's fees must be guided by enumerated factors, including the time spent, novelty and difficulty of the questions, importance of the subject matter, skill demanded, customary charges, and the benefits accruing to the client. Applying these guidelines, the SC ruled that while Atty. Rosario's claim had merit, he failed to clearly substantiate the terms of his alleged oral agreement with the deceased spouses regarding the 25% figure. Therefore, instead of remanding the case for reception of evidence — which would only prolong an already 17-year legal saga — the SC opted to fix the fees directly based on *quantum meruit* at 15% of the market value of the property at the time of payment.

## DOCTRINE

**A lawyer who renders professional services to a client is entitled to reasonable compensation based on quantum meruit when there is no clear written agreement fixing the fees, even after the judgment in the main case has become final and executory.** The court may entertain a motion to determine attorney's fees for professional services as a mere incident of the main action, and such a claim is governed by the six-year prescriptive period under Article 1145 of the Civil Code for oral contracts, with the cause of action accruing only upon the client's refusal to pay. **The award of extraordinary attorney's fees as damages against the losing party is distinct from the compensation owed to counsel by the client**, and granting one does not constitute a double award of the other.

In determining reasonable attorney's fees on quantum meruit, courts must be guided by **Rule 20.01 of the Code of Professional Responsibility**, which enumerates the following factors: (a) the time spent and extent of services rendered; (b) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved; (c) the importance of the subject matter; (d) the skill demanded; (e) the probability of losing other employment due to acceptance of the case; (f) customary charges and IBP schedule of fees; (g) the amount involved and benefits resulting to the client; (h) the contingency or certainty of compensation; (i) the character of the employment; and (j) the professional standing of the lawyer.

## NOTES

This case is a useful reminder for bar review that when no written fee agreement exists, the lawyer's claim is treated as one based on an oral contract under Article 1145 (six-year prescriptive period), not under Article 1491 on the ten-year prescriptive period for written contracts. The 15% award illustrates the SC's readiness to step in and fix fees itself when remanding would only delay justice, but it also signals judicial skepticism toward blanket percentage claims tied to property values without clear substantiation. For practitioners, the case underscores the value of putting fee arrangements in writing — under Article 2208 of the Civil Code, a **written agreement** is the safest basis for claiming attorney's fees against a former client.
