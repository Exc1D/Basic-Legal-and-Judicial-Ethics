
**Full Case Citation:** A.C. No. 11641, March 12, 2019 (En Banc)

**Ponente:** Hernando, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Mandatory Continuing Legal Education (MCLE); Candor to the Court; Lawyer's Oath

> **Gist:** A lawyer who falsely claims MCLE exemption in pleadings — rather than truthfully disclosing non-compliance — commits dishonest conduct that violates the Lawyer's Oath and the duty of candor to the court, independent of whether the underlying MCLE non-compliance is eventually cured.

## FACTS

Atty. Jose M. Caringal failed to complete his Mandatory Continuing Legal Education units for two consecutive compliance periods spanning 2004 to 2010. Rather than disclosing this truthfully, he indicated in at least eleven pleadings and motions, filed across several cases, that he was "exempt" from MCLE II and III, citing an official receipt that was in fact only proof of payment of the non-compliance fee — not any exemption. Opposing counsel Marilu Turla obtained an MCLE Office certification confirming his non-compliance and confronted him with it, yet he continued filing pleadings with the same false MCLE information afterward. He eventually completed his MCLE requirements belatedly, in 2011.

## ISSUES

Whether a lawyer's false representation of MCLE exemption in pleadings — as opposed to simple non-compliance — constitutes an independent disciplinable offense, and whether belated compliance with MCLE cures the misrepresentation.

## RULING

The SC suspended Atty. Caringal from the practice of law for three years. The Court explained that non-compliance with MCLE, standing alone, is addressed through Bar Matter No. 850's own internal mechanism — a non-compliance fee and potential listing as a delinquent member — and Atty. Caringal had, in fact, already paid that fee. But falsely stating he was "exempt" when he was merely non-compliant was a separate, more serious wrong: a knowing, willful misrepresentation to the courts, repeated across eleven filings even after he was confronted with proof of his true status. This violated his sworn duty to "do no falsehood," Canon 1 and Rule 1.01 (unlawful, dishonest, or deceitful conduct), Canon 10 and Rule 10.01 (candor to the court), and Canons 17-18 (fidelity and diligence, since the false filings put his own clients' pleadings at risk of expunction — and one set of pleadings was, in fact, expunged under Bar Matter No. 1922). Belated MCLE compliance in 2011 did not erase the dishonesty already committed in the pleadings filed years earlier.

## DOCTRINE

**Failing to complete MCLE requirements and falsely claiming MCLE exemption are two distinct wrongs.** The first is addressed administratively under Bar Matter No. 850's own compliance mechanism (a non-compliance fee, then possible listing as a delinquent member); the second is an independent violation of the Lawyer's Oath and the duty of candor to the court, warranting suspension regardless of eventual, belated compliance with the underlying MCLE requirement.

## NOTES

A clean illustration that curing the underlying default (finishing MCLE late) does not erase the separate misconduct of having lied about it in sworn court filings in the meantime — structurally similar to how [[In Re Petition to Sign in the Roll of Attorneys, Michael Medado|Medado]]'s eventual disclosure did not erase the willfulness of his continued practice after discovering his own omission. Also worth remembering for its Remedial Law crossover: pleadings with false or missing MCLE information can be expunged from the record under Bar Matter No. 1922, at the client's expense.
