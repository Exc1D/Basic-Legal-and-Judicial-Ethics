
**Bar Matter No. 553, June 17, 1993**

**Ponente:** Regalado, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Unauthorized Practice of Law, Advertising by Lawyers

> **Gist:** The SC ruled that The Legal Clinic, Inc. is engaged in the unauthorized practice of law because its advertised services — covering foreign divorces, annulments, secret marriages, immigration matters, and the like — require the application of legal knowledge and therefore fall within the exclusive domain of lawyers; only natural persons duly admitted to the Philippine Bar may practice law, and the corporation's lawyer-incorporator, Atty. Rogelio P. Nogales, is hereby reprimanded for aiding unauthorized law practice and for unethical advertising.

## FACTS

Mauricio C. Ulep, a lawyer, filed this administrative bar matter against The Legal Clinic, Inc., a corporation with offices at the 7th floor of the Victoria Building along U.N. Avenue in Manila. The complaint arose from newspaper advertisements — published in two separate issues — that openly solicited clients for services such as "secret" marriages for ₱560.00, information and arrangements on **divorce**, **annulment of marriage**, **declaration of absence**, **remarriage to Filipina fiancées**, **adoption**, **immigration problems**, **visa extensions**, quota and non-quota immigrant visas, special retiree's visas, and even "US/Foreign Visa for Filipina Spouse/Children." The ads prominently featured the availability of free books on Guam divorce being distributed through a certain attorney in Guam. In effect, the corporation was holding itself out to the general public as a provider of legal solutions across a wide range of family, immigration, and investment law matters.

The Legal Clinic was incorporated in 1984 by **Atty. Rogelio P. Nogales**, who served as prime incorporator, majority stockholder, and proprietor. As later disclosed in a *Starweek* feature, Atty. Nogales patterned the enterprise after the medical model of specialization, staffing it with lawyers who are "specialists" in taxation, criminal law, medico-legal problems, labor, litigation, and family law, and backing them up with paralegals, counselors, and attorneys. The clinic served both regular and walk-in clients, beginning every engagement with an "analysis of the problem" before referral to a specialist. Simple documentation work — deeds of sale, affidavits of loss — was handled by staff on a "while-you-wait" basis, while complex matters like contested estates or litigation were elevated to senior lawyers.

Because of the serious implications on the legal profession, the SC required six bar organizations — the **Integrated Bar of the Philippines (IBP)**, **Philippine Bar Association (PBA)**, **Philippine Lawyers' Association (PLA)**, **U.P. Women's Lawyers' Circle (WILOCI)**, **Women's Lawyers' Association of the Philippines (WLAP)**, and **Federacion Internacional de Abogadas (FIDA)** — to submit position papers and memoranda on the controversy. All six bar associations uniformly opposed the Legal Clinic's operations.

## ISSUES

The principal question is whether the activities of The Legal Clinic, Inc. — as advertised and described — constitute the **practice of law**, thereby making them the exclusive prerogative of duly admitted members the Philippine Bar, and whether such activities, when performed through a corporate vehicle accompanied by newspaper solicitation, violate the canons on **unauthorized practice of law** and **unethical advertising**. A subsidiary question is whether the concept of paralegals, as recognized in the United States, can be unilaterally adopted in the Philippines to justify the clinic's corporate setup. The resolution of these issues also requires determining the proper disciplinary consequence for Atty. Nogales, the lawyer behind the corporation.

## RULING

The SC held that the activities of The Legal Clinic, Inc. constitute the **practice of law**. Citing a long line of authorities, the SC emphasized that *practice of law* means any activity, in or out of court, which requires the application of law, legal procedures, knowledge, training, and experience. It is not confined to courtroom litigation; it includes legal advice and counsel, the preparation of legal instruments and contracts by which legal rights are secured, and even appearances before quasi-judicial bodies. In *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]* (1991), the SC had already articulated a functional test that asks whether the rendition of services requires the knowledge and application of legal principles and technique to serve the interest of another with his consent — a definition that embraces the preparation of pleadings, conveyancing, drafting of legal instruments, and the giving of all legal advice to clients. The clinic's advertised offerings — advising clients on annulment of marriages, foreign divorces and their recognition under **Article 26 of the Family Code**, immigration and visa remedies, declarations of absence, adoption, and the tax consequences of inheritance — clearly call for legal training, knowledge, and experience, and squarely fall within that definition. The admission of Atty. Nogales himself in the *Starweek* article confirms that lawyers run the establishment and that the work performed ranges from routine documentation to complex litigation and estate matters.

The SC likewise rejected the corporation's claim that it merely offers "legal support services" or acts as a "paralegal" entity. **Section 1, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court** expressly limits the practice of law to natural persons who have been duly admitted as members of the Philippine Bar in good and regular standing. Corporations cannot practice law, and this limitation cannot be circumvented by employing competent lawyers to practice for them. Public policy requires that the practice of law remain restricted to qualified individuals because the privilege is personal and subject to withdrawal for failure to maintain proper standards, and the rule exists primarily to protect the public, the courts, the clients, and the bar from the incompetence or dishonesty of unlicensed persons who are beyond the disciplinary reach of the SC. As the PBA pointed out, a corporation functions as a convenient shield behind which the public cannot ventilate grievances for malpractice, precisely defeating the rationale of confining law practice to members of the bar.

The plea that Philippine practice adopt the American concept of paralegals likewise failed. While the SC recognized the potential merits of paralegalism, it held that such a development must first come through **judicial rules or legislative action**, not unilateral adoption by a private corporation. In the United States, paralegal education is offered by accredited schools and universities, regulated under guidelines like those issued by the American Bar Association, and further governed by professional associations with their own codes of ethics; in the Philippines, none of these structures exist, and allowing the clinic to label its personnel "paralegals" without any standard or regulatory framework would expose the public to exploitation by unqualified persons. Even the limited exceptions in Philippine law where non-lawyers may render specific legal services — law students in clinical programs under **Rule 138-A**, government prosecutors under **Section 33, Rule 138**, counsel *de oficio* under **Section 4, Rule 116**, agents in municipal or cadastral cases, and accredited representatives before the NLRC — are strictly confined and do not extend to the broad practice undertaken by the clinic.

On the matter of advertising, the SC applied **Canon 3** and its implementing rules of the **Code of Professional Responsibility**, reinforced by **Canon 27 of the Canons of Professional Ethics**. A lawyer may not advertise his services except in allowable instances: publication of brief biographical and informative data in reputable law lists, use of a simple professional card containing only name, firm, address, telephone number, and branch of law practiced, simple announcements of the opening of a firm or changes in partnership, and listing in telephone directories without designation of specialty. The newspaper ads in this case did not even fall under those exceptions; they solicited clients by promising services, often on matters — like **secret marriages** — that the Family Code itself considers impossible, since **Article 1** defines marriage as a special contract of permanent union, not a clandestine arrangement. Worse, by offering information on "divorce," the ads created the false impression that divorce exists in this jurisdiction, when in fact Philippine law does not recognize it except under the Code of Muslim Personal Laws. The SC echoed its earlier pronouncements that the best advertising for a lawyer is a well-merited reputation earned through competent and faithful service, never the artificial stimulus of propaganda.

In sum, Atty. Nogales committed misbehavior by establishing and operating a corporate vehicle that effectively practices law, by permitting the newspaper advertisement of legal services, and by aiding lay personnel in what amounts to the unauthorized practice of law. He was therefore **reprimanded, with warning** that any repetition of the same or similar acts shall be dealt with more severely. The SC explicitly refrained, however, from revoking or suspending the clinic's corporate franchise, holding that remedy to be the domain of the **Solicitor General**, to whom the matter was referred for the institution of the appropriate **quo warranto** proceedings under **Sections 2 and 3 of Rule 66** in relation to the Corporation Code.

## DOCTRINE

The practice of law is any activity, in or out of court, which requires the application of law, legal procedures, knowledge, training, and experience; it includes legal advice and counseling, the preparation of legal instruments, and the rendition of services for another that demand knowledge of legal principles and technique — and it embraces all advice to clients and all actions taken for them in matters connected with the law.

**The practice of law is reserved exclusively to natural persons who are duly admitted members of the Philippine Bar in good and regular standing (Section 1, Rule 138, Rules of Court).** A corporation cannot engage in the practice of law either directly or by employing lawyers to practice for it, because the privilege is personal and exists principally to protect the public, the courts, the clients, and the bar from the incompetence of unlicensed persons and to keep those practicing law subject to the disciplinary authority of the SC.

**A lawyer violates the Code of Professional Responsibility when he advertises his services beyond the permissible exceptions, or when he aids a layman or a non-lawyer entity in the unauthorized practice of law.** Allowable forms of advertising are confined to brief biographical data in reputable law lists, the use of a simple professional card, simple announcements of firm changes, and telephone directory listings — never newspaper solicitations promising legal services.

The recognition of paralegals as an occupation separate from the law profession requires judicial rules or legislative action establishing educational standards, regulatory frameworks, and ethical guidelines; in the absence of such infrastructure in the Philippines, no private entity may unilaterally adopt the American paralegal model and offer the corresponding services to the public for compensation.

## NOTES

A useful takeaway for recitations is that *Ulep* stands for the corporate non-practice rule — corporations, regardless of their lawyer employees, are not themselves members of the bar and therefore cannot "practice" law; remember that the proper remedy against a corporate entity engaged in unauthorized law practice is a **quo warranto** action, which the SC in this case referred to the Solicitor General rather than taking on directly. The decision also reinforces the continuing validity of the traditional ban on lawyer advertising under **Canon 3**, even after *Bates v. State Bar of Arizona* (1977) loosened the American rule, because Philippine courts have expressly chosen to retain the more restrictive ethical posture to preserve public trust in the bar. Finally, the case is frequently paired with *[[Cayetano v. Monsod]]* (1991) as the standard authorities for the modern, expansive definition of "practice of law" extending well beyond courtroom work.
