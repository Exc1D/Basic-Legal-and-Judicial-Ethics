## In the Matter of Proceedings for Disciplinary Action Against Atty. Vicente Raul Almacen

**G.R. No. L-27654, February 18, 1970**

**Ponente:** Castro, J.

**Subject:** Legal and Judicial Ethics — Discipline of Lawyers; Contempt by Publication; Limits of Criticism of Courts

> **Gist:** A lawyer's post-litigation tirade against the SC — accusing its members of being "calloused" to pleas for justice, ridiculing the administration of justice as "blind, deaf and dumb," and offering to surrender his lawyer's certificate in protest — constitutes **gross misconduct** warranting **indefinite suspension from the practice of law**, notwithstanding the constitutional right to criticize courts.

## FACTS

This case arose from a civil action, *Virginia Y. Yaptinchay vs. Antonio H. Calero*, where Atty. Vicente Raul Almacen represented the defendant. The trial court rendered judgment adverse to his client, and Atty. Almacen moved for reconsideration on July 5, 1966 — within the reglementary period — but served on the adverse counsel only a copy of the motion, **without notifying the latter of the time and place of hearing**. The trial court denied the motion for lack of proof of service. A second motion for reconsideration, with proof of service attached, was later withdrawn by Atty. Almacen himself upon verbal motion, and he instead perfected an appeal. The Court of Appeals, applying the SC's ruling in *Manila Surety & Fidelity Co., Inc. vs. Batu Construction & Co.*, dismissed the appeal on the ground that the first motion for reconsideration was "a useless piece of paper" — because it lacked a notice of hearing — and therefore did not interrupt the running of the period to appeal. Atty. Almacen's motion for reconsideration was denied, as was his attempt to distinguish *Manila Surety* by citing *Republic vs. Venturanza*, which the CA held was not authoritative on the matter.

Aggrieved, Atty. Almacen elevated the matter to the SC via a petition for certiorari. The SC denied the petition by minute resolution, denied his motion for reconsideration, denied his petition for leave to file a second motion for reconsideration and for extension of time, and entry of judgment was made on September 8, 1967. A second motion for reconsideration filed after that date was ordered expunged.

At this juncture, Atty. Almacen filed a "Petition to Surrender Lawyer's Certificate of Title" — a pleading saturated with scathing accusations against the SC and its members, branding them as "calloused to pleas for justice," alleging that they "ignore without reasons their own applicable decisions and commit culpable violations of the Constitution with impunity," and ridiculing the administration of justice as not only "blind, but also deaf and dumb." He vowed to argue his client's cause in "the people's forum" so that "the people may know of the silent injustices committed by this Court." He likewise gave statements to the press repeating the same charges. When the SC required him to show cause why no disciplinary action should be taken against him, Atty. Almacen stood his ground, refused to apologize, and went on to reiterate and embellish his attacks — quoting the Scriptures, accusing the Court of "hypocrisy," and insisting that his statements were "borne by TRUTH" and made with "NO MALICE."

## ISSUES

The principal issues are whether Atty. Almacen's post-litigation utterances and actuations — made after his client's case had already become final and executory — may be the subject of **disciplinary action** by the SC; whether the language used constitutes legitimate criticism of the courts or, conversely, gross misconduct that violates the lawyer's oath and the Canons of Legal Ethics; whether the SC is disqualified from acting on the matter because its members are allegedly the "complainants, prosecutors and judges" rolled into one; and whether the SC's practice of denying petitions for review by minute resolution renders the underlying dismissal constitutionally infirm.

## RULING

The SC ruled that Atty. Almacen's conduct constitutes **gross misconduct** warranting **indefinite suspension from the practice of law**. While the SC acknowledged — even emphasized — that lawyers, both as citizens and as officers of the court, enjoy a **wide latitude to criticize** the actuations of courts and judges, such right is not unbounded. The SC anchored this ruling on several interlocking propositions.

First, the SC recognized the constitutional dimension of the right to criticize. Under Section 12, Article VIII of the Constitution, every citizen has the right to comment upon and criticize the actuations of public officers, and this right is not diminished merely because the criticism is directed at a judicial authority or is articulated by a lawyer. Critiques of the judiciary — like those of the executive and legislative branches — are essential to democratic life; judges, as Chief Justice Sharswood of Pennsylvania once observed, occupy positions whose merits and demerits members of the bar, by virtue of their constant attendance on the courts, are in the best position to assess. The SC reaffirmed this principle, citing the Philippine rulings in *U.S. vs. Bustos*, *In re Gomez*, *Salcedo vs. Hernandez*, *Austria vs. Masaquel*, and *Cabansag vs. Fernandez*, as well as the U.S. Supreme Court decisions in *Bridges v. California* and *Pennekamp v. Florida*, and Justice Frankfurter's statement that judges "must be kept mindful of their limitations and of their ultimate public responsibility by a vigorous stream of criticism expressed with candor however blunt."

Second, the SC clarified that the **limits** of this right are well-settled. The **cardinal condition** of all such criticism is that it must be **bona fide** and must not "spill over the walls of decency and propriety." Between **fair criticism** and **abuse or slander of courts**, a wide chasm exists. The lawyer's oath (Section 3, Rule 138) and the Canons of Legal Ethics require a member of the bar to conduct himself "with all good fidelity ... to the courts" and "to observe and maintain the respect due to courts of justice and judicial officers." The first canon of legal ethics commands him "to maintain towards the courts a respectful attitude, not for the sake of the temporary incumbent of the judicial office, but for the maintenance of its supreme importance." The duty, as Justice Field stated in *Bradley v. Fisher*, is "not discharged by merely observing the rules of courteous demeanor in open court, but includes abstaining out of court from all insulting language and offensive conduct toward judges personally for their judicial acts." Applying this framework, the SC held that Atty. Almacen's petition, answer, and oral arguments before the Court "bristl[e] with vile insults" — picturing his client as "a sacrificial victim at the altar of hypocrisy," describing SC justice as "deaf and dumb," branding the members as "calloused" and impugning their integrity — language that "far transcend[s] the permissible bounds of legitimate criticism" and "could never serve any purpose but to gratify the spite of an irate attorney" and to bring the Court and its members into disrepute.

Third, the SC brushed aside Atty. Almacen's attempt to escape liability by pointing out that his statements were made **after** his client's case had become final. The once-settled rule that the protective mantle of contempt may be invoked only against scurrilous remarks made **while** a case is pending has lost much of its vitality. Citing its own ruling in *In re Brillantes* — and the dissent of Chief Justice Moran in *People vs. Alarcon* — the SC adopted the view that publications or utterances which tend to degrade the courts and destroy public confidence in them constitute **criminal contempt** even after litigation has terminated, because what is sought to be protected in such cases "is the court itself and its dignity." More fundamentally, however, the SC held that the distinction is immaterial here: the November 17, 1967 resolution concerned not contempt, but the **disciplinary power** of the Court over its officers, where the sole objective is to preserve the purity of the legal profession by removing or suspending a member whose misconduct has proved him unfit. The pendency or non-pendency of a case is altogether of no consequence in such an inquiry.

Fourth, the SC addressed — and rejected — Atty. Almacen's contention that the members of the Court are disqualified to act because they are simultaneously the "complainants, prosecutor and judge." The Court held that **disciplinary proceedings are sui generis**: they are neither purely civil nor purely criminal, do not constitute a trial of an action or suit, and may be initiated by the Court *motu proprio*. There is neither a plaintiff nor a prosecutor. Public interest is the primary objective, and the real question is whether the attorney remains a fit person to enjoy the privileges of a member of the Bar. In exercising its disciplinary powers, the Court acts as an **entity separate and distinct from the individual personalities of its members** — as a collegiate body whose distinct individualities are "lost in the majesty" of the office. The power to admit to the practice of law necessarily carries with it the **inherent and incidental power to discipline and exclude**, an authority expressly recognized by Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court and rooted in the constitutional mandate vesting in the SC the power to promulgate rules for the admission to the practice of law under Article VIII, Section 12 of the Constitution. The Court cannot abdicate this duty.

Fifth, the SC addressed the related complaint about its **minute resolution** practice. Acknowledging the "abject frustration" of a lawyer whose carefully prepared petition is rebuffed with a terse denial, the SC nevertheless defended the practice as constitutional and necessary. It cited *Novino vs. Court of Appeals*, where, through Chief Justice Bengzon, the Court had held that such "resolutions" are not "decisions" within the meaning of Section 12, Article VIII of the Constitution but merely refusals to entertain the petition under Rule 46, and that review of CA decisions is "not a matter of right, but of sound judicial discretion." The practice, patterned after that of the U.S. Supreme Court, is essential to enable the Court to discharge its indispensable duties given its heavy docket. The Court also noted that in Atty. Almacen's particular case, the CA had fully and correctly considered the dismissal of the appeal under settled doctrine, leaving no occasion for the exercise of the SC's supervisory power. The lawyer's predicament, the SC pointedly observed, was attributable to his own admitted failure to notify the adverse party of the time and place of hearing of his motion for reconsideration — a requirement then mandated by Rule 15, Sections 4 to 6 (formerly Rule 26) and unequivocally articulated in *Manila Surety & Fidelity vs. Batu Construction & Co.* His loss of the appeal "has no justification" beyond his own negligence.

## DOCTRINE

The SC laid down the following doctrinal principles:

**First, a lawyer enjoys the right — and indeed the duty — as both a citizen and an officer of the court to criticize the actuations of courts and judges, provided that the criticism is bona fide, intelligent, discriminating, and does not exceed the bounds of decency and propriety.** The **cardinal condition** is that the critique must not "spill over the walls of decency and propriety"; intemperate and unfair criticism that tends to bring the courts into disrepute or subvert public confidence in the integrity of the judiciary constitutes **gross misconduct** for which the lawyer may be suspended or disbarred.

**Second, post-litigation utterances of a lawyer attacking the courts may be the proper subject of disciplinary action** — the rule that bars contempt after a judicial proceeding has terminated has lost much of its vitality, but in any case, what controls in a disciplinary proceeding is the fitness of the lawyer to remain in the Bar, not the pendency of a case.

**Third, disciplinary proceedings against lawyers are sui generis** — they are not actions or suits, are not criminal prosecutions, may be initiated *motu proprio* by the SC, and have as their primary objective the preservation of the purity of the legal profession and the protection of public interest. The SC acts as an institutional entity in such proceedings, and the personal interest of its members in the derogatory remarks does not disqualify it from exercising its constitutionally-mandated power to discipline members of the Bar.

**Fourth, the SC's practice of denying petitions for review by minute resolution is constitutional** and does not violate the constitutional requirement that the Court must state the facts and law on which its decisions are based, because such resolutions are not "decisions" but mere refusals to entertain the petition in the exercise of sound judicial discretion. Review of CA decisions is not a matter of right.

**Fifth, the power to discipline and exclude members of the Bar is an inherent and incidental power of courts of record**, expressly recognized under Section 27, Rule 138 of the Rules of Court, and rooted in Article VIII, Section 12 of the Constitution. This power is co-extensive with the power to admit to the practice of law and cannot be abdicated.

## NOTES

This case is foundational in **Philippine Legal Ethics** and is frequently cited for the proposition that a lawyer's freedom of expression, while broad, is bounded by the duty of **respectful subordination to the courts** — a duty rooted in the lawyer's oath and the Canons of Legal Ethics. It is best remembered for the principle that **fair criticism is not the same as scurrilous attack**; the line is crossed when the language employed is calculated to degrade the courts and destroy public confidence in the administration of justice.

It is also a leading authority on the **sui generis** character of disciplinary proceedings, settling once and for all the recurrent objection that members of the SC cannot sit in judgment over lawyers who have insulted them. The case clarifies that the Court acts in such matters as an institutional body, with public interest — not personal vindication — as the controlling consideration.

Finally, the case is a useful counterpoint to the U.S. line of cases (*Bridges v. California*, *Pennekamp v. Florida*) cited approvingly by the Court: while the right to criticize is robustly protected, Philippine doctrine treats attacks that transcend the bounds of decency — particularly those that impute dishonesty or lack of integrity — as **professional misconduct** independent of any question of contempt or pending litigation.
